# Golden Reference Test Passages

Standardized text passages for persona regression testing. Each passage exercises specific vocal qualities and should be used consistently across all personas to enable meaningful comparison.

---

## Core Passages

These four passages form the primary test suite. Every persona should have golden references for at least the Standard Narrative passage.

---

### 1. Standard Narrative

**Purpose**: Tests pacing, tone, literary quality, and baseline voice consistency.

**Tests**: Cadence, breath management, emotional subtlety, descriptive prose handling.

**Word count**: ~250 words

```text
The house had been empty for three years when Sarah finally returned. She stood at the gate, keys cold in her palm, watching the October wind push dead leaves across the front porch. The paint was peeling now. The garden had gone wild.

Inside, dust covered everything in a fine gray film. She ran her finger along the banister, remembering how her mother used to polish it every Sunday morning, the smell of lemon oil filling the hallway. The grandfather clock had stopped at 4:47—she didn't know which day, which month. It didn't matter now.

The kitchen still held the faint ghost of coffee and cinnamon. Sarah opened the cabinet above the stove and found her mother's recipe box exactly where it had always been, the index cards yellowed but legible. Gingerbread. Apple pie. The Christmas cookies they'd made together every December until they didn't anymore.

She sat down at the table, the same table where she'd done her homework, eaten ten thousand dinners, learned that her mother was sick. The chair creaked beneath her weight. Outside, a car passed slowly, its headlights sweeping across the wall.

There would be so much to sort through. Papers and photographs and clothes and all the accumulated evidence of a life. But not tonight. Tonight she would simply sit here in the growing dark, letting the house remember her, letting herself remember the house. Tomorrow would be for decisions. Tonight was for grief.
```

### 2. Dialogue-Heavy

**Purpose**: Tests character voice switching, emotional shifts, natural conversation flow.

**Tests**: Voice differentiation, pacing in exchanges, emotional authenticity, handling of dialogue tags.

**Word count**: ~280 words

```text
"You're late," Marcus said, not looking up from his newspaper.

Elena dropped her bag on the counter. "Traffic was hell. The bridge was backed up for miles."

"The bridge is always backed up. That's why you leave early."

"I did leave early. There was an accident." She opened the refrigerator, stared at its contents, closed it again. "Are we really doing this right now?"

Marcus finally looked at her. His expression was carefully neutral, the way it always was when he was angry. "Doing what?"

"This. The passive-aggressive thing. The 'you're late' thing."

"I simply observed that you were late. You're the one making it a thing."

Elena laughed, but there was no humor in it. "God, Marcus. Listen to yourself."

Silence stretched between them. The kitchen clock ticked. Somewhere in the apartment above, a child was practicing piano, the same phrase over and over, never quite getting it right.

"I'm sorry," Marcus said finally. His voice had changed—softer now, tired. "I had a rough day. I shouldn't take it out on you."

Elena felt the tension in her shoulders ease slightly. "What happened?"

"They're closing the Henderson project. Six months of work, just—gone. Budget cuts."

"Oh, Marcus." She crossed the room and put her hand on his shoulder. "Why didn't you say something?"

"Because I wanted to be angry for a while first." He managed a small smile. "Is that allowed?"

"It's allowed." She sat down beside him. "Tell me about it. All of it. We have time."

And for once, it felt like they did.
```

### 3. Emotional Peak

**Purpose**: Tests dynamic range, intensity, building tension and release.

**Tests**: Emotional modulation, dramatic emphasis, controlled intensity, recovery to baseline.

**Word count**: ~270 words

```text
The doctor was still talking, but James had stopped hearing the words. Something about cells and stages and treatment options. The fluorescent lights buzzed overhead. The chair beneath him felt insubstantial, as if he might fall through it at any moment.

"Mr. Chen? James?"

He looked up. Dr. Ramirez was watching him with professional concern, her pen hovering over a clipboard. Behind her, a poster showed a cartoon diagram of a human body, all the organs labeled in cheerful colors.

"I'm sorry," he said. "Could you repeat that?"

"I said we caught it early. The prognosis is good. Better than good—with treatment, the survival rate is over ninety percent."

Ninety percent. He turned the number over in his mind. Nine out of ten. One in ten. One out of ten did not survive. One out of ten.

"James." Dr. Ramirez leaned forward. "Are you with me?"

"Yes. Yes, I'm here." He forced himself to breathe, really breathe, filling his lungs and letting the air out slowly. "Ninety percent. That's—that's good. That's really good."

"It is. And we're going to fight this together, okay? You're not alone in this."

He nodded, not trusting his voice. Through the window, he could see the parking lot, cars glinting in the afternoon sun. People walking to their appointments, returning to their lives, having ordinary days. He remembered parking his own car this morning, thinking about dinner plans, about the grocery list, about nothing at all. That had been a different person.

"Okay," he heard himself say. "Okay. What do we do first?"
```

### 4. Technical/Expository

**Purpose**: Tests clarity, maintaining engagement with informational content, educational tone.

**Tests**: Articulation, pacing of complex ideas, emphasis on key terms, authoritative delivery.

**Word count**: ~260 words

```text
The process of fermentation has shaped human civilization for at least ten thousand years. Long before anyone understood the science behind it, our ancestors discovered that certain foods, left to their own devices under the right conditions, transformed into something entirely new—and often something better.

At its core, fermentation is remarkably simple. Microorganisms, primarily bacteria and yeasts, consume sugars and convert them into other compounds: alcohol, acids, and gases. This process not only preserves food by creating an environment hostile to harmful bacteria, but also develops complex flavors and increases nutritional availability.

Consider bread. When flour and water combine, wild yeasts present in the flour begin to feed on the available sugars. They produce carbon dioxide, which creates the airy structure we expect from a good loaf, and alcohol, which evaporates during baking. The longer this process continues, the more complex the flavor becomes—which is why traditional sourdoughs, fermented for days rather than hours, develop their characteristic tang and depth.

The same fundamental principle applies to wine, cheese, sauerkraut, miso, and hundreds of other fermented foods found across every culture. Each represents a partnership between human intention and microbial activity, a collaboration that predates written language.

Modern science has given us unprecedented control over these processes. We can select specific strains of bacteria, maintain precise temperatures, and monitor chemical changes in real time. Yet the essence remains unchanged: we provide the conditions, and the microorganisms do the work. It is, in many ways, one of humanity's oldest and most successful technologies.
```

## Genre-Specific Passages

These passages provide specialized testing for genre-focused personas. Use when a persona's primary use case demands genre-appropriate reference material.

---

### Thriller/Suspense

**For**: `narrator-thriller`, noir/crime personas

**Purpose**: Tests tension maintenance, pacing control, controlled intensity.

**Word count**: ~240 words

```text
The footsteps stopped.

Catherine pressed herself against the wall, willing her breathing to slow. The warehouse was vast and dark, broken only by thin shafts of streetlight filtering through the grimy windows high above. She counted to ten. Nothing.

Then she heard it—the soft scrape of shoe leather on concrete, closer than before.

He was hunting her. Patient, methodical, in no particular hurry. He knew she was here. He knew there was nowhere to go.

She edged along the wall, fingers trailing over rough brick, searching for a door, a window, anything. Her phone was dead. Her car was three blocks away. And the man with the knife was somewhere in the darkness between her and escape.

A stack of pallets loomed ahead. She slipped behind them, crouching low, and finally allowed herself to breathe. The wound on her arm throbbed with each heartbeat. Not deep, but bleeding steadily. She needed to move fast. She needed to think.

The footsteps began again, slow and deliberate. He was talking now, his voice echoing off the high ceiling.

"I really didn't want it to come to this, Catherine. You understand that, don't you? This is your doing. All you had to do was stay quiet."

She spotted it then—a service door, twenty feet away, a sliver of streetlight visible beneath it. Twenty feet of open floor. Twenty feet of exposure.

She ran.
```

### Children's/Picture Book

**For**: `narrator-childrens`, early reader personas

**Purpose**: Tests read-aloud quality, age-appropriate pacing, warmth, clear enunciation.

**Word count**: ~200 words

```text
Once upon a time, in a garden at the edge of the forest, there lived a very small hedgehog named Pip.

Pip was curious about everything. He wanted to know why the leaves changed colors in autumn. He wondered where the sun went at night. And most of all, he wanted to find out what was on the other side of the Old Stone Wall.

"Don't go past the wall," his mother told him every day. "The forest is for bigger animals."

But Pip couldn't stop wondering.

One morning, when the fog hung low over the garden like a soft gray blanket, Pip found a tiny hole at the bottom of the Old Stone Wall. It was just big enough for a very small hedgehog.

He looked back at his cozy home under the rose bush. He looked at the dark forest beyond the wall. His heart beat fast—thump, thump, thump—like a tiny drum.

"Just a peek," Pip whispered to himself. "Just one little peek."

And he squeezed through the hole.

What do you think Pip found on the other side?
```

### Literary/Philosophical

**For**: `narrator-literary`, `narrator-literary-female`, `narrator-global-literary`

**Purpose**: Tests handling of complex prose, philosophical nuance, measured pacing.

**Word count**: ~280 words

```text
Memory, we are told, is unreliable. It reconstructs rather than replays, filling gaps with plausible fictions, smoothing rough edges, assigning meaning in retrospect. The woman I remember my grandmother being may have little in common with the woman she actually was. My childhood, as I carry it, is a kind of novel—curated, compressed, significant in ways the original days were not.

And yet.

When I hold her fountain pen—the one she used to write letters in her careful slanted script, the one I found last month in a box of forgotten things—I am not remembering. I am there. The weight of it in my hand is true. The smell of ink and old paper is true. Something in me opens that no photograph could open, no story could unlock.

Perhaps this is what we mean when we speak of the soul: not some ethereal passenger, but this capacity to be reached, to be touched across time. The pen does not know it carries her presence. It is merely an object. But I am not merely an object, and when I hold it, I become a bridge between what was and what is.

She would have called this nonsense. She was practical, unsentimental, suspicious of abstraction. "Things are things," she would say. "Don't make them more than they are."

But she kept this pen for fifty years. She chose it, used it, wore it smooth with her grip. And now it has found its way to me, across all the distance and silence, and I understand something I could not have understood while she lived.

Things are things. But sometimes things are everything.
```

### Memoir/Personal Essay

**For**: `narrator-warm-female`, `narrator-nonbinary-contemporary`, first-person narrators

**Purpose**: Tests intimacy, conversational authenticity, emotional vulnerability.

**Word count**: ~250 words

```text
I was thirty-four when I learned to swim.

I know how that sounds. Everyone learns to swim as a kid, at summer camp or the YMCA or some neighbor's backyard pool. Everyone except me. My mother was afraid of water—genuinely afraid, the kind of fear that makes your hands shake—and she passed that fear down to me like a family heirloom. For decades I just avoided pools, lakes, beaches, anything deeper than a bathtub.

But then I met David, who grew up on a lake in Michigan and couldn't imagine summer without water. And I didn't want to be the person who stood on the shore, watching, forever on the edge of the experience.

So I signed up for lessons. Adult beginners, Tuesday evenings, a class full of immigrants and late bloomers and people with their own complicated reasons for starting over. Our instructor was a college student named Maya who treated us with the same matter-of-fact encouragement you'd give a nervous child.

The first time I put my face in the water, I panicked. My whole body locked up. I couldn't breathe, couldn't think, couldn't remember that I was standing in four feet of water and could simply stand up.

But I went back. And the next week was a little better. And the week after that.

I'm not a graceful swimmer. I probably never will be. But last summer, I swam out to the floating dock at David's family lake, and I stayed there for an hour, just floating, just being in the water.

It felt like freedom.
```

## Usage Guidelines

### Minimum Test Coverage

Every persona should have golden references for at least:
- **Tier 1 (Required)**: Standard Narrative
- **Tier 2 (Recommended)**: Standard Narrative + Dialogue-Heavy
- **Tier 3 (Complete)**: All four core passages + relevant genre passage

### Audio Specifications

All golden references must be:
- **Format**: WAV, 16-bit PCM
- **Sample rate**: 44.1 kHz
- **Channels**: Mono
- **Normalization**: -3 dB peak, -16 LUFS integrated loudness
- **Noise floor**: Below -60 dB

### File Naming Convention

```
{persona-id}_{passage-type}.wav
```

Examples:
- `narrator-literary_standard.wav`
- `narrator-thriller_thriller-genre.wav`
- `narrator-childrens_childrens-genre.wav`

### Passage Selection by Persona Type

| Persona Category | Recommended Passages |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| Literary narrators | Standard, Dialogue, Emotional, Literary-Genre |
| Thriller/Crime | Standard, Dialogue, Thriller-Genre |
| Children's | Standard, Childrens-Genre |
| Contemporary/Commercial | Standard, Dialogue, Memoir-Genre |
| Character voices | Dialogue, Emotional |
| Technical/Educational | Standard, Technical |
