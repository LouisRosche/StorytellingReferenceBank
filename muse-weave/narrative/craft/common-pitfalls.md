# Common Pitfalls

Diagnostic guide for recognizing what's going wrong. Organized by level: structural failures first, prose-level last.

**Counterpart**: `@references/master-techniques.md` (what to do) | `@templates/revision-workflow.md` (when to apply)

---

## How to Use This Guide

A draft that isn't working is usually failing at one identifiable level. Start with structure. If structure is sound, move to scene. If scenes earn their place, move to line. Fixing prose in a broken structure is wasted effort.

---

## I. Structural Failures

### 1. Passive Protagonist

**Symptom**: Things happen *to* the protagonist. They react, endure, witness. Others make the decisions that drive the plot.

**Why it fails**: Story is transformation through choice. A protagonist who doesn't choose has no arc. The reader can't invest in someone who doesn't act.

**Diagnostic**: In every act, ask: Did the protagonist cause this, or did it happen anyway? List the three most important plot turns — who initiated each?

**Fix**: Give the protagonist the wrong goal at first. Let them pursue it aggressively. The wrong choice, vigorously made, is more interesting than the right choice discovered by accident.

---

### 2. Stalled Tension

**Symptom**: Scenes don't escalate. The middle feels like a plateau. Each chapter is roughly as dangerous, emotionally charged, or high-stakes as the one before.

**Why it fails**: Narrative requires escalation. If stakes don't rise, readers have no reason to keep reading.

**Diagnostic**: Rank every major scene from 1 (low stakes) to 10 (maximum pressure). Plot the numbers chronologically. The line should trend upward, not flat or random.

**Fix**: Identify what would make each scene more costly. Add consequences that carry forward. Never let a setback be completely recovered by the next chapter.

---

### 3. Setup Without Payoff / Payoff Without Setup

**Symptom A**: Plot elements introduced and dropped. Characters appear, matter, and vanish. Promises made and forgotten.

**Symptom B**: Resolutions that feel arbitrary. The protagonist succeeds by coincidence. The villain is defeated by a tool never mentioned before.

**Why it fails**: Story is a contract. Every significant element is a promise. Violations of this contract feel like cheating.

**Diagnostic**: List every setup element (objects introduced, character abilities established, foreshadowed events). List every payoff. Map them. Do they match?

**Fix**: Every plant needs a harvest. Every harvest needs a plant. Work both directions: identify orphaned setups and un-planted payoffs, then either create the missing half or cut the orphan.

---

### 4. Theme by Announcement

**Symptom**: Characters state the theme directly. "I've learned that love requires sacrifice." "Family is the most important thing." The meaning of the story is explained rather than embodied.

**Why it fails**: Readers resist being told what to think. Stated theme closes the story down; enacted theme opens it up.

**Diagnostic**: Highlight any line where a character articulates the moral of the story, especially near the end.

**Fix**: Cut the line. Trust the story. If you've earned the thematic moment through action and character, the reader can supply the statement themselves. Your job is to give them the evidence, not the verdict.

---

### 5. False All-Is-Lost

**Symptom**: The low point doesn't actually feel lost. The protagonist always seems one good idea away from winning. No genuine despair, no real reckoning with failure.

**Why it fails**: Catharsis requires real loss. If the audience never believes the protagonist might actually lose, the climax has no emotional weight.

**Diagnostic**: The All-Is-Lost moment should be a moment you, the writer, genuinely dread. If you wrote it quickly and with relief, it's probably false.

**Fix**: Make something irreversible happen. Death, betrayal, destroyed relationships, burned bridges. The protagonist must genuinely lose something they cannot simply take back.

---

### 6. "And Then" Plotting

**Symptom**: Events follow each other without causal connection. Scene A ends, Scene B begins — they're adjacent, not linked.

**Why it fails**: Without causation, there's no momentum. The reader has no reason to expect the next scene to matter.

**Diagnostic**: Between each scene, ask: "therefore" or "but"? If the only link is "and then," the causal chain is broken.

**Fix**: The Trey Parker/Matt Stone method: every scene transition should be "therefore" (the previous caused this) or "but" (something opposes this). Never just "and then."

---

## II. Character Failures

### 7. Static Character

**Symptom**: The protagonist ends the story essentially the same as they began. Events have occurred; the character has not changed.

**Why it fails**: Story is transformation. Without change, there's no arc — just events.

**Diagnostic**: Write one sentence describing the protagonist at page one. Write one sentence describing them at the last page. Are they the same sentence?

**Fix**: Identify the protagonist's Lie — the false belief they carry into the story. The story should force them to confront it. If they don't confront it, the arc doesn't close.

---

### 8. Antagonist as Cardboard

**Symptom**: The antagonist has no comprehensible motivation. They're evil because the story needs evil. They exist to be defeated.

**Why it fails**: The antagonist defines the protagonist. A weak antagonist produces a weak climax. If the antagonist isn't right about something, the conflict isn't real.

**Diagnostic**: Can you articulate the antagonist's worldview with genuine sympathy? Could you write a version of the story from their point of view where they're the hero?

**Fix**: Give the antagonist a coherent reason to believe they're correct. They should want something understandable, pursue it through comprehensible means, and have a point of view the protagonist cannot simply dismiss.

---

### 9. Character Inconsistency

**Symptom**: Characters behave in ways that serve plot rather than psychology. They forget what they know. They're brave in one scene, inexplicably cowardly in the next.

**Why it fails**: Readers track characters against an internal model of who they are. Violations of that model break immersion.

**Diagnostic**: Read each character's scenes in sequence. Does each choice follow from previous choices and established psychology? Flag any action that requires the character to forget something they know.

**Fix**: Every character choice must be motivated by character, not plot need. If the plot requires a character to behave inconsistently, change the plot.

---

### 10. Unearned Transformation

**Symptom**: The protagonist changes — but the change isn't connected to what happened in the story. They're different because the story needs them to be different, not because their experience transformed them.

**Why it fails**: Arc is causation. The transformation must grow from the ordeal. If you can swap in a different transformation and the story still works, neither the story nor the transformation is earning its place.

**Diagnostic**: Could you replace the transformation with its opposite and have the story still function? If yes, the arc is decorative.

**Fix**: Trace the transformation backward to its cause. The cause must be specific events in this story, choices this character made, losses they experienced. If the cause is generic ("they went through a lot"), specificity is missing.

---

## III. Scene-Level Failures

### 11. Scenes Without Goals

**Symptom**: Characters talk or move without any of them wanting anything specific from the interaction.

**Why it fails**: Without competing wants, there's no tension. Without tension, there's no scene.

**Diagnostic**: For each scene, name what each character wants from this specific interaction. If you can't name it, neither can they.

**Fix**: Before writing the scene, define the goal for the POV character and the obstacle. See `@templates/scene-card.md` for the full diagnostic.

---

### 12. Late Entry, Late Exit

**Symptom**: Scenes begin with characters settling in, small talk, arriving at locations. Scenes end with characters departing, wrapping up, saying goodbye.

**Why it fails**: The interesting part of a scene is almost never the beginning or the end. The tension is in the middle.

**Diagnostic**: Find where the scene's central conflict actually begins. That's your new first line. Find where the central conflict resolves. That's your new last line.

**Fix**: Enter the scene in media res — after the tension has started. Exit the scene while the reverb is still happening — before the characters have fully processed what occurred.

---

### 13. Emotional Labeling

**Symptom**: Prose states emotions directly. "She was devastated." "He felt a surge of anger." "The grief was overwhelming."

**Why it fails**: Naming emotion forecloses the reader's experience of it. The reader should *feel* the devastation, not be told it exists.

**Diagnostic**: Search the manuscript for words like: felt, seemed, was + emotion words (devastated, angry, afraid, sad, happy). Each is a candidate for replacement.

**Fix**: Show the body, the behavior, the thought. The character's hand shakes. She answers too quickly. He can't look at the photograph. The reader supplies the emotion.

---

### 14. Sequel Without Scene

**Symptom**: Extended passages of characters processing, feeling, deciding — without anything externally happening.

**Why it fails**: Scene (action) and Sequel (reaction) must alternate. Continuous sequel produces reader fatigue; it's all feeling, no event. See `@templates/structures/scene-sequel.md` for the full Scene-Sequel framework.

**Diagnostic**: Mark every passage where no external conflict occurs. If you have more than a page of unbroken interior processing, check the ratio.

**Fix**: Compress the sequel. The essentials of reaction, dilemma, and decision can often be achieved in a paragraph. Give the character a new external challenge before they've finished processing the previous one.

---

## IV. Prose Failures

### 15. Filtering

**Symptom**: Prose creates distance between reader and character through filtering verbs: *she saw*, *he noticed*, *she realized*, *she felt*, *he thought*.

**Why it fails**: Filters interrupt immersion. They remind the reader they're getting information through a reporting layer, not experiencing it directly.

**Diagnostic**: Search for: saw, noticed, realized, thought, felt, wondered, seemed, appeared.

**Examples**:
- Filtered: *She saw the door was open.*
- Direct: *The door was open.*

- Filtered: *He noticed the room smelled of cigarettes.*
- Direct: *The room smelled of cigarettes.*

**Fix**: Remove the filter. Put the reader inside the perception, not outside it watching the character perceive.

---

### 16. Adverb Dependence

**Symptom**: Adverbs modify verbs to do work the verbs should do alone. *walked slowly*, *said angrily*, *smiled sadly*.

**Why it fails**: The adverb is often a sign that the verb isn't working. *Trudged* replaces *walked slowly*. *Snapped* replaces *said angrily*. The specific verb carries texture the adverb can only approximate.

**Diagnostic**: Search for `-ly` words modifying verbs. Flag each one.

**Fix**: Replace verb + adverb with a precise verb. If no precise verb exists, a behavioral detail often serves better: *"You're wrong," she said, not looking at him.* (versus *"You're wrong," she said coldly.*)

---

### 17. Throat-Clearing

**Symptom**: Paragraphs begin with setup for the actual sentence. "It was at this point that she realized..." "There was a sense of unease in the room..." "What happened next was..."

**Why it fails**: The setup delays the content without adding anything. It signals hesitancy in the writing.

**Diagnostic**: Check each sentence's first five words. How often are they scene-setting rather than content?

**Fix**: Cut to the content. What's the sentence actually saying? Start there.

---

### 18. Repetition Without Purpose

**Symptom**: The same information appears in different forms — dialogue states something that narration already established, or action shows something that was just described.

**Why it fails**: Every page has a limited economy of attention. Saying the same thing twice splits the attention without adding.

**Diagnostic**: After writing a scene, flag any moment where information appears twice. Ask: which version is stronger? Cut the other.

**Fix**: Trust the reader. Show it once, and well. They caught it.

---

### 19. Summary Disguised as Scene

**Symptom**: Important events are summarized rather than dramatized. "Over the next several weeks, they grew apart." "The argument that followed was terrible."

**Why it fails**: Summary tells the reader something happened. Scene lets them experience it. The most important moments in the story need to be *lived*, not reported.

**Diagnostic**: List your five most important story moments. Are they dramatized? Or summarized?

**Fix**: Dramatize the pivotal moments. Reserve summary for transitions and scenes that don't need to exist in real time.

---

## V. Dialogue Failures

*See `@references/dialogue-craft.md` for full dialogue treatment.*

### 20. On the Nose

**Symptom**: Characters say exactly what they mean, feel, and want. Subtext doesn't exist.

**Fix**: What would this character actually say, given their evasions, agendas, and relationships? Start there.

---

### 21. Everyone Sounds Alike

**Symptom**: Remove the character names from dialogue and you can't tell who's speaking.

**Fix**: Differentiate through vocabulary, sentence length, register, and what each character notices. See `@references/dialogue-craft.md` — Voice Differentiation.

---

### 22. Exposition Dump

**Symptom**: One character explains things to another character who already knows them. "As you know, we've been investigating this for six months..."

**Fix**: Embed exposition in conflict. Give one character genuine ignorance. Make revelation costly.

---

## VI. Pacing Failures

### 23. Unvaried Sentence Length

**Symptom**: All sentences follow the same rhythm — all short, or all long, or all medium. Prose reads as monotonous even when the events are exciting.

**Why it fails**: Rhythm is meaning. Short sentences create speed, urgency, impact. Long sentences create expansion, complexity, flow. Without variation, neither works.

**Diagnostic**: Read the last five pages aloud. Does the pace change when the scene demands it?

**Fix**: Short sentences for action, impact, revelation. Long sentences for interiority, description, tension that expands rather than snaps. Mix deliberately.

---

### 24. Equal Weight to Unequal Events

**Symptom**: The chapter where a character orders coffee receives as much prose attention as the chapter where their father dies.

**Why it fails**: Readers calibrate importance by page count. If everything gets the same treatment, nothing is important.

**Diagnostic**: Compare word count for your five most important scenes against your five least important. Are the proportions right?

**Fix**: Compress the small moments. Expand the major ones. Let significance be legible in the architecture.

## Quick Reference: Failure Levels

| Level | Common Failures | Where to Fix |
|-------|----------------|--------------|
| **Structure** | Passive protagonist, stalled tension, unearned ending | Before drafting or Pass 1 revision |
| **Character** | Static arc, cardboard antagonist, inconsistency | Pass 1-2 revision |
| **Scene** | No goal, late entry, emotional labeling | Pass 2 revision |
| **Prose** | Filtering, adverb dependence, repetition | Pass 3 revision |
| **Dialogue** | On the nose, uniform voice, exposition dump | Pass 2-3 revision |
| **Pacing** | Monotony, unequal weight | Pass 3 revision |
