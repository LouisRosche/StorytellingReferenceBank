# Dialogue Craft

Dialogue is action. Every line has a purpose: to reveal character, advance conflict, exchange information, or create subtext. Lines that do none of these should be cut.

**See also**: `@references/master-techniques.md` (The Voice, The Iceberg) | `@references/voice-direction-vocabulary.md` (TTS delivery)

---

## The Fundamental Rule

**Characters don't speak to communicate — they speak to get what they want.**

Real conversation is transactional, strategic, and evasive. People interrupt, deflect, change the subject, lie, and avoid. The character who always says exactly what they mean is not a character — they're a mouthpiece.

---

## I. Dialogue as Action

Every line of dialogue is a tactic. Map the scene before you write the words.

| Character | Wants | Tactic | Will use dialogue to... |
|-----------|-------|--------|------------------------|
| A | Confession | Pressure | Accuse, corner |
| B | Escape | Deflection | Change subject, redirect |

When tactics collide, dialogue generates conflict automatically.

### Scene Goal vs. Line Goal

- **Scene goal**: What the character wants by scene's end
- **Line goal**: What each specific line is trying to accomplish right now

Never write a line without knowing its line goal. "Just chatting" produces dead dialogue.

---

## II. Subtext

Subtext is the conversation beneath the conversation. Characters rarely say what they mean.

### The Iceberg Model Applied to Dialogue

| Surface | Subtext |
|---------|---------|
| "Nice sweater." | You look ridiculous. |
| "I'm fine." | I am not fine. |
| "Take care of yourself." | I'm done caring for you. |
| "We should do this again." | I hope we never do this again. |

### How to Create Subtext

1. Write the direct version: "I'm angry you didn't come."
2. Ask: What would they actually say? "I just thought you'd want to be there."
3. Ask: What does the listener hear? Everything unsaid.

### The Gap

The wider the gap between what's said and what's meant, the more tension. Subtext is not vagueness—it's precision about the unspoken thing.

## III. Voice Differentiation

Each character has a distinct voice. A reader should be able to remove dialogue tags and know who's speaking.

### Dimensions of Differentiation

| Dimension | Questions |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Vocabulary level** | Latinate or Anglo-Saxon? Technical or plain? |
| **Sentence length** | Short, declarative? Long, winding? |
| **Register** | Formal? Casual? Aggressive? Careful? |
| **What they notice** | What details do they reference? |
| **What they avoid** | What topics do they steer away from? |
| **Rhythm** | Do they complete sentences? Interrupt? Trail off? |
| **Humor style** | Dry? Broad? Dark? None? |

### Diagnostic Test

Copy three lines from three different characters. Remove the names. Can you tell them apart? If not, the voices haven't separated.

### Voice Markers in Practice

**Character A** (educated, evasive, formal):
> "I'm not sure that's entirely accurate. There were — circumstances."

**Character B** (direct, working-class, blunt):
> "He lied. That's what happened. He lied and we lost everything."

**Character C** (young, nervous, incomplete):
> "I thought maybe — I don't know. It seemed like it might have —"

## IV. Attribution and Action Beats

### Said Is Invisible

`said` disappears on the page. Readers process it and move on. Use it by default.

Verbs like `retorted`, `quipped`, `ejaculated`, `breathed` call attention to themselves — usually when you can't afford the distraction.

**Rule**: If you need a fancy attribution verb, the dialogue isn't doing its own work.

### Action Beats

An action beat grounds dialogue in the body. It shows how something was said without naming the emotion.

**With tag** (weaker): "You're lying," she said coldly.

**With action beat** (stronger): She set down her fork. "You're lying."

### When to Use Beats

- To show emotion through behavior (iceberg)
- To pace the scene — beats slow things down
- To indicate who's speaking without interrupting rhythm
- To anchor a long exchange in physical reality

### When to Use Nothing

Fast exchange: no attribution needed if the rhythm carries it.

> "You knew?"
> "For months."
> "How long is months?"
> "Since the funeral."

## V. Rhythm and Pace

Dialogue controls scene pace. Short exchanges accelerate; longer speeches slow.

### Short Lines = Tension

> "He knows."
> "He can't."
> "He does."
> "Then we're done."

### Long Speeches = Revelation or Character

Long speeches signal: this character is either revealing something important, or revealing themselves through how much they talk. Either is earned. Be wary of the speech that exists to convey plot information.

### The Interruption

Interruptions show power, urgency, or anxiety. Mark with em dash:

> "If you'd just let me ex—"
> "No. I've heard enough."

### Trailing Off

Ellipsis signals hesitation, incompletion, trailing thought:

> "I thought we were... I thought this was different."

## VI. Exposition in Dialogue

The two worst words in dialogue: *as you know*.

> "As you know, Bob, our department has been struggling since the merger." ← Never write this.

### How to Embed Exposition

1. **Give one character ignorance** — they genuinely need information; the other character tells them reluctantly
2. **Make exposition cost something** — revelations should carry emotional weight, not just information
3. **Filter through character** — two people can describe the same event differently; both versions reveal character
4. **Bury in conflict** — argument is a natural occasion for revelation

### The Confession Pattern

Information revealed under pressure — interrogation, confrontation, breakdown — feels earned. The reader receives exposition as drama, not as briefing.

---

## VII. Silence and What Isn't Said

The pause, the non-answer, the subject change: these are dialogue moves.

### Non-Answer as Answer

> "Did you love her?"
> [silence]
> "That's what I thought."

### Subject Change as Deflection

> "Where were you on Thursday?"
> "Have you eaten? I made something."

The deflection is its own answer. The reader tracks what was avoided.

### When to Break Dialogue

Sometimes the right move is no response. A character walks out. Hangs up. Looks away. The silence communicates more than a line could.

## VIII. Dialect and Register

### Dialect

Use sparingly and consistently. Heavy phonetic spelling slows reading and signals condescension.

**Over-written**: "Ah wuz jus' tryin' ta help, ya know?"

**Selective marker** (stronger): "I was just trying to help, you know?" + specific idiom + rhythm

One or two consistent markers establish dialect without the phonetic burden.

### Register Shift

When a character shifts register — from formal to casual, from calm to profane — it signals something has changed. Use it deliberately.

> "I appreciate your concern about the timeline. We're monitoring the situation closely." [then, alone] "Christ. We're completely screwed."

The shift shows what the public mask costs.

## IX. Diagnostic Questions

For every scene of dialogue, ask:

1. **What does each character want?** Not their long-term want — what do they want from this specific conversation?
2. **Are they getting it?** If yes immediately, add resistance.
3. **Could anyone say these lines?** If so, the voices aren't differentiated enough.
4. **What's the subtext?** What is nobody saying directly?
5. **Is any line "as you know"?** Find it. Cut it. Rebuild.
6. **Does attribution add or obstruct?** Remove unnecessary tags.
7. **What does the silence accomplish?** If nothing, find the silence.
8. **Has anything changed by the end?** Relationship, information, power — something should shift.

## X. Dialogue and TTS Production

When writing for audio narration:

### Speaker Clarity

The TTS parser must identify speakers. Ambiguous attribution creates errors.

**Ambiguous**: "She said it was fine," said the girl.

**Clear**: Luna said, "That's fine."

### Names in Dialogue

Natural speech rarely uses names. But for TTS multi-speaker mapping, names provide anchors. Place them at scene entry or after long gaps.

### Rhythm for the Ear

What reads smoothly on page may sound awkward aloud. Read every exchange aloud. Listen for:
- Sentences that require breath mid-word
- Identical sentence rhythms (monotony)
- Lines that are hard to deliver without sounding stilted

### Voice Persona Link

Each character's voice markers in dialogue should align with their TTS persona file. If a character speaks in short, clipped sentences, their persona should reflect low energy variance and measured pace. See `@templates/characters/character-sheet.md` — Voice and Speech section.

## XI. Common Dialogue Failures

| Failure | Symptom | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| **On the nose** | Characters say exactly what they mean | Add agenda, subtext, evasion |
| **Indistinct voices** | All characters sound the same | Differentiate vocabulary, rhythm, register |
| **Exposition dump** | One character briefing another | Break into conflict; give ignorance a cost |
| **Ping-pong** | Perfect, equal turn-taking | Add interruptions, non-answers, length variation |
| **Emotional label** | "I'm so angry," she said angrily | Show through word choice, syntax, action beat |
| **Purpose-free chat** | Pleasantries with no subtext | Cut or add a hidden agenda |
| **Dialect overload** | Phonetic spelling makes reading work | Use one or two markers consistently |
| **Action orphan** | Beat with no connection to next line | Integrate or cut the beat |
