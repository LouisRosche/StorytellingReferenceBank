# Scene-Sequel Pattern (Dwight Swain)

Paragraph-level pacing control. Alternates between **Scenes** (action) and **Sequels** (reaction). The atomic unit of narrative propulsion.

## Scene Structure

A Scene is a unit of **conflict**. Something is at stake; someone tries to get it.

| Element | Function | Example |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **Goal** | What protagonist wants in this scene | "Get the key from the guard" |
| **Conflict** | Obstacles to goal | Guard is suspicious, door is watched |
| **Disaster** | Outcome—things get worse | Gets key but triggers alarm |

### Goal Requirements

- **Specific**: Not "survive" but "reach the emergency exit"
- **Achievable in principle**: Must be possible to succeed
- **Clear stakes**: Reader knows what success/failure means

### Disaster Types

| Type | Outcome | Effect on Story |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| **No** | Outright failure | Must find new approach |
| **Yes, but...** | Success with complication | Victory is pyrrhic |
| **No, and furthermore...** | Failure plus new problem | Situation worsens |
| **Yes** | Clean success | Use sparingly; lowers tension |

## Sequel Structure

A Sequel is a unit of **transition**. Protagonist processes the disaster and commits to new action.

| Element | Function | Example |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **Reaction** | Emotional response to disaster | Fear, anger, despair |
| **Dilemma** | Weighing options | "Do I run or fight?" |
| **Decision** | Committing to action | "I'll fight" |

### The Decision-Goal Bridge

The Decision at the end of a Sequel becomes the Goal of the next Scene. This creates narrative propulsion:

```
Scene Goal → Conflict → Disaster
                ↓
        Sequel Reaction → Dilemma → Decision
                                        ↓
                            Scene Goal → Conflict → Disaster
                                            ↓
                                    (repeat)
```

## Pacing Control

**Fast pacing**: Compress or skip Sequels. Action to action.

```
[Scene: Goal-Conflict-Disaster]
[Scene: Goal-Conflict-Disaster]
[Scene: Goal-Conflict-Disaster]
```

Use for: Action sequences, chase scenes, climaxes

**Slow pacing**: Expand Sequels. Dwell in reaction, deepen dilemma.

```
[Scene: Goal-Conflict-Disaster]
[Sequel: Long reaction, extended dilemma, delayed decision]
[Scene: Goal-Conflict-Disaster]
```

Use for: Emotional beats, character development, processing major events

**Medium pacing**: Balance both.

## Sequel Depth Guide

| Story Moment | Sequel Length | Focus |
|--------------|---------------|-------|
| Minor setback | 1-2 paragraphs | Quick reaction, immediate decision |
| Moderate disaster | Several paragraphs | Some reflection, weigh 2-3 options |
| Major disaster | Full chapter | Deep emotional processing, multiple false decisions |
| All Is Lost | Multiple chapters | Extended dark night; protagonist must rebuild |

## Scene-Sequel Ratio by Genre

| Genre | Typical Ratio | Notes |
|-------|---------------|-------|
| Thriller | 4:1 Scenes | Action-heavy, minimal reflection |
| Literary | 1:2 Sequels | Heavy on interiority |
| Romance | 1:1 | Balances action and emotion |
| Horror | 3:1 Scenes | Sustains tension through relentless pressure |
| Epic Fantasy | 1:1 | World-building in Sequels |

## Common Problems

**Scene without Goal**: Meandering action; reader doesn't know stakes. Fix: Clarify what protagonist wants.

**Sequel without Dilemma**: Character immediately knows what to do. Fix: Present genuine competing options.

**Missing Disaster**: Scene ends in success without complication. Fix: Add "but..." or "and furthermore..."

**Endless Sequel**: Protagonist ruminates without deciding. Fix: External pressure forces decision.

## Micro-Application

Scene-Sequel can operate at paragraph level within larger structures:

```
[Paragraph 1: Mini-goal, mini-conflict, mini-disaster]
[Paragraph 2: Quick reaction, choice]
[Paragraph 3: New mini-goal...]
```

Useful for: Dialogue scenes, negotiations, chase sequences
