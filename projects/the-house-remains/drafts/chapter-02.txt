[NARRATOR]
The dining room table was set for six, though there would only be five. Eleanor had laid the place settings automatically—Ruth's chair at the head, her own at the foot, Marcus and Helen on either side, Sophie and Ray filling in. The empty chair would stay empty. No one would mention it.

Ray had arrived an hour after Marcus, bringing groceries Eleanor hadn't asked for and a calm presence she hadn't known she needed. He'd kissed her forehead, squeezed her shoulder, and set about making himself useful—carrying boxes, answering the phone when the funeral home called, keeping Marcus supplied with coffee and small talk.

They made a good team, Ray and Marcus. Both men preferred the practical to the emotional. They discussed flight delays and rental car companies while Eleanor stood at the stove, stirring a pot of chowder she didn't remember starting.

[RAY]
"You want me to take over there, Elle?"

[ELEANOR]
"I'm fine."

[RAY]
"You've been stirring that same pot for twenty minutes."

[NARRATOR]
She looked down. The chowder was beginning to stick to the bottom. She hadn't noticed.

[ELEANOR]
"It's fine. It's all fine."

[NARRATOR]
The words came out sharper than she intended. Ray and Marcus exchanged a glance she pretended not to see.

The doorbell rang at four-fifteen, and Eleanor felt her shoulders tighten. Helen. Of course Helen would ring the bell instead of walking in. Helen always needed to make an entrance.

[HELEN]
"Eleanor!"

[NARRATOR]
She swept through the door in a swirl of scarves and bracelets, her graying hair loose around her shoulders, her eyes already wet. Helen had been crying in the car, Eleanor could tell. Helen was always crying somewhere.

[HELEN]
"Oh, Elle, I can't believe it. I just can't believe she's gone."

[NARRATOR]
She wrapped Eleanor in an embrace that smelled of patchouli and something floral. Eleanor stood rigid in her arms, her own hands patting Helen's back in the automatic rhythm of comfort she didn't feel.

[ELEANOR]
"I know. I know."

[HELEN]
"I kept thinking I'd have more time. I thought we'd have Christmas together, at least. I was going to come for Christmas."

[NARRATOR]
You didn't come last Christmas, Eleanor thought. Or the one before.

But she didn't say it. She never said it.

[SOPHIE]
"Hi, Aunt Eleanor."

[NARRATOR]
Sophie stood in the doorway, a duffel bag over her shoulder, looking uncertain about whether to enter. At twenty-five, she had her mother's height and her father's caution—a combination that made her seem perpetually on the verge of apologizing.

[ELEANOR]
"Sophie. Come in, sweetheart. You must be exhausted from the drive."

[SOPHIE]
"I'm okay. I'm really sorry about Grandma."

[NARRATOR]
Eleanor pulled her niece into a hug that felt easier than Helen's. Sophie was uncomplicated. Sophie was young enough to grieve without agenda.

[MARCUS]
"Helen."

[NARRATOR]
Marcus had emerged from the kitchen, coffee mug in hand, his face arranged in the expression Eleanor recognized from their father's funeral: politely present, emotionally unreachable.

[HELEN]
"Marcus. You look thin."

[MARCUS]
"You look exactly the same."

[NARRATOR]
It was not a compliment. Helen heard it anyway.

[HELEN]
"I've been painting again. It takes it out of you, the creative work. But Mother always said—"

[ELEANOR]
"Let's get you settled. Sophie, you're in the blue room. Helen, I put you in Mother's—in the guest room."

[NARRATOR]
She caught herself. The guest room was what they'd always called it, but it had been Ruth's room for the past two years, after the stairs became too difficult. The master bedroom upstairs sat empty now, a museum to a marriage that ended fifteen years ago.

The house was full of rooms that were one thing and another thing simultaneously. Eleanor had never noticed how heavy that was until now.

[HELEN]
"I'd like to see her room. Mother's room, I mean. Where she... where it happened."

[ELEANOR]
"She died at the hospital, Helen."

[HELEN]
"But her room. Her space. I'd like to feel her presence there."

[NARRATOR]
Of course you would, Eleanor thought. Of course you need to commune with her spirit now that her body is inconveniently absent.

[RAY]
"Dinner's almost ready. Why don't we get you settled first, then eat? Eleanor's made chowder."

[HELEN]
"I'm not really hungry."

[MARCUS]
"You'll eat anyway. It's what we do."

[NARRATOR]
The sharpness in his voice surprised Eleanor. Usually Marcus saved his edge for her.

Helen blinked. Then she picked up her overnight bag and headed for the stairs, Sophie trailing behind her. Eleanor watched them go and felt something loosen slightly in her chest.

[RAY]
"That went well."

[MARCUS]
"That went exactly as expected."

[ELEANOR]
"Both of you. Stop."

[NARRATOR]
But there was no heat in it. The truth was, they were right. Helen's arrival had followed the script they all knew by heart: the performative grief, the need to be the most affected, the gentle competition for the position of chief mourner.

What none of them would say out loud was that Eleanor had earned that position. Eleanor had been here. Eleanor had held their mother's hand as the machines beeped and the monitors flatlined and the nurses exchanged glances that said we've seen this before, it's almost over, there's nothing more to do.

Helen had been in Vermont, painting.

Marcus had been in Seattle, living his separate life.

And Eleanor had been here, as she always was, as she always had been, holding everything together while it fell apart.

[HELEN]

[NARRATOR]

[MARCUS]

[HELEN]

[ELEANOR]

[HELEN]

[ELEANOR]
"Mother said a lot of things."

[NARRATOR]

[SOPHIE]

[MARCUS]
"She was strict. Fair, but strict. Everything had a place."

[HELEN]
"She was magical. She told the best stories. She believed in things—spirits, signs, the old ways."

[ELEANOR]

[NARRATOR]
Three descriptions of the same woman. Three children who had grown up in the same house with the same mother and remembered entirely different people.

[SOPHIE]
"That's funny. How you all see her differently."

[MARCUS]

[HELEN]

[ELEANOR]

[HELEN]
"Eleanor—"

[ELEANOR]

[MARCUS]
"Yes, you did."

[NARRATOR]

[MARCUS]

[ELEANOR]

[MARCUS]

[NARRATOR]

[ELEANOR]

[MARCUS]

[HELEN]

[ELEANOR]
"Mother died two weeks ago, Helen. While I was holding her hand. Where were you?"

[NARRATOR]
The silence that followed was absolute. Sophie stared at her bowl. Ray stared at his hands. Helen's eyes filled with tears—real ones, this time, Eleanor thought, though she was no longer sure she could tell the difference.

[HELEN]
"That's not fair."

[ELEANOR]
"No. It's not."

[NARRATOR]
Eleanor stood, her chair scraping against the floor. She didn't know where she was going—only that she couldn't stay in this room, at this table, with these people who shared her blood and nothing else.

[ELEANOR]
"I need some air."

[NARRATOR]
She walked out the back door and kept walking until she reached the edge of the property, where the grass gave way to rocks and the rocks gave way to the sea. The tide was coming in. The waves crashed against the shore with the same rhythm they'd had her whole life—before her father died, before her mother died, before any of them were born or would be remembered.

The ocean didn't care about their arguments. The ocean didn't care about anything.

She stood there until the light began to fade and the cold crept through her sweater. Then she turned back toward the house.

Through the kitchen window, she could see them: Ray doing the dishes, Helen drying, Marcus and Sophie sitting at the table with cups of tea. They were talking. Not fighting. Just talking.

They had managed fine without her.

They always did.

[NARRATOR]
She went back inside without comment. No one asked where she'd been. No one mentioned the argument. They had all become experts, over the years, at pretending the hard things hadn't happened.

Later that night, after everyone had gone to bed, Eleanor stood once more in the doorway of her mother's room. The locked box sat on the dresser where she'd left it, its brass fittings dull in the moonlight.

She still couldn't bring herself to cross the threshold.

But tomorrow, she would have to. Tomorrow, they would have to decide what to do about the box.

Tomorrow, everything would change.

She just didn't know it yet.
