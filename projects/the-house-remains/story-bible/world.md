# World: The House Remains

## Primary Setting: The Callahan House

### Location
Port Clyde, Maine - a working fishing village at the tip of the St. George Peninsula. Population under 100 year-round, swelling in summer. The kind of place where everyone knows whose car is whose, where boats matter more than credentials, where the land ends and the sea begins without ceremony.

### The House
**Built**: 1892 by Captain Isaiah Callahan (Ruth's husband's grandfather)
**Style**: Victorian Gothic Revival with New England adaptations - steep gables, widow's walk, weathered cedar shingles gone silver with salt
**Condition**: Well-maintained but showing age. Ruth refused to "modernize" - original hardware, original wood floors, storm windows that stick

**Layout**:
- First floor: Front parlor (formal, rarely used), living room with fieldstone fireplace, kitchen with breakfast nook, mudroom with boot rack and oilskins
- Second floor: Master bedroom (Ruth's), three smaller bedrooms (children's rooms, unchanged since they left), one bathroom with clawfoot tub
- Third floor: Attic converted to "the dormitory" - bunk room for grandchildren
- Basement: Stone foundation, root cellar, heating system, the accumulated detritus of 130 years

**The Widow's Walk**:
A defining feature. Accessed by a narrow stair behind a door everyone assumed was a closet. Ruth went up there daily, weather permitting. "To check the water," she said, though there was nothing to check. Her chair remains there, wooden, cracked, positioned to see the harbor and beyond it, the ocean.

### Objects of Significance

**The Locked Box**: Cherry wood, 12x8x6 inches, brass hardware. Made by a local craftsman Ruth knew. No visible lock mechanism - the lock is inside, released by a skeleton key through a hidden keyhole in the base. Contains: letters, photographs, the complete archive of Ruth's relationship with Sarah.

**Ruth's Sewing Table**: Maple, three drawers, one with a false bottom. Where the first letters are found. Ruth was always sewing something - a way to keep hands busy during conversation, to avoid eye contact when needed.

**The Kitchen Table**: Round, oak, six chairs but only one with arms (Ruth's place). Every family conversation of consequence happened here. Cigarette burn from when Marcus was seventeen. Water stain from a forgotten vase.

**The Photograph Wall**: Hallway leading to the stairs. Generations of Callahans. Ruth added one picture of Sarah, disguised among cousins - a test to see if anyone would notice. No one did.

## Seasonal/Temporal Context

**Time**: Late October, approximately ten days after Ruth's death
**Weather**: Post-foliage gray. Temperature in the 40s. Fog common. Rain threatens but hasn't fallen.
**Light**: Dim. Days short. Lamps needed by 4pm.
**Sound**: Halyards on the few boats still in the harbor. Gulls. The harbor seals sometimes. Wind through the eaves.

The timing matters: this is the dying time in Maine. Summer people gone. Most boats hauled. The peninsula closing in on itself for winter. The landscape mirrors the family's mood - contracted, gray, waiting.

## Social Context

### Port Clyde
- Population knows the family, has known them for generations
- Ruth was respected, slightly feared, definitely private
- The Callahans are "from here" - not wash-ashores, not summer people. This grants status and scrutiny.
- Pastor Oakes serves the small Methodist church where Ruth attended sporadically
- Gossip travels fast; the family assumes everyone will know about Sarah after the funeral

### Family History (Public Version)
- Isaiah Callahan built the house on lobstering money
- His son Harold married a Portland girl, Ruth's mother-in-law
- Harold's son Edward (Ruth's husband) ran the post office, died 2001
- Ruth raised three children here: Eleanor, Marcus, Helen
- Ruth was a teacher's aide, a library volunteer, on the historical society board
- A respectable life, quietly lived

### Family History (Hidden Version)
- Ruth came to Port Clyde in 1957, two years after giving up her daughter
- She came from Bangor, where people knew
- She reinvented herself completely: new town, new name (maiden name), new start
- Met Edward at the library, married 1959
- Never told him about Sarah - or so the children believe (truth more complicated)
- Reconnected with Sarah in 2009 when Sarah found her through DNA testing
- Sarah lives in Nova Scotia, ninety minutes by ferry from Portland
- Ruth visited her twice a year, telling the children she was visiting "her friend Judith" in Brunswick

## Atmosphere and Sensory Notes

### Smell
- Salt, always
- Ruth's Shalimar perfume, fading but still present in the bedroom
- Woodsmoke from the fireplace (Eleanor lights it for the first time since Ruth's death)
- Old house smell: wood, must, generations

### Sound
- The house creaks, settles, speaks in its own language
- Wind off the water
- Silence heavy between conversations
- The clock in the front parlor still ticking (Ruth wound it every Sunday)

### Touch
- Cold surfaces - the house has been empty, heating minimal
- Wooden banisters worn smooth
- Ruth's quilt on the master bed - wedding ring pattern, made by her mother
- Paper of the letters - slightly stiff with age, Ruth's handwriting slanting right

### Light
- Gray predominant - fog diffuses everything
- Lamplight creates pools of warmth in a cold house
- The widow's walk catches what light exists

## Symbolic Elements

**The Ocean**: Always visible, always present. Represents the unknowable, the vast, that which exists beyond our control. The saltwater of the title.

**The Fog**: Obscures, softens, hides. Mirrors how Ruth lived - everything slightly unclear, boundaries blurred.

**The Widow's Walk**: Originally for watching for returning ships. Ruth watched for something she never named - forgiveness? Sarah? Her own past? The walk represents vigil, waiting, the space between hope and acceptance.

**The Locked Box**: Secrets contained, protected, inaccessible without the key. The key hidden but findable - Ruth wanted it found, eventually, but couldn't bring herself to unlock it herself.

## Rules of the World

### Emotional Rules
- These are repressed New Englanders. Feelings are implied, not declared.
- Physical affection is rare; significant when it occurs.
- Silence is a form of speech.
- The house holds memory - objects trigger involuntary remembering.

### Practical Rules
- Cell service is spotty - calls happen in specific locations.
- The nearest town with real stores is Thomaston, 30 minutes.
- People drop by without calling - it's that kind of place.
- Food appears: casseroles from neighbors, baked goods from church.

### Narrative Rules
- The house is almost a character - give it presence.
- Weather reflects emotional state without being on-the-nose.
- Objects tell stories - let them.
- The past is always present; memory bleeds into now.
