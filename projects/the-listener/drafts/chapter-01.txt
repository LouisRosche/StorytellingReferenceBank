The appointment book showed three patients for Friday: a recurring panic disorder at nine, a couples session at eleven, and a new intake at two. Sarah Mercer reviewed her notes for each as she waited for the coffee to brew, the morning light slanting through the blinds of her home office.

She had designed this space herself—warm grays, soft lighting, two leather chairs angled toward each other with the precise geometry of confession. A box of tissues on the side table, always full. A small clock on the bookshelf, positioned so she could see it without her patients noticing her glance.

Eight years of practice had taught her the importance of such details. People came to therapy vulnerable, and the room needed to hold that vulnerability without amplifying it.

The coffee maker chimed. Sarah poured herself a cup and checked her phone: two texts from Owen about dinner, one from Emma asking for a ride home from soccer. The ordinary arithmetic of family life.

She answered both, then turned her phone face-down on her desk. The morning awaited.

[SARAH]
"Good morning, David. Come in."

[NARRATOR]
David Chen, fifty-two, software engineer, had been seeing her for eighteen months. His panic attacks had begun after a car accident that killed his passenger—a colleague, a friend. He blamed himself, though the other driver had run a red light. Survivor's guilt was a patient companion, and Sarah had learned not to rush its departure.

For fifty minutes, she listened. She reflected. She gently challenged the cognitive distortions that kept David trapped in his loop of self-recrimination. When the session ended, his shoulders were slightly lower than when he'd arrived. Progress, measured in millimeters.

[SARAH]
"Same time next week?"

[DAVID]
"Same time."

[NARRATOR]
He left, and Sarah wrote her notes. She had forty-five minutes before the couples session—time for a walk around the block, time to clear the residue of one patient before taking on two more.

But instead, she found herself standing at the window, looking out at the empty street.

Maya would have been thirty-seven this year.

Sarah rarely let herself think about it directly. The thought was a door she kept locked, checking occasionally to ensure the lock held. But sometimes, on quiet mornings, the lock slipped.

Her sister had been twenty-seven when she died. Sarah had been thirty-two, newly licensed, just starting her practice. She remembered the phone call from their mother—the raw, animal sound of grief that told her everything before words could. She remembered the flight home, the funeral, the police interviews that led nowhere.

Ten years. The man who killed Maya had never been found.

Sarah turned from the window. The Hendersons were due in fifteen minutes, and she needed to prepare. Marital discord over finances—manageable, familiar, blessedly un-tragic.

The lock held. She moved on.

The form was sparse—no previous psychiatric history, no medications, no emergency contacts listed. Just a name and a presenting complaint.

At 1:58, her doorbell rang.

[SARAH]
"Mr. Vane?"

[NARRATOR]

His face was pleasant and unremarkable—the kind of face you might pass on the street without noticing. He smiled when he saw her, and his smile was warm.

[THOMAS]
"Dr. Mercer. Thank you for fitting me in."

[SARAH]
"Please, come in."

[NARRATOR]
She led him to her office, gestured to the patient's chair. He sat with the ease of someone comfortable in his own body, crossing one ankle over the opposite knee.

Sarah settled into her own chair, notepad balanced on her knee.

[SARAH]
"So, Thomas. What brings you to therapy?"

[NARRATOR]
He was quiet for a moment, as if considering the question carefully. When he spoke, his voice was measured, articulate.

[THOMAS]
"I have thoughts. About a woman who died."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah kept her face neutral. This was the work—receiving whatever a patient offered without judgment, without visible reaction.

[SARAH]
"Can you tell me more about these thoughts?"

[THOMAS]
"They're intrusive. Persistent. I find myself thinking about her at odd moments—when I'm working, when I'm eating dinner, when I'm trying to sleep. I can't seem to let her go."

[SARAH]
"Was this woman someone close to you?"

[NARRATOR]
Again, that pause. His eyes met hers—gray-blue, intelligent, faintly searching.

[THOMAS]
"I knew her. Yes. I felt—I feel—a connection to her that I've never quite understood."

[SARAH]
"When did she die?"

[THOMAS]
"Ten years ago."

[NARRATOR]
Something flickered at the edge of Sarah's awareness. A coincidence, surely. People died all the time. The number was meaningless.

[SARAH]
"And these intrusive thoughts—when did they begin?"

[THOMAS]
"They never stopped. They've been with me since the day she died. But lately, they've become more... demanding."

[SARAH]
"Demanding how?"

[THOMAS]
"I feel compelled to understand. To make sense of what happened between us. I thought perhaps talking to someone might help me process it."

[NARRATOR]
It was a reasonable presentation. Grief complicated by some unresolved relationship—an affair, perhaps, or a friendship that had ended badly before her death. Sarah had treated similar cases before.

And yet.

There was something in the way he looked at her. Something in the precision of his words. Something that made the fine hairs on her arms rise in attention.

She pushed the feeling aside. Intuition was a useful tool, but it could also lead astray. Better to gather data, to let the patient reveal himself in his own time.

[SARAH]
"Tell me about her. What was she like?"

[NARRATOR]
Thomas's face softened. It was almost tender, the way he smiled.

[THOMAS]

[NARRATOR]
Sarah's pen stopped moving.

Maya had slept with her curtains open. Their mother used to scold her for it—anyone could see in, she'd say. Maya would just shrug. I like the moonlight, she'd answer. It keeps me company.

Coincidence. It had to be coincidence. Thousands of people slept with their curtains open.

[SARAH]
"It sounds like you observed her closely."

[THOMAS]

[SARAH]
"Were you in a romantic relationship?"

[NARRATOR]

[THOMAS]

[SARAH]
"What kind of obstacles?"

[THOMAS]

[NARRATOR]
The room seemed to contract. Sarah felt her heart rate spike, felt the familiar flood of adrenaline she had spent years learning to control.

This was nothing. This was a patient with a sad story about an unrequited love. The details were echoes, phantoms, the pattern-matching of a mind still haunted by loss.

[SARAH]
"The sister—did you know her?"

[THOMAS]
"Only from a distance. But I watched her too, sometimes. She seemed so capable. So in control. I often wondered what it would be like to talk to her."

[NARRATOR]

[SARAH]

[NARRATOR]

[THOMAS]
"Does it matter? She's gone. Names are for the living."

[SARAH]

[THOMAS]
"Why?"

[NARRATOR]

[SARAH]
"Because I'm trying to understand you, Thomas. That's my job. And I can't do that job if I feel you're withholding information."

[THOMAS]

None of this was evidence of anything except her own unresolved grief. She was projecting. She was seeing patterns that weren't there. She was being, in the clinical terminology she taught to her supervisees, counter-transferential.

[SARAH]

[SARAH]

[NARRATOR]

[SARAH]

[THOMAS]

[SARAH]

[SARAH]

[THOMAS]
"Of course."

[NARRATOR]

[THOMAS]

She was being ridiculous. She was a trained professional with nearly a decade of experience. She knew the difference between intuition and paranoia, between clinical judgment and personal projection.

And yet.

She walked to her desk and opened her laptop. Typed his name into the search engine: Thomas Vane.

The results were unremarkable. An architect with a small practice in the city. A few articles in trade publications. A professional website with a tasteful portfolio of residential projects. No criminal record. No red flags.

She should stop. She was violating the trust that should exist between therapist and patient. She was letting her own unresolved trauma contaminate her clinical judgment.

But she didn't stop. She refined her search: Thomas Vane, Seattle. Thomas Vane, 2014. Thomas Vane, Maya.

Nothing. Nothing connected him to her sister. Nothing suggested he was anything other than what he appeared to be: a successful professional struggling with complicated grief.

Sarah closed the laptop. Rubbed her eyes. Checked her phone and saw that Owen had texted again—picking up Emma, home by six, Thai or Italian?

Italian, she typed back. Then: Love you.

Love you too, came the reply.

The ordinary arithmetic of family life. The antidote to the darkness that sometimes crept in.

Sarah left her office and locked the door behind her. In the hallway, she paused to look at the photograph on the wall—she and Maya, ages twelve and seven, building a sandcastle on the beach. Their mother had taken it. It was the last photo of them together before everything changed.

[SARAH]
"I'm being crazy. Aren't I?"

[NARRATOR]
The photograph didn't answer. Photographs never did.

That night, Sarah dreamed of her sister for the first time in months. They were in the old apartment, the one Maya had died in, and Maya was trying to tell her something important. But every time she opened her mouth, no sound came out.

Sarah woke at three a.m., drenched in sweat, her husband's arm heavy across her chest.

She lay in the dark, listening to Owen breathe, and tried to convince herself that Thomas Vane was just a patient. Just a man with intrusive thoughts about a woman who happened to share certain traits with her dead sister.

But she couldn't quite believe it.

And somewhere in the city, in an apartment she would never see, Thomas Vane was also awake. He was looking at a photograph—an old one, yellowed at the edges—of a woman sleeping, curtains open, moonlight on her face.

She had never known he was there.
