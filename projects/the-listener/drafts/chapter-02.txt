[NARRATOR]
The nightmare was always the same.

Sarah stood in Maya's apartment, the one on Belmont she'd helped her sister move into two months before she died. Everything was wrong—the furniture too large, the windows too small, the light coming from the wrong direction. Maya was there, sitting on the couch, and she was trying to tell Sarah something important.

But every time Sarah got close enough to hear, the phone began to ring.

Not the phone in the dream. Sarah's actual phone, on the nightstand, shattering the darkness at 3:47 a.m.

She woke with her heart pounding and her t-shirt damp with sweat. Beside her, Owen stirred but didn't wake. He slept through everything. It was one of the things she both loved and resented about him.

The phone stopped ringing. Then started again.

[SARAH]
"Hello?"

[CARLA]
"Dr. Mercer? This is the answering service. I have an urgent message from a patient."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah sat up, her training kicking in past the fog of the dream. Urgent meant suicidal ideation. Urgent meant potential harm. Urgent meant someone needed her right now.

[SARAH]
"Who is it?"

[CARLA]
"A Mr. David Chen. He says he's having a panic attack and can't reach the crisis line."

[NARRATOR]
David. Not surprising. He'd been escalating for weeks—the anniversary of the accident approaching like a storm front he couldn't outrun.

[SARAH]
"Patch him through."

[NARRATOR]
For the next forty minutes, Sarah talked David down from the ledge. Not a literal ledge—David wasn't suicidal, just terrified. His panic attacks convinced him he was dying, that his heart was giving out, that he would suffocate in his own bed. She knew the script. Breathe with me, David. Name five things you can see. You're safe. This will pass. It always passes.

By the time she hung up, the first gray light was creeping through the blinds. Owen rolled toward her, his hand finding her hip.

[OWEN]
"Everything okay?"

[SARAH]
"David. Panic attack."

[OWEN]
"That's the third time this month."

[SARAH]
"I know."

[NARRATOR]
She could hear the concern in his voice, wrapped in something else—frustration, maybe, or the particular exhaustion of being married to someone who was always on call for other people's emergencies.

[OWEN]
"You should refer him out. To someone with more bandwidth."

[SARAH]
"He trusts me."

[OWEN]
"And you're stretched thin. We both are."

[NARRATOR]
He was right. She was stretched thin. The practice was growing faster than she'd anticipated, and she'd taken on three new patients last month when she shouldn't have taken any. But she couldn't say no. Couldn't turn away someone who needed help.

Couldn't stop trying to save people who reminded her of the one she couldn't save.

[SARAH]
"I'll think about it."

[OWEN]
"That means no."

[SARAH]
"It means I'll think about it."

[NARRATOR]
She leaned down and kissed him—a peace offering, a deflection, both at once. He accepted it the way he accepted most things from her: with a patience that sometimes felt like distance.

[NARRATOR]
The office of Dr. Ruth Henley smelled like old books and Earl Grey tea, the two constants in Ruth's professional life for the past forty years. Ruth herself was seventy-three, officially retired but still maintaining a small supervision practice for therapists she considered worth her time. Sarah had been coming to her for eight years, first as a supervisee, then as a colleague, now as something harder to categorize.

Ruth Henley was the closest thing Sarah had to a mother figure, though she would never say that out loud.

[RUTH]
"You look tired."

[SARAH]
"I had a late night. David Chen."

[RUTH]
"The panic attacks."

[SARAH]
"He's getting worse as the anniversary approaches."

[RUTH]
"And you're absorbing it."

[NARRATOR]
It wasn't a question. Ruth had a way of stating things that made denial feel pointless.

[SARAH]
"I'm managing."

[RUTH]
"Are you? When did you last take a vacation?"

[SARAH]
"We went to the coast in June."

[RUTH]
"Three nights doesn't count. When did you last take a real vacation—two weeks, no patients, no phone?"

[NARRATOR]
Sarah thought about it. The answer was before Maya died. The answer was a lifetime ago.

[SARAH]
"I don't need a vacation. I need to get David through October."

[RUTH]
"And after October? There will be another crisis. There always is. That's the nature of this work."

[NARRATOR]
Ruth poured tea from the ceramic pot she'd had as long as Sarah could remember—green with gold trim, slightly chipped at the spout. The gesture was ritual, as much a part of their sessions as the conversation itself.

[RUTH]
"You've increased your caseload again."

[SARAH]
"I took on three new patients."

[RUTH]
"Why?"

[SARAH]

[RUTH]
"Many people need help. Not all of them need help from you specifically."

[SARAH]
"What are you suggesting?"

[RUTH]

[SARAH]
"Because that's my job."

[RUTH]

[NARRATOR]

[SARAH]
"I'm not avoiding anything."

[RUTH]
"The wound we don't examine is the one that festers."

[NARRATOR]

[SARAH]

[RUTH]
"You haven't spoken about her in months."

[SARAH]
"There's nothing new to say. She's been dead for ten years."

[SARAH]

[RUTH]
"Sarah. I'm not trying to push you somewhere you're not ready to go. But I've watched you for eight years. You're a brilliant therapist. You're also a woman who has built her entire identity around being in control. And I worry about what happens when that control cracks."

[SARAH]

[RUTH]

[SARAH]

[NARRATOR]
The session ended the way their sessions always ended: with Ruth's gentle persistence and Sarah's polite deflection, neither of them quite willing to push past the boundaries they'd carefully negotiated over eight years.

But as Sarah walked to her car, Ruth's words echoed in her mind.

The wound we don't examine is the one that festers.

She thought about Maya's apartment. About the four phone calls she'd ignored because she was tired, because Maya was always having some crisis, because she'd finally decided to set boundaries the way her therapist at the time had suggested.

She'd set boundaries.

And Maya had died alone.

Run.

She woke up gasping, heart pounding, the word still echoing in her ears.

On the nightstand, her phone showed one new notification: a confirmed appointment for tomorrow afternoon.

Just a dream.

Nothing more.
