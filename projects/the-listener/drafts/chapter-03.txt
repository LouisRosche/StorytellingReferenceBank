[NARRATOR]
The weekend had felt like waiting.

Not the productive kind—not the kind where you work through a problem and arrive, Monday morning, at a solution. The kind where you go through the motions of a normal life while something sits behind your sternum, dense and unmoving. Sarah had made dinner on Saturday. Had stood at Emma's soccer game on Sunday with Owen's arm around her shoulders, watching her daughter sprint down a muddy field in the November drizzle. She had smiled at the right moments. Said the right things. Been, to all appearances, present.

And behind her eyes, Thomas Vane had waited.

She had told herself, repeatedly, that she was being foolish. That the details she had seized upon—a woman who died ten years ago, a protective sister, curtains open for moonlight—were coincidences she was inflating into significance because she was still, after a decade, looking for something to name. That was what good clinical training looked like from the inside: you recognized the mechanism. You named it. Projection. Counter-transference. Grief constructing a face for itself out of available material.

She had repeated these words through two nights of broken sleep and arrived at her office Monday morning with Thomas Vane's intake form already open on her desk.

[SARAH]
"Stop it."

[NARRATOR]
She said it aloud, to the empty room, and felt ridiculous. Then picked up her phone and searched his name anyway.

Thomas Vane. Architect. The results were clean, professional, unremarkable. The website for his firm—Whitmore & Associates—carried a portfolio of residential work: a cantilevered house on a hillside outside the city, a converted warehouse in the arts district, a church renovation that had placed in the AIA awards two years ago. His biography was brief and precise: Princeton-educated, partner level, fifteen years of practice, a list of civic organizations he supported in amounts that suggested both wealth and calculation.

His photograph looked the same as the man she'd seen across her office. Pale blue eyes. Neat dark hair. That near-smile that committed to nothing.

She searched more specifically. Architectural awards ceremony, 2013. A trade publication article surfaced: a list of young designers to watch, five names, his third from the top. Smaller photograph, slightly younger. She studied it. He had been here then. In this city. The year before Maya died.

Which proved nothing. She had been here too.

She tried a different search. Thomas Vane, Seattle. An old LinkedIn profile, mostly dormant: he had been with a smaller firm there for three years before joining Whitmore. The dates overlapped.

Maya had lived in Seattle. Had moved there for her graduate program. Art history, University of Washington.

Sarah set the phone face-down on her desk. She was being ridiculous. There were four hundred thousand people in Seattle. Architects worked in cities. Graduate students lived in cities. Coincidence was not evidence. Coincidence was coincidence.

She was still sitting with her hands flat on the desk, pressing them down to stop the trembling she could feel building at her wrists, when Carla's voice came through the intercom.

[CARLA]
"Dr. Mercer? Your nine o'clock is here."

[NARRATOR]
David Chen was wearing the same jacket he always wore to sessions—a dark blue windbreaker that had seen too many winters. He settled into his chair with the practiced ease of a man who had been doing this for eighteen months, crossed his ankle over his knee, and looked at her.

[DAVID]
"You look different."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah kept her expression neutral. The professional mask, worn so long it sometimes felt structural.

[SARAH]
"Different how?"

[DAVID]
"I don't know. Somewhere else."

[NARRATOR]
He said it without judgment, which was almost worse. David Chen was, in addition to his many other qualities, observant. It was part of what made his grief so persistent—he noticed things, catalogued them, turned them over looking for evidence of his own culpability. That observational reflex didn't turn off at the door of her office.

[SARAH]
"I'm here, David. Tell me how the week went."

[NARRATOR]
He told her. She listened. She asked the questions she should ask, reflected what he offered, navigated the approach of the accident anniversary with the practiced care of someone who understood that grief had topography—that getting someone through October meant knowing every slope. She did the work. She was competent. She was present.

But in the small, unguarded space between his sentences, she kept returning to Seattle. To the overlapping dates. To the photo from 2013 and the way something about it refused to settle.

David paused mid-sentence.

[DAVID]
"You just went somewhere again."

[SARAH]
"I'm sorry. I'm listening."

[DAVID]
"It's fine if you have something going on. You're allowed to be a person."

[NARRATOR]
She almost laughed at that. Allowed to be a person—as if it were a permission she could grant herself, like extended office hours or a parking permit.

[SARAH]
"I appreciate that. But this hour is yours. Where were we?"

[NARRATOR]
By the time David left, she had written three lines in her session notes and would have to reconstruct the rest from memory. She sat for a moment after his footsteps faded down the hall, looking at the sparse notations, and felt the particular shame of a therapist who had given a patient less than she owed him.

She pulled up the search again.

Thomas Vane, University of Washington, 2013. Nothing direct. She tried: Thomas Vane, Seattle, 2014. An archived news item from a local outlet—a brief mention in a piece about a community board meeting, his name among a list of attendees, an address in the Capitol Hill neighborhood.

Maya had lived in Capitol Hill.

Sarah's hand went still over the keyboard. She was aware of the quality of the silence in the room—the white noise machine outside the door, the radiator's occasional clank, the muffled sound of rain against the windows. The ordinary sounds of an ordinary November morning. Everything normal except the specific weight of what she was holding.

She closed the laptop. Opened it again. Typed: Maya Aldridge, Seattle, 2014.

The results came up as they always did when she searched her sister's name: the brief news items from the time, the police report citations, the online obituary their mother had written and that remained, ten years later, the top result. Beloved daughter and sister. Graduate student of art history. Died November 16th in what police described as a home invasion. No arrests.

Sarah closed the laptop. This time she kept it closed.

She made tea. The kettle was heating when her phone buzzed—Carla, texting rather than using the intercom: *Couples cancelled for 11. You have a gap. I can try to fill it.*

Sarah typed back: *Leave it.*

She needed the hour to think. She needed the hour to talk herself back from the edge of something that, named aloud, would sound exactly like what it was: a woman ten years into unresolved grief, constructing a monster out of an architecture firm and two coincidental cities.

She was a trained clinician. She understood projection. She understood that the mind under stress was a meaning-making machine that would impose pattern on noise because pattern was bearable and noise was not.

She knew all of this.

She also knew what Maya had told her, in the year before she died, about the man she couldn't shake. The one who'd asked her out at a bookstore and taken the rejection too gracefully. The car that appeared in her parking lot. The feeling of being watched that Maya had described as weather—persistent, sourceless, changing the quality of the air.

Sarah had listened. Had offered the standard frameworks: cognitive distortions, hypervigilance, anxiety spiraling. Had gently suggested that Maya's imagination was vivid and her feelings were real but might not be tracking something real. Had been, in the terminology she would later use only in her own head, precisely wrong.

She picked up her tea. Sat at the window. The street below was wet, the morning light already thinning toward the early dark that November brought, bare branches against a sky the color of old pewter.

What was it he had said? About the woman. *She was afraid of the dark, but she always slept with her curtains open because she loved the moon.*

And before that: *She had a sister. The sister was very protective. Too protective, perhaps.*

Too protective. Sarah turned the phrase in her mind. That particular word—too—with its implication of excessive, misaligned, embarrassing. As if protectiveness were a character flaw. As if the older sister's concern had been an obstacle rather than a reasonable human response to a man who—

She stopped herself.

She did not know that Thomas Vane was that man. She had a feeling. She had a cluster of coincidences that her grief had welded into a structure. She had no evidence of anything except her own inability to be fully present with a patient she'd been seeing for eighteen months.

The intercom buzzed.

[CARLA]
"Dr. Mercer? Just confirming—your Wednesday three o'clock is confirmed. Mr. Vane."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah looked at her calendar. Wednesday at three. Two days away.

[SARAH]
"Thank you, Carla."

[NARRATOR]
She set down her tea. Opened her session notes from Friday. Read through them slowly, as she should have done over the weekend. She had written careful observations: the patient's presentation, affect, stated history, treatment goals. Everything clinical and measured.

At the bottom, in her own handwriting—the looping lefty script she'd never quite made neat—she had written one line that she didn't remember writing.

*He pauses before answering. Not thinking. Savoring.*

She looked at it for a long time. Then she picked up her pen and drew a single careful line through it, until the words were gone.

It didn't help. She could still read them underneath.
