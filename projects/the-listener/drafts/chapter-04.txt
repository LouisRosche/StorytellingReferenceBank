[NARRATOR]
He arrived at two fifty-eight.

Sarah heard the front door of the building open—she always heard it, had trained herself to note arrivals with the peripheral awareness of someone who managed her environment the way a surgeon managed a sterile field—and then Carla's muffled greeting, and then the particular quality of silence that followed. Not the silence of a waiting room at rest. Something denser.

She had two minutes. She used them the way she used every difficult two minutes: she breathed, organized her attention, and chose a face.

When Carla buzzed, she was ready.

[CARLA]
"Dr. Mercer? Your three o'clock is here."

[SARAH]
"Send him in."

[NARRATOR]
Thomas Vane entered the way he'd entered the week before: unhurried, economical. Gray sport coat today, charcoal slacks. The same watch. He sat in the patient's chair with the ease of someone who was comfortable in the rooms of other people's houses, crossed one ankle over the opposite knee, and offered her the same warm, measured smile.

She had spent two days preparing for this. She was prepared.

[THOMAS]
"Dr. Mercer. Thank you again."

[SARAH]
"Of course. How has your week been?"

[THOMAS]
"Productive. I finished a project I've been working on for some time. There's a particular satisfaction in completing something—seeing how the finished shape relates to the original intention. Sometimes they match. More often they don't."

[NARRATOR]
He said it mildly, as if discussing weather. Sarah made a note.

[SARAH]
"What happens when they don't match?"

[THOMAS]
"You decide which to honor. The intention or the form. They can't always coexist."

[NARRATOR]
A pause. He looked at her with those pale eyes—not invasively, not warmly, but with the focused attention of a man studying a blueprint.

[SARAH]
"Last week you described someone who's been central to your thoughts. A woman who died. I'd like to continue there, if you're willing."

[THOMAS]
"Willing. Yes."

[NARRATOR]
He settled back slightly, and the quality of his attention shifted—less appraising, more inward, as if the thought of her required a different kind of processing.

[THOMAS]
"She was a student. Graduate level. Something in the humanities—art, I believe. The history of it. She had opinions about things most people think require no opinion. Which chair in a room has the best light. How to read a face in a painting. Whether a building can be lonely."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah kept her pen moving. Art history. Graduate level.

[SARAH]
"She sounds specific."

[THOMAS]
"She was. That was what drew me to her initially. Most people are general. She was specific. She had a way of seeing things I hadn't considered."

[SARAH]
"How did you meet?"

[THOMAS]
"At a bookstore. I was looking for something on spatial theory and she was in the same section, looking for something on Flemish interior painting. We got talking. Or rather, she got talking. I listened."

[NARRATOR]
He smiled at that—a private smile, not for Sarah, turned inward toward the memory.

[SARAH]
"And from the bookstore?"

[THOMAS]
"We had coffee. A few times. She was—entertaining. Alive in the way some people are. Everything interesting to her. She collected things. Old earrings, mostly, from estate sales and thrift shops. She said they carried history with them. That wearing something old was a way of continuing a conversation someone else had started."

[NARRATOR]
The word arrived in Sarah's chest before her mind had processed it.

Earrings.

She felt her left hand move before she could stop it—a small, involuntary gesture upward, toward the pearl drops at her ears. Maya's pearls. From a tin box of Maya's jewelry that Sarah had kept, had eventually begun to wear, had told herself was a way of honoring her sister without becoming sentimental about it.

She stopped the movement with her hand still at her collarbone, fingers brushing the neckline of her blouse. Turned the gesture into the casual adjustment of a collar.

Thomas did not look at her hand.

He was looking at the window. But she had the absolute and entirely unprovable certainty that he had seen.

[SARAH]
"She sounds like she had a distinctive way of looking at the world."

[THOMAS]
"She did. And she was funny, which I hadn't expected. She had a particular kind of humor—dry, self-deprecating, unexpected. She would say things that made you understand the world slightly differently after."

[SARAH]
"And the intrusive thoughts you described—when did they begin in relation to knowing her?"

[THOMAS]
"Immediately. From the first conversation. I recognized something in her. A quality I hadn't encountered before."

[SARAH]
"What quality?"

[NARRATOR]
He paused. Not the hesitation of uncertainty—he had none of that. The pause of someone who had been asked a question they'd long wanted to answer precisely.

[THOMAS]
"Openness. But not naive openness. She was open the way a good room is open—deliberately. She had decided to let things in. I found that very rare."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah wrote: *openness, deliberate. Not naive.* She was writing it down so that her hands were occupied, her eyes had somewhere to be. She was writing it down because it was, in its way, an accurate description of her sister, and because her pulse was a steady hammer in her ears and she needed the pen to anchor her.

[SARAH]
"You mentioned last week that she had a sister. A protective one."

[THOMAS]
"I did."

[SARAH]
"Tell me more about that dynamic."

[NARRATOR]
Something shifted in his expression—barely perceptible, a fractional adjustment, like a camera lens moving to focus.

[THOMAS]
"The sister was older. Capable. She had a career in—some kind of professional service. Helping people, I gathered. She and my—" He paused. "She and the woman I knew had a complicated relationship. The sister didn't approve of certain things. Certain people."

[SARAH]
"Including you?"

[THOMAS]
"We never met. But I was aware of her presence. She had opinions about her sister's choices that weren't invited."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah kept her face still. The radiator clanked once, distantly. Rain tapped at the window.

[SARAH]
"In what ways did that awareness affect you?"

[THOMAS]
"It made me more deliberate. If someone is working against a connection—even indirectly—you become more careful about it. More intentional. You build around the obstacle."

[NARRATOR]
Build around the obstacle. She wrote it. Underlined it once. Looked at what she'd written.

[SARAH]
"Thomas, I want to ask you something and I'd like you to engage with it directly. The relationship you're describing—coffee a few times, a friendship that formed—why did it end? You've described her with considerable—"

[THOMAS]
"She ended it."

[NARRATOR]
The sentence fell into the room like something solid.

[THOMAS]
"She became—uncertain. Nervous. I think she misinterpreted some things. Attributed intentions to me that weren't accurate. She grew frightened of something that wasn't frightening. I found it—" He considered. "Sad. She had made a decision about me that I hadn't consented to. That I couldn't argue against, because she refused to discuss it."

[SARAH]
"And how did you respond to that?"

[THOMAS]
"I gave her time. I thought she would come to understand. I was patient."

[SARAH]
"For how long?"

[THOMAS]
"For as long as it took. Time is not a constraint I experience the way most people do."

[NARRATOR]
He said it with such evenness that it took Sarah a moment to recognize what he had described. Not patience. Something wearing patience like a coat.

She set her pen down with deliberate care.

[SARAH]
"Thomas, when you use the word 'frightened'—you said she grew frightened. What do you think she was frightened of?"

[THOMAS]
"Of what she felt. Toward me. I think she was frightened of the intensity of it. Some people are."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah looked at him. He looked back. The pale eyes, steady. The slight lift at the corner of his mouth that was not quite a smile and was not quite anything else.

She thought: he believes this. Not as a performance, not as a lie constructed for her benefit. He actually believed that Maya—if this was Maya, if she was right, if the Seattle connection and the earrings and the curtains and the moon were not simply grief searching for purchase—had been afraid of her own feelings rather than of him.

The reframe was total. Internal. She had treated patients with similar architectures of belief, though none of them had sat across from her like this: composed, articulate, dressed in a suit that cost more than her car payment, describing the woman they had watched die as someone who'd been afraid of love.

[SARAH]
"We're coming up on time. Is there anything else you wanted to bring today?"

[THOMAS]
"No. I think this was useful. Thank you for the space to speak clearly."

[SARAH]
"Same time next week."

[THOMAS]
"I wouldn't miss it."

[NARRATOR]
After he left, Sarah sat in the silence of her office for six full minutes. She knew the time precisely because she was watching the clock on her bookshelf—the small one angled so patients couldn't see her check it—and counting each minute as it passed.

She thought about the woman he'd described. The graduate student. The collector of earrings. The one who had grown frightened and was now, in his telling, simply a woman who had misunderstood what he was offering.

She thought about Maya calling her. Four times. The last message that was only silence and then *I love you.*

She thought: I have no evidence. I have a feeling and two cities and a description of a woman who collected earrings. I have a session note I crossed out.

She picked up her phone. Dialed the main number for the city's police department. Asked for the cold case unit.

[DISPATCHER]
"Cold case, this is Officer Tran."

[SARAH]
"I'm looking for information about an old case. A homicide. November of—ten years ago. The victim's name was Maya Aldridge."

[NARRATOR]
A pause. The sound of typing.

[DISPATCHER]
"Can I ask your relationship to the deceased?"

[SARAH]
"Her sister."

[NARRATOR]
More typing. A longer pause.

[DISPATCHER]
"That case is active, which means I can't share investigative details with—"

[SARAH]
"Active? Someone is working it?"

[DISPATCHER]
"All cold cases are technically active, ma'am. If you have new information you'd like to provide—"

[SARAH]
"There was a detective. Ten years ago. His name was Cole. Marcus Cole."

[DISPATCHER]
"Detective Cole retired approximately two years ago."

[SARAH]
"Do you have a contact number for him?"

[NARRATOR]
The pause that followed had a different quality. Not bureaucratic. Something like sympathy, or its cousin.

[DISPATCHER]
"I'm not able to provide personal contact information for retired officers. If you'd like to submit new information about the case, I can transfer you to—"

[SARAH]
"Thank you. I'll call back."

[NARRATOR]
She hung up. Sat looking at her phone.

Thomas Vane would be in his car now. Or walking. She didn't know how he traveled, where he went after these sessions, what his apartment looked like or what he did in the evenings. She knew only what he'd offered her in this room, in these careful weekly installments of a grief she no longer believed was grief.

She thought about what it would take to find Marcus Cole. A retired homicide detective who had worked her sister's case. Who had, presumably, interviewed anyone connected to Maya in those first weeks—including, possibly, a young architect living in Capitol Hill.

She opened her desk drawer and took out the index card where she kept numbers she hadn't transferred to her phone. The handwriting went back years. She found what she was looking for: a number her mother had given her, before her mother died, written in the cramped hand of a woman who didn't fully trust digital things.

The number for the private investigator her mother had hired, briefly, after the police stopped calling. The PI had found nothing. Had taken the money and returned apologies.

But at the bottom of the card, in her own handwriting, something she must have added later: *Cole - Homicide - ask about*. No number. Just the name, circled once.

She put the card in her bag. It wasn't evidence. It wasn't a plan. It was simply a name on a card in a drawer, the way certain things stay with you because some part of you knows you will need them eventually.

She turned off the lamp in her office and went to collect her coat.

Outside, the rain had thickened to something between mist and intent.
