[NARRATOR]
She went to the cemetery on a Saturday.

She hadn't been since August—the anniversary of Maya's birthday, which Sarah marked privately, without ceremony, because ceremony would have required acknowledging that she was doing it. The drive took twenty minutes through quiet weekend streets, the city going slow with November, the light already losing color by three in the afternoon.

Evergreen Memorial had the particular stillness of old cemeteries: mature oaks and cedars holding what light remained, gravel paths that wound without urgency, the distant sound of a leaf blower someone was running three sections over. Section 7, Row 14. Sarah knew the way without thinking about it. Muscle memory of grief.

The headstone was simple granite, the lettering clean. Her mother had chosen it. Maya Aldridge. Beloved daughter and sister. The dates—the gap between them small in the way that a wrong number is small, impossible to accept as fact.

Sarah stood. She didn't speak. She never spoke here, though she understood the impulse—understood it clinically, understood why it helped, had encouraged other people toward it in her office. She couldn't do it herself. The distance between speaking aloud and having the words go nowhere felt, in this particular place, unnavigable.

What she did instead was stand, and look, and let herself know what she knew.

She had been here two weeks without admitting it to herself, and the not-admitting had cost her David Chen forty minutes of partial attention. She was now admitting it. That was the transaction.

Thomas Vane had been in Maya's neighborhood at the same time Maya was reporting a man who wouldn't stop watching her. He had described Maya—her field of study, her habit of collecting earrings, her curtains, her moonlight—without naming her. He had described Sarah herself, thinly veiled, as the obstructive older sister. He had been patient, in his word. Patient for as long as it took.

Maya had called four times. Sarah had not answered. At two in the morning on November sixteenth, ten years ago, Maya had died.

The leaf blower cut off. In the silence that followed, a crow called once from somewhere in the oaks.

Sarah said, quietly, to the headstone: "I'm looking at him."

Then she turned around and walked back to her car.

Owen was making soup when she got home—the good kind, the Saturday kind, with stock from scratch and the sourdough she couldn't justify buying every week but he bought anyway because he liked the ritual of bread on the counter. He was listening to something on the speaker above the refrigerator, some folk singer Emma had recently declared was the only person worth listening to and that they would all forget about by March.

He looked up when she came in. He had the gift, which she'd always resented slightly, of reading her the way she read patients.

[OWEN]
"Hey. How was—where did you go?"

[SARAH]
"Cemetery."

[NARRATOR]
He paused the music.

[OWEN]
"You should have told me. I would have come."

[SARAH]
"I know. I wanted to go alone."

[NARRATOR]

[OWEN]

[SARAH]
"Probably."

[OWEN]

[SARAH]

[NARRATOR]
The third session was on a Wednesday. The first frost of the year had come overnight—the cars on her street wearing it like a fine white fur when she left for the office, the air with that particular winter clarity that made everything look sharp and slightly unreal.

Thomas arrived in a coat she hadn't seen before. Charcoal wool, well-made. He removed it precisely and hung it over the arm of his chair. Sat. Crossed his ankle over his knee. The watch caught the light.

[SARAH]
"How have you been this week?"

[THOMAS]
"Reflective. I've been thinking about the nature of understanding. What it actually means to comprehend another person."

[SARAH]
"What brought that up?"

[THOMAS]
"Our sessions, partly. You're a skilled listener. I don't say that as flattery—I've been in rooms with people who listen and I'm aware of the difference. You actually receive what's offered."

[NARRATOR]
She noted: the compliment positioned as observation. Clinical, not personal. And yet personal.

[SARAH]
"What does the difference feel like, from your side of the room?"

[THOMAS]
"The difference between being heard and being processed. Most people process. They're looking for the familiar shape so they can respond. You wait for the shape to emerge."

[NARRATOR]
There was a beat of silence. Sarah let it breathe.

[THOMAS]
"She had that quality too, by the way. The woman I described. She listened the way you do."

[SARAH]
"What did she listen to?"

[THOMAS]
"To me, sometimes. But mostly to everything else. She was the kind of person who caught what others dropped. Stories other people forgot to finish. The feeling behind the sentence." He paused. "She had a sibling who was the opposite. Capable, efficient, but not—porous. The older one. The one who had decided on a self and committed to it entirely."

[SARAH]
"You're describing a contrast."

[THOMAS]
"A useful one. I often thought about the two of them together—how they must have shaped each other. One who opens to experience. One who contains it. Different responses to the same wound, perhaps."

[SARAH]
"What wound?"

[THOMAS]
"I wouldn't presume to know. I observed the result, not the origin."

[NARRATOR]
He looked at her then, and the quality of the look was different—more direct, more open, and because of that more dangerous, because openness from Thomas Vane felt like a room with a wall removed.

[THOMAS]
"You remind me of her. The sister. Not of her—of the contrast, I mean. With your patients, you're fully present. With yourself, I imagine, rather less so."

[NARRATOR]
The room went very quiet.

It was a technique she had used herself, exactly once, on a patient who she was certain would not otherwise stop deflecting—the direct observation, offered as clinical reflection, carrying the structural shape of intimacy. The exposure underneath it: *I see what you're doing. I see you doing it. We both know this now.*

She had used it carefully, with consent, in service of the patient's progress.

Thomas Vane had just used it on her.

[SARAH]
"I appreciate the observation. Shall we return to what you were describing?"

[THOMAS]
"Of course."

[NARRATOR]
He smiled. Not the warm smile from the door. The other one—the slight lift at the corner that committed to nothing.

He knew, Sarah thought. Not suspected. Knew.

And he was pleased.

She kept her face entirely still and made a note that said only: *porous.* And beneath it, in the same careful hand: *he knows I know.*

She did not cross this one out.
