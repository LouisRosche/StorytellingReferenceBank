[NARRATOR]
The seventh session was the second Wednesday of December.

The city had put up its lights—strings of white along the commercial streets, a tree in the lobby of her building that Carla had decorated over a weekend with the focused attention she brought to anything she considered important. Sarah had not turned on Christmas music or baked anything or done any of the things she normally did in the first weeks of December, and Owen had noticed and not said anything, which was its own kind of comment.

She had told him, the night before: "The work situation is complicated right now. I'm handling it."

He had looked at her with his reading-glasses pushed up on his forehead, and his expression had been so carefully neutral that she recognized it as her own expression, borrowed back. A small terrible irony.

"I know you are," he'd said.

She had made a plan. It was not yet a full plan—it had the shape of an intention rather than a strategy—but it was more than she'd had a week ago. She knew what he wanted. She had watched him want it across six sessions, each one a measured installment of something he had been building toward for ten years. He wanted to be understood. He wanted, specifically, to be understood by a woman whose professional function was to understand people, who was also the surviving sister of the woman he had watched die, who represented—in the particular economy of his pathology—the final validation he'd been unable to collect.

He was going to keep coming back until he got it. Or until something stopped him.

She could not go to the police. She had nothing. A feeling, a cluster of coincidences, a man describing a woman who collected earrings. She could not terminate him as a patient—not while he was presenting as functionally stable, not without violating the ethics she had spent twenty years building. She could not tell Owen. She could not tell Ruth, not yet, not without triggering the exact kind of professional intervention that would make Thomas Vane dangerous in a different, more unpredictable way.

What she could do: she could listen. She was very good at listening. And somewhere inside the listening, if she was careful enough, she might find the thing that could be used.

The intercom buzzed at two fifty-eight.

[CARLA]
"Dr. Mercer? Mr. Vane."

[SARAH]
"One moment."

[NARRATOR]
She set down her pen. Took one slow breath. Chose her face.

[SARAH]
"Send him up."

[NARRATOR]
He came in wearing a different tie—dark green, a small concession to the season. He looked, as he always looked, like a man who had organized everything within his reach and found the result satisfactory.

[THOMAS]
"Dr. Mercer. You've put up the tree in the lobby."

[SARAH]
"That's Carla's doing. How are you this week?"

[THOMAS]
"Well. Productive. December suits me."

[SARAH]
"How so?"

[THOMAS]
"The year narrows. Everything becomes about completion. Things that have been in progress come to conclusion. I find that clarifying."

[NARRATOR]
She noted the word: completion. She noted the pleasure with which he said it.

[SARAH]
"Before we begin, I want to let you know something. I've started recording sessions—standard practice when I'm thinking carefully about a clinical approach. For my own training purposes. Is that all right with you?"

[NARRATOR]
She had prepared this carefully. The phrasing, the context, the positioning as routine. She watched his face.

He didn't flinch. He didn't pause. He looked at the small digital recorder she'd set on the arm of her chair, and something passed across his face—something so brief she might have missed it if she hadn't been watching specifically for it.

Not surprise. The wrong shape for surprise.

Pleasure.

[THOMAS]
"Of course. I don't mind at all. Being heard is the point, isn't it? Whatever medium."

[NARRATOR]
She made a note and clicked the recorder on.

The session moved through its paces. He described a project at work—a residential renovation going over budget, a client who changed his mind repeatedly about what he wanted. Thomas described managing this with equanimity. He described equanimity well; she had noticed he always gave it significant time, as though rehearsing for an audience. She listened, reflected, asked the questions that opened rather than closed.

Then, toward the end of the session, he shifted.

[THOMAS]
"I've been thinking about proximity this week. How it changes things."

[SARAH]
"In what sense?"

[THOMAS]
"Physical proximity, but also psychological. When you're close to something—truly close—you begin to understand it from the inside. Most people only understand from the outside. They describe the surface. But if you've been near enough to something, for long enough, you understand the interior logic. How it moves. How it breathes."

[SARAH]
"You're talking about a person."

[THOMAS]
"I'm talking about whatever one is close to. A building. A person. A family."

[NARRATOR]
The last word.

*A family.*

Sarah kept the pen moving, kept her face exactly where she'd placed it.

[THOMAS]
"I've been thinking about her family. The woman I described. Her sister's family specifically. I found myself imagining what that house looked like. What the rhythms of it were. Whether they were—consistent people. The kind with habits."

[SARAH]
"What would knowing that give you?"

[THOMAS]
"Comprehension. I'm not someone who is comfortable with the approximate. If a thing matters to me, I want to understand it completely."

[NARRATOR]
She wrote: *a family. consistent people. habits.* She kept her hand steady.

She had thought she was prepared for this. She was discovering that prepared and ready were different things.

[SARAH]
"We should wrap up there. Same time next week?"

[THOMAS]
"Absolutely."

[NARRATOR]
He rose, gathered his coat, performed the small elegant rituals of departure. At the door, he stopped. Held up the folder he'd brought with him—a manila folder she'd noticed at the start of the session and thought nothing of, because patients sometimes brought folders. He turned and offered it to her.

[THOMAS]
"I made some notes this week. About what we've been discussing. Perhaps you'd like to look them over before next time—I find it useful to know my therapist has read what I've written. It creates continuity."

[NARRATOR]
She took the folder. Their hands did not touch.

He left. She stood at the door and listened to his footsteps—even, unhurried—receding down the hall, the elevator opening and closing.

She went to her desk. Sat down.

The folder was light—one sheet of paper inside, she could feel it. She set it on the desk and looked at it for a moment. She was aware that her hands were entirely steady, which was either a good sign or a warning.

She opened it.

One page, folded once. His handwriting: architectural, precise, each letter formed with deliberate care. Black ink. No margin notes. Dated.

*November 28. She drove home on the usual route. Arrived at the house at 6:47. The kitchen light—the pendant, the warm one—came on first. Then the study. She stayed in the study for forty-one minutes. Her husband arrived at 7:54. The light in the bedroom extinguished at 10:15. She had left the study light on. She often forgets it. I waited until midnight. She didn't come back down.*

Sarah read it once. Read it again.

November 28. That was ten days ago—a Tuesday, she had been home by six-thirty, had started dinner. Owen had been late. She remembered the pendant light in the kitchen—a warm amber, a lamp shade over the bulb—coming on when she couldn't find the overhead switch in her hurry. The study light. She did leave it on. She often forgot.

She had been home, in her house, doing ordinary things. And from somewhere in the dark outside, he had timed it.

The pendant, the warm one. He had named the specific light. He had been close enough, or watching something close enough, to distinguish between the pendant and the overhead. He had counted the minutes in the study. He had noted her husband's arrival time.

He had waited until midnight.

She folded the paper. Placed it back in the folder. Set the folder on her desk. She did all of this very slowly and carefully, the way you move around broken glass.

The recorder was still running. She reached over and stopped it.

She sat in her chair for a long time. The December light outside had given up entirely—dark by four-thirty now, the window showing her nothing but the reflection of her own office, the lamp, herself. A woman in a gray blazer at a desk, looking at a folder.

She thought about what he had done. He had handed it to her. He had stood in her doorway and handed her a piece of paper that described him watching her house and he had asked her to read it before next time. As though it were notes on their sessions. As though it were a normal exchange between a patient and his therapist.

*Perhaps you'd like to look them over. It creates continuity.*

He knew she would know what it meant. He had left it for her to know. This was his version of the thing she had told him she was doing—recording for training purposes. He was also recording. He had been recording for months.

He was telling her: *I see you seeing me. I am not afraid of your seeing. We are both in this room now, openly, and you cannot use what you know without destroying yourself.*

Professional ethics. Evidence vacuum. Gaslighting vulnerability. She had known these would be the constraints, and she had known it academically, the way you know about pain before you feel it. Now she felt it.

She pulled her notepad toward her. The clean page at the top. She wrote three lines:

*Thomas Vane.*
*What you want: to be understood.*
*What I need you to believe: that I understand.*

She looked at what she'd written. It was a beginning.

Not a plan yet. But the thing that comes before a plan—the decision, the alignment of intention with action. She had been a therapist for eight years. She had spent those years teaching people how to be heard, how to open, how to trust the room. She understood the architecture of revelation. She knew how to create conditions under which a person says the thing they have been protecting.

Thomas Vane had come here to be understood. She would understand him. She would understand him precisely and completely and she would give him what he needed—not because he deserved it, but because it was the only weapon she had.

She was a listener. It was what she was.

She picked up the folder. Carried it to the filing cabinet and locked it in the drawer with the other session materials—the ones that were private, client-confidential, to be seen only with a subpoena or her own decision to share them. Let him have the recording he didn't know about. Let him think he was collecting her. She was collecting him.

Outside, the rain had returned—the steady December kind, purposeful, without apology.

She put on her coat, turned off the lamp, and went out into the dark.
