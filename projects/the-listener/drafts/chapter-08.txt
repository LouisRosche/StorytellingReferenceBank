[NARRATOR]
The call came on a Thursday morning, between patients.

Sarah didn't recognize the number. She almost didn't answer—unknown numbers in the middle of a workday were usually insurance verification or someone who had dialed wrong. She answered because she had been answering every call lately with a readiness she didn't entirely understand, braced for something she couldn't name.

[COLE]
"Dr. Mercer. My name is Marcus Cole. I was the lead detective on your sister's case."

[NARRATOR]
She sat very still for a moment.

[SARAH]
"Mr. Cole."

[COLE]
"I know this is out of nowhere. I'd like to meet with you. In person, not over the phone."

[SARAH]
"About the case?"

[COLE]
"About the case."

[NARRATOR]
There was a quality to his voice she recognized from patients who had been carrying something too long—a compression, like words being rationed.

[SARAH]
"I called the department looking for you. About a month ago."

[COLE]
"I know. Officer Tran mentioned it. That's why I'm calling."

[NARRATOR]
She looked at her schedule. Forty minutes between her eleven and one-thirty.

[SARAH]
"Today. Eleven forty-five. Is that possible?"

[COLE]
"Tell me where."

---

[NARRATOR]
Hal's Diner was on the edge of the precinct district—an all-night place with cracked vinyl booths and bitter coffee that had been there long enough to have a layer of permanence about it, like limestone. Cole was already in the back booth when she arrived, a cup of coffee and a manila folder on the table. He was heavyset, close-cropped silver hair, his face carrying the particular wear of someone who had been looking at hard things for thirty years. He rose slightly when she sat down.

[COLE]
"Dr. Mercer."

[SARAH]
"Detective. Or is it—"

[COLE]
"Cole. Just Cole. I've been retired two years." A pause. "I don't feel retired."

[NARRATOR]
The waitress came. Sarah ordered tea, which she could tell was not what this diner was set up for. Cole made a small gesture that she interpreted as approval.

[COLE]
"I'll tell you why I called. Then you can decide what to do with it. You don't owe me anything."

[SARAH]
"All right."

[COLE]
"I worked your sister's case for four years before it went cold. When I retired, the files came with me. Not officially—I copied what I needed. I've been working it privately since then." He looked at her directly. "I believe I know who killed her. I have believed this since three weeks after her death. I've never been able to prove it."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah kept her hands flat on the table.

[SARAH]
"Tell me the name."

[COLE]
"Thomas Vane."

[NARRATOR]
The name sat between them. Cole's eyes didn't leave her face. She understood he was watching for something—had expected either recognition or surprise, was getting neither in the form he'd expected.

[SARAH]
"You know who he is."

[COLE]
"I know he's an architect. Mid-forties, downtown. Clean record, no priors. I know I interviewed him twenty-four hours after your sister died. He was in her address book—not a close contact, but a name. He gave me forty minutes of his time and an account of his movements that I could neither confirm nor break."

[SARAH]
"What made you suspect him?"

[COLE]
"Nothing I could put in a report. The way he talked about her. He called her 'a person I was acquainted with' and then described her in detail no acquaintance had any reason to collect. The color of her front door. The way she wore her hair on weekdays versus weekends. He knew what brand of coffee she bought." Cole wrapped his hands around his mug. "I've interviewed a lot of people over thirty years. There's a kind of attention that's normal—you remember what you see. And then there's the other kind. The kind where you've been seeing things you weren't supposed to see."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah was aware of the specific weight of her own stillness. She had been practicing it for weeks.

[SARAH]
"He's a patient of mine."

[NARRATOR]
Cole looked at her. For a moment his face did something complicated and then went quiet again.

[COLE]
"How long."

[SARAH]
"Two months. He came to me for what he described as intrusive thoughts about a woman who died."

[COLE]
"Christ."

[SARAH]
"He presented clinically. No red flags on the intake. He described her—Maya—without using her name. He has described himself as being in love with her. He has described sustained covert observation as an expression of that love."

[NARRATOR]
Cole set down his mug.

[COLE]
"You've been seeing him weekly."

[SARAH]
"Yes."

[COLE]
"Alone. In your office."

[SARAH]
"It's my practice, Cole. I see all my patients alone."

[COLE]
"Does he know who you are?"

[SARAH]
"He knew before he walked in the door. He has been watching me for at least as long as I've been seeing him. Possibly longer."

[NARRATOR]
She took the journal entry out of her bag. She had brought a photocopy—the original was locked in her office. She set it on the table in front of him.

Cole read it once. Read it again. Set it down.

[COLE]
"He gave you this."

[SARAH]
"Left it with my assistant for me to find. He knows I have it. This was intentional."

[COLE]
"November twenty-eighth. That's your house."

[SARAH]
"Yes."

[COLE]
"The pendant light."

[SARAH]
"Yes."

[NARRATOR]
Cole was quiet for a moment. The diner carried on around them—the griddle, the bell on the door, someone's radio from the kitchen playing something with too much bass. The normalcy of it felt theatrical, like weather in a movie.

[COLE]
"You know what he's doing."

[SARAH]
"He's telling me he can reach my family. He's also telling me he knows I know—and that there's nothing I can do about it without evidence, because this could be dismissed as a creative writing exercise."

[COLE]
"Right. I've been down that road." He tapped the photocopy. "This isn't admissible. Even if it were, any defense attorney would reframe it in twenty minutes. Poetic journal, not evidence of surveillance. No identifying information, no explicit threat."

[SARAH]
"I know."

[COLE]
"So why are you still seeing him?"

[NARRATOR]
She considered the question. It had been sitting in her chest since November, turning from anxiety to something more deliberate.

[SARAH]
"Because I'm the only one who has access to him. And because he came here for something. He wants to be understood—specifically by me. His endgame is a kind of validation I've been studying how to withhold long enough to draw out a confession."

[NARRATOR]
Cole looked at her for a long moment.

[COLE]
"You're going to manipulate your patient into confessing."

[SARAH]
"I'm going to use the tools of my profession in service of justice for my sister. Yes."

[COLE]
"The confession won't be admissible either. Anything obtained under therapeutic privilege—"

[SARAH]
"Not if it's recorded and not if the recording is provided to law enforcement under the exception for credible evidence of a crime."

[NARRATOR]
Cole picked up his coffee. Studied her over the rim.

[COLE]
"You've been thinking about this."

[SARAH]
"For weeks."

[COLE]
"What do you need from me?"

[SARAH]
"Information. You interviewed him. You know something about how he operates—his tells, his patterns, the things that broke through his composure even briefly."

[COLE]
"One thing. When I asked about Maya's last hours, he got a specific expression. Not grief. Not guilt. Satisfaction. Like someone describing a piece of work they were proud of." He set down the cup. "I've never forgotten it. I've been seeing it in my sleep for ten years."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah thought about the session where Thomas had described "sustained attention." The small, private smile when he said *that's rather the point.*

[SARAH]
"Did you ever find anything physical? Any connection to her apartment?"

[COLE]
"No. He was careful. Forensics found nothing useful—the scene had been staged to read as a break-in. Whoever did it understood procedure. Knew what to avoid."

[SARAH]
"He's an architect. He understands spatial analysis."

[COLE]
"That's what I thought. But understanding spatial analysis doesn't make you a forensics expert. He had help, or he'd studied it, or—" Cole stopped. "Or he'd done it before."

[NARRATOR]
The diner noise filled the silence. Sarah looked at the photocopy of the journal entry, sitting between them on the Formica table.

[SARAH]
"He told me in session that Maya wasn't the first person he'd paid this kind of attention to. He described a coworker, earlier. He presented it as a pattern he'd had since he was young."

[COLE]
"I looked for others. Never found a connection I could prove."

[SARAH]
"But you believed there were others."

[COLE]
"I believe men like this don't start in their thirties. I believe the one we know about is in the middle of a story, not at the beginning."

[NARRATOR]
She thought about that. About the possibility—the near-certainty, looking at it now—that Maya was not a beginning but a culmination, that somewhere in the years before her there were other women who had grown nervous and reported cars in parking lots and been told by trained professionals that they were experiencing anxiety-driven hypervigilance.

She thought about herself. In her own office. Telling her sister the same thing.

[SARAH]
"There's something else. He'll be watching your movements now if he isn't already. He knows I called the precinct."

[COLE]
"I know. I've been careful."

[SARAH]
"How careful?"

[COLE]
"Careful enough for a retired detective who has nothing to lose if Vane knows he's suspicious. Different kind of careful than you need to be." He looked at her directly. "He won't hurt you in the way you're afraid of. That's not his pattern. His pattern is—intimate. He needs the connection. Killing you would end it."

[SARAH]
"He killed Maya."

[COLE]
"Maya tried to expose him. She'd gone to the police. She was moving apartments. She was threatening the connection he'd built." A pause. "You haven't threatened the connection. You've deepened it. As long as that's true, you're more valuable to him as—"

[SARAH]
"A listener."

[COLE]
"Right."

[NARRATOR]
She gathered the photocopy and put it back in her bag. The tea she'd ordered had gone cold. She hadn't touched it.

[SARAH]
"Don't contact me at the office. Text from this number if you need me—I have a code word. If I text you *weather,* it means I need to meet within twenty-four hours."

[COLE]
"Where did you learn tradecraft?"

[SARAH]
"I didn't. I'm making it up as I go."

[NARRATOR]
He smiled at that—a brief, tired smile, the kind that had more memory in it than humor.

[COLE]
"Welcome to how it feels to work this case."

[NARRATOR]
She left him with the cold coffee and the thin folder and the decade of accumulated nowhere. Out on the sidewalk, the December air was sharp and the lunch crowd was beginning to fill the street.

She had an hour before her next patient.

She walked back to her office thinking about what Cole had said: *As long as you're more valuable to him as a listener.* The logic of it. The horrible precision of it. She was safe not because he had any feeling for her well-being, but because her destruction would end a story he was writing.

She had known this. She was more afraid now that someone else had said it plainly.
