[NARRATOR]
She had spent a week preparing for the eighth session.

Not in the way she usually prepared—the review of notes, the orientation toward whatever the patient had been working through. This was different. She had been reading. Not clinical texts: the kind of thing she had read in graduate school, the psychology of interrogation, of rapport-building with subjects who were not cooperative, of the specific seams in a narcissistic architecture where a lever could be placed. She had been building a technique from first principles.

The technique was this: Thomas Vane wanted to be understood. To be validated. To have his version of the events of ten years ago received by someone capable of receiving complexity. That was his stated want. What she needed was for that want to drive him forward, past the careful presentation, into the actual account.

To do it, she would have to give him something. Not honesty—she had no honesty to spare for him. But the shape of honesty. The vocabulary of a therapist who had come, reluctantly, to see his perspective.

It was the most repugnant thing she had ever planned to do. She had scheduled extra time with Ruth the following week and was already composing the version of it she would never be able to tell.

Thomas arrived at three o'clock in a blue tie. She noted the tie because she had been noting everything now, cataloguing every variable in the way she imagined he catalogued the lights in her house.

[THOMAS]
"Dr. Mercer. You look well."

[SARAH]
"Thank you. How was your week?"

[THOMAS]
"Productive. I've been thinking about what you said."

[SARAH]
"About?"

[THOMAS]
"About the nature of understanding. Whether it's something people can choose to do, or whether it's a capacity they're born with. A dispensation."

[NARRATOR]
She hadn't said that. He was reframing a session she had no memory of in those terms. She noted it: he was already putting words in her mouth, which meant he was already positioning her as someone who thought about understanding the way he did.

[SARAH]
"What did you conclude?"

[THOMAS]
"That most people choose not to understand. It's more comfortable to categorize. Understanding requires sitting with complexity—admitting that the same action can be both harmful and necessary. Most people aren't willing to make that admission."

[NARRATOR]
She had the recorder running. She had been running it since the seventh session—ostentatiously, the small device visible on the chair arm. If anything, his presence seemed to please Thomas. He performed for it with slightly more polish than without.

[SARAH]
"You've used the word necessary before, in different contexts. When you describe the decisions you made in relation to the woman you've been speaking about."

[THOMAS]
"Have I."

[SARAH]
"You described staying present in her life—watching her—as something you felt was necessary. I've been thinking about that word."

[NARRATOR]
She had practiced this opening. The key was not to lead—to let him hear himself in what she offered back, and then step toward the opening himself.

[THOMAS]
"And?"

[SARAH]
"I find myself interested in what the necessity felt like from the inside. Most of my patients describe their compulsive behaviors as against their will—ego-dystonic. What you describe sounds different. As if the watching was something you chose."

[THOMAS]
"It was a choice. Absolutely. I wasn't being driven by something I couldn't control. I was making a decision about where my attention belonged."

[SARAH]
"That's an important distinction."

[THOMAS]
"It is. The thing I find most frustrating about how—situations like mine are understood—is the assumption of pathology. That someone who chooses to love intensely must be disordered. As if intensity itself were the problem."

[NARRATOR]
She thought: *You watched a woman die and called it love.* She said:

[SARAH]
"And you don't experience yourself as disordered."

[THOMAS]
"I experience myself as misunderstood. Which is different."

[SARAH]
"What would it mean to you to be understood?"

[NARRATOR]
He was quiet for a moment. Not the savoring pause—something more considered, as if the question had caught him somewhere genuine.

[THOMAS]
"It would mean someone acknowledging that what I felt was real. Not pathological. Not a sickness. A form of love that doesn't conform to the approved categories, but is love nonetheless."

[SARAH]
"Do you believe that acknowledgment is possible?"

[THOMAS]
"I'm sitting in this room, aren't I."

[NARRATOR]
She held that. Let it sit in the air between them without responding, which was one of the hardest things she had ever been required to do professionally. Because what he was doing—whether he knew she knew or not—was telling her that he believed she was capable of giving him that acknowledgment. That this was what she was here for.

She thought about the journal entry. About Maya's voice on the voicemail. About the pendant kitchen light.

[SARAH]
"Tell me about the night she died."

[NARRATOR]
She had not planned to say it yet. It came out of the careful listening she'd been doing, the sense that the room had opened slightly, that a direct question now might go further than a gradual one later.

Thomas went still. The quality of the stillness was different—not his normal measured pause, but something that held at the edges, like ice that wasn't quite sure of its own thickness.

[THOMAS]
"I thought—"

[SARAH]
"You've been circling it for two months. So have I. I think we both know that's where this has been going."

[NARRATOR]
Another stillness. Longer.

Then something in his face shifted—so faint she might have missed it if she hadn't been watching exactly for it. Not relief. Closer to recognition. The expression of someone who has been waiting a long time for a particular door to open.

[THOMAS]
"She was afraid. That's the first thing."

[SARAH]
"Of what?"

[THOMAS]
"Of me. Which I had never—I had never intended that. I had never wanted that. She had come to misinterpret the nature of my feelings, and by the end, she was afraid of what she had decided I was."

[SARAH]
"What had she decided you were?"

[THOMAS]
"Someone dangerous. When I was simply—someone who loved her."

[NARRATOR]
He said it plainly. Not as justification, not as performance—or if it was performance, it was the kind that had been practiced until it felt like truth. Sarah thought about the weight of that: ten years of rehearsal. The story he had told himself until it became the only story.

[SARAH]
"And that night—"

[THOMAS]
"She had gone to the police. You knew that. She had told them someone was in her apartment, that she felt watched. They couldn't do anything, of course—there was nothing to find. But she had made it formal. Official."

[SARAH]
"And you knew this."

[THOMAS]
"I knew everything about her situation. That was the nature of my attention."

[NARRATOR]
He said it without emphasis. A statement of fact.

[SARAH]
"How did that make you feel? Her going to the police?"

[THOMAS]
"Sad. And—yes. Afraid, in my own way. That something would be misunderstood. That I would be misunderstood. That the relationship I'd built would be destroyed by a misreading."

[SARAH]
"What did you do with that fear?"

[NARRATOR]
He looked at her. The pale eyes, steady.

[THOMAS]
"I went to see her."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah kept breathing. Kept her face exactly where it was. The recorder ran its quiet thread.

[THOMAS]
"I don't want to go further than that today. I think—" He paused. "I think I'd like to sit with this for a moment. The fact of having said it."

[SARAH]
"Of course."

[NARRATOR]
They sat in the December silence. The radiator. The rain. Outside, the world going about its business.

He had said it. *I went to see her.* Not a confession—not yet. A door opened a crack. She could see the light on the other side.

She thought: *I will need more sessions. More patience. More of whatever it cost to sit in this room and give him the shape of understanding.*

She had done harder things. She was not sure she had done anything more terrible.

[SARAH]
"Same time next week."

[THOMAS]
"I'll be here."

[NARRATOR]
After he left, she sat for a long time without writing anything. The recorder light blinked once and went dark.

She had done it. She had given him just enough of what he needed to take a step forward.

She felt like something had been taken from her in exchange. She didn't know yet if it was worth it. She didn't know yet if it would matter.

She pulled her notepad toward her and wrote one line, because she needed to write it somewhere:

*He went to see her.*
