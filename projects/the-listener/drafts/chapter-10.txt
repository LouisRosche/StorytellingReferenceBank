[NARRATOR]
She had made a tactical error.

She had been keeping the Thomas research—the open-source material, the printed news articles, the architectural firm profile, the hand-written timeline she'd constructed from the facts she could name—in a folder in her home office. Not the session notes, which lived locked at the practice. The other things. The private archaeology.

She had kept it at home because the office felt contaminated, because there was a version of herself that had to be Dr. Mercer in those rooms, and this work required being someone rawer and more dangerous. She had kept it in the locked drawer of the desk in the room they called the home office and mostly used for taxes.

Owen had been looking for the insurance documents.

[OWEN]
"Sarah."

[NARRATOR]
She was in the kitchen when he said it. The tone stopped her—not angry, not panicked. Something more careful than either.

He was in the doorway of the home office, the folder in his hand. The timeline was on top, in her handwriting, a column of dates and locations running down the left margin.

[SARAH]
"I can explain that."

[OWEN]
"I haven't asked yet."

[NARRATOR]
He came into the kitchen and set the folder on the table with deliberate care, the way you set down something that might break or might go off. He sat. His reading glasses were pushed up on his forehead. He looked tired in a way she recognized from the early years of their relationship—a tiredness that was actually worry carrying the wrong name.

[OWEN]
"You've been researching a patient."

[SARAH]
"It's complicated."

[OWEN]
"Sarah. I'm a teacher. I've graded essays for nineteen years. I know research when I see it. That folder has forty pages in it."

[NARRATOR]
She sat across from him. There were dishes in the rack—she'd done them that morning without thinking, the mindless ritual of maintenance. Through the window, the December sky was the color of old pewter.

[SARAH]
"There's a patient. He has a history that intersects with—with Maya's case. I believe he may have been involved."

[NARRATOR]
Owen looked at her for a long moment.

[OWEN]
"Involved how."

[SARAH]
"Directly."

[NARRATOR]
Another pause. She watched him take it in—the recalibration, the way he always processed hard things: methodically, without rushing, giving the facts room before responding to them.

[OWEN]
"You think he killed her."

[SARAH]
"Yes."

[OWEN]
"And you've been seeing him weekly. In your office. Alone."

[SARAH]
"I know how it sounds."

[OWEN]
"I'm not sure you do. Because from here it sounds like my wife has been spending two months alone in a room with the man she believes murdered her sister, and hasn't told me."

[NARRATOR]
The sentence was quiet. He didn't raise his voice—Owen rarely raised his voice—but there was something in the quiet that was harder than volume.

[SARAH]
"I didn't want you to worry."

[OWEN]
"That's not a reason. That's an explanation that happens to be insulting."

[SARAH]
"Owen—"

[OWEN]
"You didn't want me to worry. So instead you've been handling this alone, for two months, while telling me it was 'work complications.'"

[NARRATOR]
She heard it the way he meant it—the layered failure, not just the secrecy but what the secrecy said about what she thought of him. She had, in protecting him from anxiety, also made the decision that he couldn't be trusted with the truth. That was a different thing. He knew it was a different thing.

[SARAH]
"I've been working with the original detective on the case. There's a plan. I'm not—I'm not reckless, Owen. I understand the risks."

[OWEN]
"What risks?"

[SARAH]
"He's been watching the house. He's watching Emma."

[NARRATOR]
Owen was still for a moment in a way that was different from his usual stillness.

[OWEN]
"He's watching Emma."

[SARAH]
"I have no evidence he means her harm. He's using proximity to signal—"

[OWEN]
"I don't care what he's using it to signal. There is a man who you believe killed someone, who has been watching our fifteen-year-old daughter, and you have been handling this by yourself for two months."

[SARAH]
"I didn't want to frighten her."

[OWEN]
"That's the second time you've used that construction. I didn't want to worry you. I didn't want to frighten her. You make every decision for the people you love based on what you want them not to feel."

[NARRATOR]
The kitchen was very quiet. Sarah heard the refrigerator hum. She heard a car on the street outside doing something to its brakes.

[OWEN]
"Tell me everything."

[NARRATOR]
She did.

Not the session content—that was protected, would remain protected regardless of what happened between her and Owen in this kitchen—but the rest. The first session, the details that had accumulated, the journal entry and what it described. The phone call from Cole. Maya's voicemails. The decisions she'd made and why.

Owen listened the way he always listened: completely, without interrupting, with the particular quality of attention that had been one of the first things she'd loved about him and that she had spent years subtly running away from.

When she finished, there was a long silence.

[OWEN]
"You need to go to the police."

[SARAH]
"There's nothing actionable. I've been through this with Cole. The journal entry isn't evidence. The session material is privileged. He hasn't made an explicit threat."

[OWEN]
"Then get a lawyer. Get a restraining order."

[SARAH]
"Based on what? A patient described watching a house. He'll say it's a journaling exercise. He'll say I'm harassing him. He's already thought all of this through—he's been thinking about it for longer than I have."

[OWEN]
"Then stop seeing him."

[SARAH]
"If I terminate him without cause—"

[OWEN]
"Your safety is cause."

[SARAH]
"My safety is not at issue. Cole believes—"

[OWEN]
"I don't care what Cole believes. Cole is a retired detective with nothing to lose. You are my wife. You are Emma's mother." He stopped. Pressed his fingers against his forehead. "Sarah. I need you to hear me. I am not telling you what to do. I am asking you—I'm telling you—that I cannot support this. That this is not a decision you get to make alone, because it affects all of us."

[NARRATOR]
She looked at him. The man who had been patient for years, who had waited while she held him at the precise distance she could manage, who had loved her in the way she'd let herself be loved and had not complained about the distance even when she knew he felt it.

He was right. She knew he was right. She also knew she was not going to stop.

[SARAH]
"I understand what you're asking."

[OWEN]
"That's not an answer."

[SARAH]
"I know."

[NARRATOR]
He looked at her for a long time. She watched him arrive at something—not acceptance, not agreement. A decision to remain in the room with her while she was somewhere he couldn't reach.

[OWEN]
"I want Emma to go to her cousin's for winter break. Earlier than we planned."

[SARAH]
"Owen—"

[OWEN]
"That's not a discussion. That's what I need."

[NARRATOR]
She thought about Emma. About the school play, which was still three weeks away. About Thomas in the audience she hadn't imagined yet.

[SARAH]
"After the play."

[OWEN]
"Christmas Eve. No later."

[SARAH]
"After the play. I promise."

[NARRATOR]
He nodded. Rose. Collected the folder from the table and handed it to her—not dismissively, but with a deliberateness that said: *this is yours and it stays yours and that is the problem.*

He went upstairs. She heard him in the bathroom, then the bedroom, then nothing.

She sat with the folder in her hands and the kitchen light overhead—not the pendant, the overhead, too bright—and thought about what it cost to be the kind of person who couldn't be helped.

She thought about Maya calling four times. About saying *never mind.*

She thought: I am doing the same thing to him that I did to her. And I don't know how to stop.

She turned off the overhead light and went upstairs in the dark.
