The ninth session was on a Wednesday in the third week of December, the day after Sarah had stood in the school parking lot watching Emma's rehearsal run long and thinking about Thomas in the audience when opening night came. The city had committed to its holiday performance—lights, fir trees in the plaza, the general theater of goodwill that she found, in her current state, both sustaining and unbearable.

Thomas arrived at three. She had been watching the building entrance on the street camera feed she'd added to her phone since October—a system the building had always had, which she'd never thought to access before. He walked from the direction of the parking garage. Alone, as always. She turned off the camera app before he reached the lobby.

It was thin—a hundred and twenty pages at most, a slim volume on complicated grief published in the nineties. The kind of thing that circulated in clinical circles, a little dated now, its insights absorbed into the mainstream and its specific text mostly forgotten. She'd read it in graduate school.

The cover was slightly water-damaged at the corner. The spine had been cracked and re-creased several times—someone had read it and returned to it. Used books carried their readers in them. She had always believed that.

She opened the front cover.

In pencil, in the upper right corner: a name, written in the particular looping script she had not seen in ten years. And beside it, small and instinctive and unmistakably Maya's: a doodle of a cloud with a face in it. The kind of thing Maya did in margins, on receipts, on the backs of envelopes. A signature more reliable than a signature.

The book was Maya's.

Sarah's right hand was holding the cover open. She watched it, with some detachment, maintain its steadiness. Her pulse was loud in her ears. She read the name—the one she had not needed to see because she had known the moment the book was in her hand, the moment she felt the particular texture of Maya's presence in an object the way you feel it in a room someone has just left.

She closed the cover.

[SARAH]

[THOMAS]
"I thought it might be. Grief work—the clinical side of it—seemed like something you'd have an interest in, given your specialization."

He knew she'd recognized it.

She thought: *I have to become that person. I have to give him enough of it that he gives me everything. And then I have to live with having done that.*

When the session ended and he was gone, she opened the book again.

Maya's name. Maya's cloud.

She had kept all of Maya's books. They were in boxes in the basement because she hadn't been able to throw them away and hadn't been able to look at them. This one had been missing and she had told herself she must have lent it to someone before Maya died, or that Maya had left it somewhere.

It had been here. With him. For ten years.

She put it in her bag. She would not give it back.
