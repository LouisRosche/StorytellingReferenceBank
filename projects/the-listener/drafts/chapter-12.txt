Sarah was watching the door.

She did it the way she'd learned to do everything lately: continuously, with the portion of her attention she'd carved out for surveillance. The rest of her was present, responding to Owen, tracking the room. She was getting better at the split.

She scanned. Three rows back, center section—a tall figure in a dark coat, seated alone. Program in hand, reading it.

Thomas Vane.

He had not looked at her. He was looking at the program with the focused attention of an audience member who was genuinely interested in what he was about to see. His posture was relaxed, appropriate, indistinguishable from the fifty other adults seated around him without a child in the production—a teacher, a family friend, an alumnus.

Sarah looked at Owen's profile. He was still reading the bios.

She made a decision.

[NARRATOR]
She moved toward the end of the row before he could respond—slowly, because haste would cause a scene, which Thomas would notice and which was exactly the wrong response. She reached the aisle. Walked to the back of the auditorium, where two parents were doing last-minute coat negotiations, and stood near the wall where she could see the full room.

Thomas had not turned around.

He sat reading his program, one leg crossed over the other, entirely composed, waiting for the play to begin.

He knew she had seen him. He had been waiting for her to see him. That was why he was here—not for Emma specifically, not to threaten in any actionable sense, but to sit in the row behind her daughter and let that fact exist.

She pulled out her phone. Typed to Cole: *weather.*

The lights dropped. The play began.

Sarah stood at the back of the auditorium and watched Emma walk onto the stage in a period costume and deliver her first lines with the unselfconscious confidence of a person who hadn't yet learned to be afraid of being seen.

She did not sit down again until Thomas left at intermission.

She did not tell Emma. She did not tell Owen that evening—she told him only that she'd had a headache, that the side section had been giving her trouble.

He accepted this with the careful neutrality he'd been practicing since the kitchen conversation.

That night, lying in the dark listening to Owen's even breathing, she ran through it. Thomas at the school. The deliberate placement. The program in hand, the relaxed posture, the performance of normalcy. He was not escalating in the traditional sense—no direct contact, no explicit threat. He was showing her radius. *I can be anywhere. I can sit three rows behind your daughter and read a program and there is nothing you can do.*

Cole's words: *As long as you're more valuable to him as a listener.*

She believed this. She still believed this. But the school play had moved something in her chest—a cold specific terror that her calculations hadn't fully accounted for, the difference between knowing your child is in danger abstractly and watching a predator sit three rows behind her.

She thought about Emma's face on the stage. The unselfconscious confidence. The way she moved in the costume as if she owned it.

She thought: I will not let him have any part of this. Not her, not this.

She got up at four in the morning and went to the home office and sat in the dark with the folder and thought about what it would take.

Not just to get the confession. To end it.

She thought about what Cole had said: *Solve it permanently.*

She thought about what it would mean to be the kind of person who did that. Whether she was that person. Whether, given enough time and enough provocation, anyone was.

She sat in the dark until it began to get light. Then she went downstairs and made tea and when Owen came down she told him: Emma was going to her cousin's on the twenty-third. Before the play's closing night. Before Christmas.
