[NARRATOR]
Cole had a system.

He had been running it for two years—a set of informal arrangements with three people who still worked the precinct, two who didn't, and one who worked at a cellular provider's regional office and had access to billing records in ways that were technically outside her authorized scope. The system was built on debt, loyalty, and the professional code of silence that operated below the written ethics of every institution Sarah had ever encountered.

He explained this to her in the back booth at Hal's Diner, the second week of January, while a fresh storm beat against the windows and the Christmas lights on the street outside had not yet been taken down.

[COLE]
"I can pull his call records for the last six months. Cross-reference the tower data with your address, your office, Emma's school. It won't tell us what he said. It'll tell us where he was."

[SARAH]
"That's illegal."

[COLE]
"It's inadmissible. It's not the same thing."

[SARAH]
"In this context it is. Anything we build from illegally obtained data is—"

[COLE]
"I'm not building a case. I'm building a picture. A picture for me." He wrapped his hands around his mug. "Look. I understand the ethical position. I spent thirty years in the ethical position. The ethical position is why Thomas Vane has been walking around for ten years while your sister's case file sits in a box in a storage unit."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah looked at him. He had the face of someone who had been correct too many times about things that couldn't be proven.

[SARAH]
"If there's a trial—when there's a trial—anything connected to this could contaminate—"

[COLE]
"The tower data won't go anywhere near a courtroom. It's for me. To understand his movements. To know, when you tell me he was at Emma's school, whether the school was a one-time thing or whether he's been there before."

[NARRATOR]
The silence that followed was the sound of her weighing something she didn't want to weigh.

[SARAH]
"Has he been there before?"

[COLE]
"I don't know yet."

[SARAH]
"When you have it. Then you'll know."

[COLE]
"Yes."

[NARRATOR]
She looked out at the storm. Across the diner, a man was reading a newspaper with the focused attention of someone who had been reading newspapers his entire life. Normal. Everything normal. The radiator clanking. Someone's coffee refilled. December becoming January with no ceremony.

[SARAH]
"Do it. But I don't want to know the details of how you get it. And nothing obtained through that method gets used in a way I can be connected to."

[COLE]
"Understood."

[SARAH]
"I need to tell you something else."

[NARRATOR]
She had been deciding for two days whether to tell him about the gym. She had decided, in the end, that withholding it was the worse error.

[SARAH]
"He joined my gym. I noticed him on Thursday—he was on the machine adjacent to the one I was using. He didn't approach me. He didn't speak. He was simply there, using the equipment, as if he'd been a member for years."

[COLE]
"Has he been?"

[SARAH]
"I asked the front desk afterward. He joined in November. Same week as the first session."

[NARRATOR]
Cole was quiet for a moment.

[COLE]
"He's mapping your life."

[SARAH]
"I know."

[COLE]
"The gym, the school, the neighborhood. He's filling in everything. He wants to know your entire geography."

[SARAH]
"I know. I've thought about this." She held her coffee cup without drinking it. "He's not escalating toward violence. He's escalating toward—completeness. He wants to know everything about me the way he knew everything about Maya. He's building the same structure."

[COLE]
"And when it's complete?"

[SARAH]
"I don't know. That's what I need to understand before I can end this."

[NARRATOR]
Cole looked at her with something that was not quite admiration and not quite concern. A calculation she'd seen in him before—the weighing of someone whose usefulness in a situation was tied to their willingness to accept risk.

[COLE]
"I asked you this before. I'm going to ask again. Is it possible to simply—terminate him as a patient? Make it procedurally clean, offer a referral, close the file."

[SARAH]
"He'd file a complaint. He's already thought about this. He has the licensing board as a lever—one complaint and I'm under investigation while the hearing is scheduled. I can't terminate a patient during an active complaint without it looking like retaliation."

[COLE]
"Has he threatened to file?"

[SARAH]
"No. He doesn't need to. I know he knows he can. That's sufficient."

[NARRATOR]
Cole set down his mug. Looked at the table.

[COLE]
"I have something."

[NARRATOR]
He opened the folder. Inside, a printout—a photograph, slightly blurry, timestamp in the corner. A parking lot she recognized as the one adjacent to her office building. A car, silver, parked in the same spot across two separate dates three weeks apart. The license plate visible in one of the shots.

[COLE]
"Building security camera. Your landlord's system. The footage is his property—I have a contact who pulled it with his permission. This is legal. The car is registered to Thomas Vane."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah studied the photograph. The same car, the same spot. The timestamps showed he had been there on two Fridays—not the days of their sessions. Days when she was in the office but he had no appointment.

[SARAH]
"He's been watching the building."

[COLE]
"On days he has no reason to be there. Yes."

[SARAH]
"This is something."

[COLE]
"It's the beginning of something. On its own it's not enough. But it's a start."

[NARRATOR]
She took the photograph. Added it to the folder she'd been keeping.

The storm outside had intensified. Through the diner windows, the street was white and moving, the world narrowed to the radius of the light.

[SARAH]
"I need to ask you something I've been thinking about."

[COLE]
"Ask."

[SARAH]
"The night Maya died. The official ruling was home invasion. What did the scene actually look like?"

[NARRATOR]
Cole was quiet for a long moment. He looked at the table, then at her.

[COLE]
"You want to know."

[SARAH]
"I've been avoiding it for ten years. I need to know now."

[NARRATOR]
He told her. Not everything—there were things he abbreviated, she could tell, both because he knew she was Maya's sister and because some details served no purpose except as damage. But the core of it. The scene as he'd found it. The staging that had suggested break-in but didn't quite hold up under scrutiny. The absence of things that should have been present. The presence of one thing that shouldn't have been: a single window, at the back of the apartment, that had been opened from the outside.

He was patient about the opening. He had come and gone through that window. He had waited somewhere—possibly inside, possibly in the building, possibly outside—while Maya called her sister four times.

Sarah listened. She did not look away.

When he was done, she said:

[SARAH]
"Thank you."

[COLE]
"I'm sorry I'm telling you this."

[SARAH]
"Don't be. I needed it."

[NARRATOR]
She gathered the folder and put on her coat.

Outside, the storm was still going—steady, purposeful, covering everything that had been laid down before it. She walked back to her car thinking about the window at the back of Maya's apartment. The one that had been opened from outside. The one that meant he had been in the building, or near it, during the time she had been ignoring the phone.

She had told herself, for ten years, that she hadn't known. That she couldn't have known. That the difference between her not answering and Maya dying was circumstance, not causation.

She was no longer sure that was true. She was no longer sure of the clean line between negligence and something worse.

She drove home in the storm. She parked in the garage and sat in the car for a few minutes before going in.

Then she went inside and made dinner, because dinner had to be made.
