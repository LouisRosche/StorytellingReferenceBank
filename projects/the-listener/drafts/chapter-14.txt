[NARRATOR]
The sessions were changing in quality.

In the first two months, they had the shape of therapy—the normal architecture of a clinical relationship, even one Sarah was running under false premises. She had been the observer, he the patient. Now the walls of that structure had thinned. Thomas had been moving steadily toward something and she had been moving with him, and the room felt less like a therapy office and more like a negotiating table where both parties understood that polite language was a courtesy, not a constraint.

He had begun, in the January sessions, to ask her questions.

Not the questions he'd asked before—the reasonable, boundary-testing ones about her experience of loss. These were different. More specific. He would ask about her morning routine as if curious about the professional discipline required; she would recognize the question as a request for the gap in the schedule she'd described when they first began meeting. He would ask about her "support system" in the context of discussing therapeutic self-care; she would understand this as an inventory of who, besides Owen, knew what she was dealing with.

She gave him fragments. Nothing actionable. Enough to seem forthcoming.

It was the most exhausting thing she had ever done.

[SARAH]
"Let's return to what you described last week. You said you went to see her. What happened when you arrived?"

[THOMAS]
"Her lights were on. She wasn't expecting anyone."

[SARAH]
"And you went in."

[THOMAS]

[SARAH]

[THOMAS]
"Yes. She had come around, briefly. Toward the middle of things. She allowed more closeness than she later felt comfortable with. I think she frightened herself."

[SARAH]

[THOMAS]
"I needed to speak with her. She had been avoiding me. She had filed a complaint with the police that was based on a misunderstanding. I wanted the opportunity to correct it."

[NARRATOR]
She wrote: *back entrance. code from earlier. needed to correct misunderstanding.*

She thought: he had let himself into her building because he had a code she'd given him when she still trusted him, months before, when she'd thought he was simply someone who cared about her. And Maya had come home that night to find the feeling of her apartment wrong. *Things were not moved, exactly, but the feeling of it was different.*

Because he had been there. Inside her home. Waiting.

"What happened when you found her?"

[THOMAS]
"She was—frightened. As I said. She asked me to leave. I wanted to talk."

[SARAH]
"And?"

[NARRATOR]

[THOMAS]

[NARRATOR]
He said it with an almost clinical detachment—as if he were describing a chemical reaction, something that had proceeded from its own logic without his participation. *The situation had gone somewhere.*

Sarah thought: he is describing Maya's murder as an accident of emphasis. A loss of control he normally manages carefully. He was not describing regret—she could hear the absence of it in the flatness of his voice. He was describing an outcome he had not planned, which in his internal economy was a sufficient distinction from wrongdoing.

[SARAH]

[THOMAS]
"No."

She wrote it down. The recorder was running. She wrote it down anyway, because she needed her hands to be doing something other than shaking.

*Not guilt. Loss.*

She thought about Maya's body, found by a neighbor at nine in the morning. About the window at the back of the apartment, opened from the outside. About the four phone calls and the silence and the *never mind, go to sleep, I love you.*

She thought: he was there. He was in the apartment when she called me. He heard those calls. And she understood, with a clarity that settled into her like cold water, that Maya had not called her because she was afraid of him. She had called her because she was trying to get help while he was there—and at some point in those two hours, he had taken the phone.

*She tried. There was a brief struggle.*

The last voicemail. The long silence. The breathing that wasn't Maya's.

[SARAH]
"We should stop there for today."

[THOMAS]
"Are you all right?"

[SARAH]
"It's a great deal to hold. I want to give it proper attention rather than rushing through."

[NARRATOR]
He nodded. Accepted this. She walked him to the door and watched him go and then came back and sat in her chair and stared at the recorder.

*I lost control of something I normally manage carefully.*

She had almost everything now. Not a formal confession—he had been careful about naming anything directly. But a narrative: the building code, the attempt to talk, the struggle, the outcome. Combined with the parking lot photographs, the journal entry, the book with Maya's name—

She called Cole when she got home. Told him about the session. Not the content—she would not share that—but that progress had been made, that there was movement, that the shape of the endgame was coming clear.

He listened. Said: "How much longer do you need?"

She said: "One more. Maybe two."

He said: "Be careful. He's getting close to the bottom of whatever he came here for."

She poured wine afterward. Owen was in the living room with a book and looked up when she came in and looked at her face and looked back down at his book, which was the kindest thing he could have done.

She drank the wine standing at the kitchen counter, looking at the window above the sink where her own reflection stared back at her against the dark outside. The woman in the reflection looked tired in the way of people who have been carrying something for too long.

She thought: *one more. Maybe two.*

She thought: *and then I have to become the person he needs me to be. Completely. For long enough.*

She thought: *and then I have to come back from that.*

She wasn't sure, standing at the counter, whether that last part was true.
