[NARRATOR]
Ruth's office was the mirror of Sarah's in layout and the opposite of it in atmosphere—more plants, warmer light, a rug that had been there since the practice opened and had absorbed twenty years of difficult conversations into its weave. Ruth was sixty-one and had been a therapist for thirty-five of those years and had the particular quality of someone who had heard almost everything and been surprised by very little, which was both her great professional gift and the thing that made her hardest to deceive.

She had asked for the meeting. That was the first thing Sarah noted when she got Ruth's message. Not a standard check-in. A request.

[RUTH]
"Your clinical notes for the past three months."

[SARAH]
"What about them?"

[RUTH]
"They're thin. Your standard documentation has always been thorough—more thorough than required, actually. Something has shifted in one thread of your caseload. Possibly more than one."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah sat with her hands in her lap.

[RUTH]
"I'm not going to ask you to tell me which patient. I'm not going to ask you for privileged information. What I'm going to ask is whether you're okay."

[SARAH]
"I'm managing."

[RUTH]
"That's not what I asked. I've seen you manage. You manage excellently, and when you're managing excellently your notes are thorough. The thinness of the notes suggests you're managing something that is taking everything you have and leaving nothing for the documentation."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah looked at the plant in the corner. A philodendron that had been there as long as the rug. A survivor.

[SARAH]
"There's a patient whose case is—ethically complex."

[RUTH]
"Complex how."

[SARAH]
"He has information about a personal matter of mine. Information that affects my objectivity. I have been attempting to maintain clinical neutrality while also—navigating a parallel investigation."

[NARRATOR]

[RUTH]
"You're running two things simultaneously in the session room. The clinical relationship and something else."

[SARAH]
"Yes."

[RUTH]

[SARAH]
"Yes."

[RUTH]
"Are you safe?"

[SARAH]
"I believe so."

[SARAH]
"Indirectly. Not in session. The risk is—structural rather than immediate."

Ruth looked at her for a long time. Sarah met her eyes, which required more effort than it should have.

[RUTH]
"I've seen this before. The therapist who becomes the only person with access to something important. The therapist who convinces themselves that the therapeutic relationship is the vehicle for something else entirely. I am not judging that position—I understand why it happens. I'm going to tell you what I tell everyone I've seen in it."

[SARAH]
"All right."

[RUTH]

[SARAH]

[RUTH]

[SARAH]
"Yes."

She left Ruth's office and walked down the hall to her own. She was reaching for the handle when she noticed the door was not fully closed.

She always closed it. She had closed it that morning—she could recall the specific click of the latch because she had been careful about it since November.

She stood in the hallway for a moment. The building was quiet; it was between hours, the after-lunch lull before the two o'clock sessions began. Carla had stepped out for lunch. The other two offices on the hall were dark.

She pushed the door open slowly with her fingertips.

Nothing visible. The office as she'd left it. Chair, desk, the small table with the water carafe. Her diplomas on the wall. The bookshelf with the clinical texts.

She stepped in. The air was different—not wrong exactly, but disturbed in the way of a room that has been recently occupied. A subtle rearrangement of molecules. The same feeling Maya had described.

She went to the filing cabinet. Locked, as she'd left it, the small padlock she'd added in November intact. She checked the desk drawers. Intact. She checked the side table where she kept the recorder between sessions.

The recorder was there. But it had been moved. Slightly—an inch, maybe less. The angle was different from how she'd left it.

She stood very still, looking at it.

He had been in the room. He had found the recorder, examined it, replaced it. He had not taken it—which meant either he'd decided it didn't matter, or he'd decided the contents were less dangerous to him than she'd thought, or—

Or he wanted her to know he'd found it. The way the journal entry had been a message about the kitchen window. The way the play had been a message about Emma. The recorder put back slightly wrong was a message: *I know what you're doing. I have always known what you're doing.*

She sat down at her desk. Put her hands flat on the surface.

She picked up her phone and texted Cole: *weather.*

Then she sat and waited for Carla to come back and unlock the building entrance for the two o'clock.

She thought about what Ruth had said: *covers cannot be maintained indefinitely.*

She thought: I know. I know they can't. But I only need it to hold one more session.
