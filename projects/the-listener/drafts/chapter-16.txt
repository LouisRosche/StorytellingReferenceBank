[NARRATOR]
The session was scheduled for a Wednesday. February now, the city finally committing to its worst weather—ice on the pavement since Tuesday, the kind that looked like nothing and was everything. She had canceled her morning appointments because two patients had called to say they couldn't make it out safely, and she had stood at the window of her office at eleven, watching the street, and thought: good. The quiet helped.

Thomas had not canceled. He never canceled.

She had prepared. She had talked to Cole the day before in a coffee shop three blocks from her office, the photographs and notes spread on the table between them. Cole had the phone tower data back—Thomas's phone had been within range of Emma's school on three separate occasions before the night of the play. He had been there, watching, before Sarah had known to look.

Cole had also found a reference in a database of civil complaints—a harassment filing from eight years ago, a woman in the city's east side, withdrawn after three weeks. No criminal proceedings. Insufficient to act on, but a shape. The shape Cole had described months ago: *I believe men like this don't start in their thirties.*

The session was at three. At two-fifty she adjusted the recorder—more visibly than usual, set it at a different angle from the position Thomas had moved it to. A small acknowledgment. She had been thinking about what to do in this session for a week. She had a plan and she had a fallback plan and she had the cold awareness that he was also prepared, that whatever happened in this room would happen because he had chosen to let it happen.

Carla sent him in at three-oh-two.

He came in, removed his coat, sat with his customary unhurried precision. He looked at the recorder. Looked at her. Said nothing about either.

[THOMAS]
"You've been thinking."

[SARAH]
"I'm always thinking."

[THOMAS]
"More than usual. Since last week."

[SARAH]
"We covered difficult material last week."

[THOMAS]
"We did."

[NARRATOR]
She waited. There was a quality to the room she hadn't felt in the earlier sessions—something that had been slowly pressurizing for months was near the edge of its containment. She could feel it. She thought he could too.

He sat with his hands on the arms of the chair and looked at her with the steady pale attention she had been studying for four months.

[THOMAS]
"I think we've been pretending for long enough."

[NARRATOR]
She kept her face exactly still.

[SARAH]
"What do you mean?"

[THOMAS]
"I mean that the structure we've been working inside—the clinical structure—has been useful. It gave us a frame. A set of rules. Both of us knew the rules and both of us have been operating within them and around them simultaneously." He paused. "But I think you know who I am. I think you've known for some time. And I know you know."

[SARAH]
"What makes you say that?"

[THOMAS]
"The way you listen changed. In the first few sessions you listened the way a therapist listens—for pattern, for affect, for what the words pointed toward below the surface. Around the fifth or sixth session it changed. You began listening for something specific. For a particular—shape. The way a detective listens, not a therapist."

[NARRATOR]
The room was very quiet.

[SARAH]
"You've been thinking about this for a while."

[THOMAS]
"Since October, approximately. Since the second or third session where I noticed the change. I've been watching you watch me. It's been—illuminating." The faint almost-smile. "I imagine it has been for you as well."

[SARAH]
"Thomas—"

[THOMAS]
"I'm not angry. I want to be clear about that. I've been many things in this room, but anger hasn't been one of them. What I've been, mostly, is—interested. In what you would do. In whether you would break first or whether I would."

[SARAH]
"And?"

[THOMAS]
"You didn't break. I didn't either. We've been at parity for four months, which means we're at an impasse."

[NARRATOR]
He rose, with the unhurried precision she had catalogued and recorded and studied. He adjusted his cuffs. Picked up his coat from the arm of the chair.

[THOMAS]
"I think the pretending has been exhausting for both of us. I think we can do better. Somewhere that isn't this room, with its rules and its recorder and its pretense of treatment."

[NARRATOR]
He said *recorder* with a careful emphasis that confirmed: he knew it was running. Had known for some time. Had said everything he'd said in the last sessions with that knowledge in mind.

Which meant either he'd calculated that the recordings were not dangerous to him—that what he'd said fell short of a legal threshold—or he had something else in mind entirely.

[SARAH]
"I'm not going to meet you outside this room."

[THOMAS]
"No. I don't suppose you are." He moved toward the door. "But you might reconsider, given time."

[NARRATOR]
He stopped at the door. Reached into his inside coat pocket and removed an envelope.

[THOMAS]
"I'll leave this with Carla. Good afternoon, Dr. Mercer."

[NARRATOR]
He was gone before she could speak. She heard his voice in the outer office—brief, pleasant—and then the main door, and then nothing.

She stood. Walked to the window. Watched him cross the icy street below, unhurried, hands in his coat pockets. He did not look up.

Carla knocked.

[CARLA]
"Dr. Mercer? Mr. Vane left an envelope for you."

[SARAH]
"Bring it in, please."

[NARRATOR]
It was a small envelope, cream-colored, no writing on the outside. She opened it.

A single card, written by hand in a script that was precise without being showy.

She read it.

She read it again.

*Your sister called you four times that night. You didn't answer. I know because I was there. I have wondered, in the years since, whether she called you because she saw me arrive, or whether she simply called because she was afraid. I believe it was both. She did not leave a message after the first call. She understood, I think, that whatever she would have said would reach you too late. I have thought about that understanding on her part often. It shows considerable clarity, given the circumstances. You did not answer. I have never blamed you for that. I only mention it because I think it is something you have been carrying, and I thought you should know that someone else has been carrying it alongside you, for all the years since.*

*Yours,*
*T.*

[NARRATOR]
Sarah read it a third time.

She was aware of the recorder on the table. She was aware of Carla outside the door, going about the small administrative rituals of the end of the workday. She was aware of the ice on the street and the pale February light failing at the windows.

She thought: *he was there when she called me. He was in the apartment when she called me. He was there for all four calls and then she stopped calling and then he was gone and then she was dead and I was asleep.*

She thought: *he has been using this for ten years. This knowledge. He has shaped it into the most precise thing he could build—not a threat, not a confession, something worse than either. A shared secret. A complicity he is trying to press into my hands.*

She thought: *he wants me to feel that we are alike. That my failure to answer is in the same moral territory as what he did afterward.*

She thought: *it isn't. It isn't. Whatever I did or didn't do, it isn't.*

She sat very still with the card in her hands and told herself that, and almost believed it.

Then she picked up her phone and called Cole.

One sentence: "He knows I know."

Cole said: "I'll be there in an hour. Don't go anywhere."

[NARRATOR]
She sat in her office while the February light went out of the windows and the street below emptied and the heating system ticked and groaned and the city went about its ordinary business all around her.

The card was on the desk in front of her.

*I have never blamed you for that.*

She understood what he was doing. She understood it clinically, strategically, personally. She understood the architecture of it—the offer of absolution from someone with no right to offer it, the attempted exchange: his forgiveness for her participation in his version of events.

She understood all of this. It didn't make the card easier to look at.

She turned it face down. Left it there.

She picked up the recorder and removed the memory card and put it in her pocket. She labeled a new card and placed it in the recorder for next time, though she did not yet know if there would be a next time, or what form it would take, or whether whatever came next would happen in this room or somewhere else.

He had said: *we can do better.*

She thought: yes. We can.

But not in the way you mean.
