[NARRATOR]
She spread everything on the kitchen table on Sunday evening, while Owen was at a faculty event and the house was quiet in the specific way of houses that have recently held a teenager and now do not.

Emma had been gone eleven days. She called on Wednesdays and Sundays, and Sarah had been careful on those calls—present and warm and giving nothing of the house's current texture.

The table:

The journal entry, photocopied. The parking lot photographs—two cars, two Fridays, the same silver vehicle each time. The building security stills. Cole's printed summary of the phone tower data, annotated in his careful handwriting: four dates, four locations, the first predating the first session by six weeks. The envelope from the practice mail, Thomas's letter inside. The note Carla had handed her after the midpoint session, its brief paragraphs dense with careful implication.

The session recordings—twelve of them on memory cards in a small case, labeled by date. She had not listened to them since making them; she had reviewed her notes instead. She could not listen to them here. She could not hear his voice in the kitchen.

She had, finally, also printed the complaint that had arrived by certified mail three days earlier.

The licensing board document was eleven pages. It was precise in the way of documents drafted with legal assistance—the language calibrated, the claims structured to be difficult to disprove without triggering further disclosure. Thomas, identified as "the complainant," alleged that Dr. Sarah Mercer had:

Used the therapeutic relationship to conduct a personal investigation into a matter involving the complainant and a deceased third party.

Engaged in deliberate and sustained deception of the complainant regarding the nature of her therapeutic intent.

Recorded sessions without maintaining appropriate clinical documentation, suggesting an extraclinical purpose for the recordings.

Failed to maintain appropriate therapeutic boundaries, including by involving law enforcement contacts in a matter related to the complainant.

Each allegation was carefully constructed to be true enough to require defense while not quite actionable enough for immediate sanctions. He was not claiming abuse of a vulnerable patient—that would require evidence he didn't have. He was claiming professional misconduct of the kind that required investigation.

The board would investigate. She would be required to respond. During the period of inquiry, her license remained valid but her ability to conduct certain types of work was, effectively, under review.

She had spoken to a licensing attorney that morning, a woman named Chen who practiced exclusively in professional defense. Chen had reviewed the complaint and said: *This is well-written. Someone helped him with this.* When Sarah said she believed the complainant had prepared for this, Chen had said: *Then you need to be equally prepared.* She had a preliminary hearing scheduled for March.

She looked at the table. All of it.

She thought about what the DA's contact would say on Tuesday. She had been through this herself, privately, and she knew the answer before she heard it: not enough. The parking lot photographs placed his car near her office on days he had no appointment. Suggestive, not criminal. The phone tower data, obtained through Cole's informal arrangement, was not only inadmissible but potentially evidence of its own misconduct. The building security footage was the strongest piece and it placed a car, not a person, and the car had not been engaged in any illegal activity. The sessions—the partial disclosures, the admission that he'd "gone to see her," the description of a "brief struggle," the clinical detachment about the aftermath—were therapeutically privileged and even if that privilege were broken, Thomas's language had been careful enough to support multiple interpretations.

She had four months of evidence and none of it was enough.

The note was the closest thing she had to a direct admission, and the note could be reframed—had already been reframed in the licensing complaint—as the correspondence of a man processing grief over a woman who had died, not a man confessing to her murder.

*I was there. I have never blamed you for that.*

A defense attorney would read it as a love letter from a man who had been present when a woman died accidentally, who had been blamed by implication ever since, who was now attempting to find peace with the survivor who'd been avoiding him.

She thought about this. Sat with it.

Then she pulled a legal pad from the drawer and wrote at the top: *What would be enough.*

Below that:

A physical connection between Thomas and Maya's apartment. DNA, fiber, print—something the forensics team had missed or hadn't looked for. After ten years unlikely, the scene long since altered.

A witness. Someone who saw him at the building that night or leaving it. Also unlikely—it had been night, a rear entrance, and Cole had spent years looking.

A prior victim. The civil complaint that had been withdrawn—if that woman would speak. Possible. Uncertain. She had withdrawn the complaint for reasons Cole didn't know.

A direct confession, on record, in a context that wasn't therapeutically privileged.

She drew a circle around that last one.

A dinner. A public place of her choosing. A wire, if Cole could obtain equipment. And Thomas, who wanted to finish a conversation he'd spent ten years preparing for, who believed she was moving toward the acknowledgment he'd been seeking, who would be more forthcoming in an informal setting than he had been in a clinical one—because the clinical setting had its own kind of constraint, its own managed disclosure, and he had been managing very carefully.

Over dinner, he might not manage as carefully.

Over dinner, she might get the thing that pushed the case across the threshold.

She wrote: *What would it cost.*

She sat looking at the question.

She crossed it out. It was the wrong question. She knew what it would cost. The question was whether the cost was less than the alternative, which was Thomas Vane walking around for another ten years while the complaint proceeded through the licensing board and the evidence sat in a folder and Cole got older and the prior victim stayed silent and Maya's case remained cold.

She gathered the papers. Put them back in the folder. Locked it in the drawer.

She made herself a plate of something and ate it standing at the counter, because she didn't want to eat alone at the table she'd been using for evidence.

She thought: Tuesday. And then a decision.

She thought about Maya. Maya had also arrived at a decision—the police, the move, the growing-louder understanding that the man at the edges of her life was dangerous. Maya had made every correct decision and it had not been enough because Thomas was already inside the apartment when she arrived home that night.

Sarah thought: that is the difference. He was inside her apartment. He will not be inside mine.

She turned off the kitchen light.

She had until Tuesday.
