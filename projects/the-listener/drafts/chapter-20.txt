He left the next morning. She stood on the front steps in her coat and watched the car back out of the driveway and felt, in the specific way of someone who has made all their own choices, entirely alone.

The house went quiet. Not the quiet of a house that was empty—she had lived here alone for periods before Owen, before Emma, and she knew the quality of that quiet. This was different. This was the quiet of a house that was supposed to have people in it.

She went inside and made coffee and sat at the kitchen table with the legal pad, and the complaint, and the folder, and the weight of the decisions she'd been making for four months without anyone to help her carry them.

She thought about Maya. Maya had also been alone at the end—the apartment, the back entrance, the phone in her hand. Maya had tried to call.

Sarah thought: I will not be alone in the same way. I have Cole. I have the DA contact. I have the attorney.

But she was alone in the house, which was a different thing.

She drank her coffee. Went through the legal pad again.

Tuesday was in two days.

She had a complaint to respond to, a plan to finish, and a decision to make that she had been making slowly for four months without admitting that she was making it.

She wrote nothing new. She read what she'd written the night before—*What would be enough*—and looked at the circle she'd drawn.

She thought: I already know what I'm going to do.

She had known since the night of the play, watching Thomas sit three rows behind Emma's unsuspecting, brilliant, unguarded daughter.

She just hadn't admitted it to herself yet.
