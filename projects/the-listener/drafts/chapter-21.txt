[NARRATOR]
She went into the basement on Monday evening because she was looking for a legal document from three years ago and the drawer she thought it was in turned out to be wrong, and while she was moving boxes she found Maya's.

She hadn't moved them since Owen had carried them down here four years ago, after they'd bought the house—the boxes from the storage unit she'd been paying monthly for two years after Maya died because she couldn't make herself deal with what was in them. Owen had not asked what was in the boxes. He had moved them down here when she asked, labeled them in his handwriting—*Maya's books, Maya's papers, Maya's kitchen/miscellaneous*—and left her to decide what to do with them in her own time.

She had never decided.

She sat on her heels in front of the book box. The tape she'd used to seal it in that first terrible month was still intact, yellowed at the edges, her own handwriting on the label. She had written it in a period she barely remembered—the weeks immediately after, when she'd moved through tasks with a mechanical efficiency that she later understood was her mind's self-protective arrangement.

She opened the box.

The books were packed tightly, spines up, the way she'd found them on Maya's shelves. Maya had organized by color rather than subject or author—a system that had driven Sarah mildly insane when she'd seen it in the apartment and that she now recognized as exactly Maya. Idiosyncratic. Visually precise. Organized around beauty rather than utility.

She went through them slowly, lifting them out and setting them in stacks on the basement floor. Reference books. Novels. A cluster of books about grief that were dated—Maya had always researched whatever she was going through, turning toward literature the way some people turned toward experts.

She was three-quarters of the way through the box when she found the gap.

Not immediately visible—the books were packed tightly enough that the gap was only apparent when you reached a section where the spines should have been close and they weren't, where there was a looseness, a compression that implied an absence. She spread the books on either side gently.

One book was missing. The gap between a blue spine and a green one was exactly the width of a slim paperback.

She sat on the cold concrete floor and looked at the gap.

He had been in Maya's apartment. She had known this since Cole told her about the back entrance, about the building code. She had known he had been there that night, and before that night, and possibly many times before that night during the period when Maya had felt things were *not moved, exactly, but the feeling was different.*

He had taken a book from Maya's shelf. A specific one: the grief text that Sarah had read in graduate school, that Maya had acquired at some point and kept on her color-organized shelves. He had taken it and kept it for ten years and then brought it to Sarah's office in a paper bag with the explanation that he'd found it at a used bookshop.

*I thought it might interest you professionally.*

She thought about the session. About holding the book and recognizing Maya's writing in the inside cover. About keeping her face still.

He had held this book for ten years. Had known it was Maya's—had taken it from her apartment, either before her death or in the hours after, because he had been in the apartment and the book had been on the shelf.

A souvenir. The word came to her with a flat clarity. He had taken something of hers to keep.

She thought about what else he might have taken. About what was in this box and what was not.

She sat on the floor for a long time. The basement was cold—the house's heating didn't reach down here well. She could hear the furnace cycling on and off. Above her, the house was quiet, and outside, the February night pressed against the small basement windows.

She thought about Maya putting the book on the shelf. The color-coded system—she would have placed it by its spine color, not its subject, and she probably hadn't re-read it after buying it, and so it had sat there while Thomas came and went from her life, and one day it had been taken, and Maya had either not noticed or had noticed and added it to the list of things that were *not moved, exactly.*

Sarah put the books back in the box. Replaced the tape as best she could.

She went upstairs. Poured a glass of water. Stood at the kitchen sink looking out the dark window at the backyard.

She thought: he had access to her apartment for months. Possibly longer. Long enough to know the book was there, to decide he wanted it, to take it on one of his visits.

She thought: what else did he take. What else does he have.

And then, arriving more slowly: *what else does he know that he hasn't told me.*

He had said, in the letter: *I have a great deal to tell you. I think some of what I have to tell you would be clarifying.*

Clarifying. About Maya's life, her last months, the period of the surveillance that Sarah was only now beginning to understand the extent of.

He had been there. For much of it. He had watched and taken and remembered.

And he was offering to tell her.

She stood at the sink until the water glass was empty.

Tuesday. The DA contact. And then.

She turned off the kitchen light and went to bed in the quiet house, lying in the dark listening to the February wind against the windows and thinking about what she was willing to do in exchange for the truth.
