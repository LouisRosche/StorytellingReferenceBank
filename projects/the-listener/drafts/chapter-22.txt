[NARRATOR]
She had been going to the same coffee shop on Tuesday mornings since she started the practice nine years ago. It was three blocks from her office, small, with good natural light and a table by the window where she could work between eight and nine before patients started. She knew the staff by name. She knew the regulars by face.

She didn't know how long Thomas had been coming on Tuesdays. She hadn't asked when he sat down across from her—she had kept her face still and her voice even and treated it the way she'd been treating everything, which was as information to be processed and used.

He sat with coffee and the composed unhurried attention she had come to know across twelve sessions, and he said:

[THOMAS]
"I wasn't sure you'd stay."

[SARAH]
"I considered leaving."

[THOMAS]
"I know. You looked at the door."

[NARRATOR]
She had not been aware that she'd looked at the door. She noted this.

[SARAH]
"This is outside our arrangement."

[THOMAS]
"Yes. I know you haven't formally responded to the letter. I thought perhaps a direct encounter would be easier."

[SARAH]
"You thought appearing in a location you know I frequent on a schedule you've been tracking would be easier."

[THOMAS]
"I thought the alternatives were harder." A pause. "I'm not going to hurt you. I want to say that plainly, because I imagine it's the primary concern."

[SARAH]
"It's a concern."

[THOMAS]
"I know. It shouldn't be, but I understand why it is." He held his coffee. "Can we simply—talk. Not as patient and therapist. Not as anything but two people who knew the same woman."

[NARRATOR]
She thought about the DA meeting. Yesterday, Cole's contact—a woman named Farris, compact and direct, who had been doing exactly what Cole said for fifteen years—had looked at the complete picture and said what Sarah had expected her to say.

*It's not enough.*

She had elaborated. The parking lot photographs: suspicious, not actionable. The building footage: circumstantial. The phone tower data: inadmissible and potentially a liability for Cole. The session recordings: therapeutically privileged in their current form, and even if that privilege were broken, the language was careful enough to require interpretation. The note: the single most suggestive piece, but defensible as something other than a confession by any competent attorney.

Farris had looked at her across the conference room table and said: *I understand what you believe happened. I believe you're right. That's not sufficient.*

And then: *If he were willing to tell you directly what he did—without the clinical framing, in his own words, in a context where privilege doesn't apply—that would be different.*

Dinner. A wire. A public place of her choosing.

[SARAH]
"Tell me about the last months. Before she died."

[NARRATOR]
Thomas looked at her. Something shifted in his expression—not surprise, but a re-calibration. He had expected her to be more resistant. He was adjusting.

[THOMAS]
"What would you like to know."

[SARAH]
"She told people she felt watched. That things were—not quite right in her apartment. What was that period like, from your perspective."

[THOMAS]
"Difficult. I could see that she was frightened, and the cause of her fright was my attention, which made the situation—recursive. The more frightened she became, the more I needed to be close to her. The closer I was, the more frightened she became."

[SARAH]
"You understood that you were the cause of her fear."

[THOMAS]
"Yes. I always understood that. I simply—could not resolve the tension. Withdrawing felt like losing her. Staying felt like frightening her further. I was in an impossible position."

[SARAH]
"She went to the police."

[THOMAS]
"Yes. In September, about two months before she died. They were—as police often are in these situations—unable to act on what she described. There was nothing illegal, from a technical standpoint. Someone paying attention is not a crime."

[SARAH]
"She reported she'd seen you in her building. Outside her door."

[THOMAS]
"She reported she'd felt a presence in her building. She didn't identify me by name in that report—I know because I checked."

[NARRATOR]
He said it without any visible sense of what that disclosure revealed—the matter-of-fact acknowledgment that he had known the contents of a police report filed about him.

[SARAH]
"You had access to the report."

[THOMAS]
"I had access to someone who had access. I wanted to understand my situation."

[THOMAS]

Sarah kept her face still. Her hands in her lap, loose, unhurried.

She thought: *that is as close as he will ever come. That is what the dinner is for. She pushed him to a threshold he doesn't usually cross.*

She thought: *I need him to cross it.*

[THOMAS]

[SARAH]

[THOMAS]
"I'm glad."

[NARRATOR]
He said it simply. Not with triumph. She thought about what Cole had told her—the satisfaction in Thomas's expression when asked about Maya's last hours, the pride of craftsmanship. What she saw now was not that. What she saw was something closer to the relief she'd caught once or twice in the sessions—the expression of a man who had been carrying something alone for a very long time and was being offered, finally, a table for two.

She thought: this is the most dangerous thing I've ever done.

She said: "I'll send you a time and place."

She gathered her laptop and her bag and left before he did, walking back to the office through the February cold thinking about what a decision made in a part of yourself you can't fully account for actually meant. Whether it was the same as a choice. Whether the distinction made any difference to Maya.

She thought: it doesn't. It doesn't make any difference.

But for the purposes of what came next, she needed him to say it without the softening. Without the passive construction. Plainly.

She thought: one dinner. That's all I need.
