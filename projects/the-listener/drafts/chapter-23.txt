[NARRATOR]
She went back to the DA's office on Thursday, without Cole.

Farris met her in the same conference room. Farris was the kind of person who was exactly what she appeared to be—direct, prepared, comfortable with silence. She set a notepad on the table and looked at Sarah and said:

[FARRIS]
"You've made contact with him outside the clinical setting."

[SARAH]
"Yes. He came to a coffee shop I frequent. We spoke for approximately forty minutes."

[FARRIS]
"What did he say."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah had written the coffee shop conversation down in full, immediately after, while she was still walking back to her office. She pulled the notes from her bag.

Farris read them. Read them again.

[FARRIS]
"He said a decision was made. His language."

[SARAH]
"Yes."

[FARRIS]
"That's as close as you've gotten."

[SARAH]
"Yes. I believe he's willing to go further. In a more controlled setting—a dinner, private, where the conversation can continue without the coffee shop constraints."

[FARRIS]
"He proposed the dinner."

[SARAH]
"In a letter to my practice, yes. I've accepted, verbally, and I'm arranging a time and location."

[NARRATOR]
Farris set down the notes.

[FARRIS]
"Walk me through what you're envisioning."

[SARAH]
"A restaurant I choose. Public. I arrive first, secure a table. A concealed recording device—Cole has access to equipment. The dinner lasts as long as it takes for him to be specific enough about the night to constitute actionable disclosure."

[FARRIS]
"Actionable how. What are you looking for."

[SARAH]
"An unambiguous statement that he was physically responsible for Maya's death. Not the passive construction—not *the situation went somewhere.* A first-person acknowledgment."

[FARRIS]
"And if he makes that acknowledgment, what do you do with the recording."

[SARAH]
"Bring it here."

[NARRATOR]
Farris was quiet for a moment. Looked at the table.

[FARRIS]
"I have to tell you something, and I need you to hear it completely before you respond."

[SARAH]
"All right."

[FARRIS]
"A recording made by a private individual in a one-party consent state, of a conversation between two adults who both chose to be present, is generally admissible as evidence. That's the good news. The complications are these."

She counted on her fingers.

[FARRIS]
"One: you have an existing professional relationship with this man, and an active licensing board complaint against you arising from that relationship. Any recording you make of him will be scrutinized for the context in which it was obtained. His defense will argue the recording was elicited by someone with a documented personal vendetta, operating under the guise of a therapeutic relationship she had already been accused of misusing."

[SARAH]
"The relationship is formally terminated. The session ended when he left the office and I haven't seen him as a patient since."

[FARRIS]
"The board complaint is still active. That's the context."

[SARAH]
"Continue."

[FARRIS]
"Two: anything he says over dinner will be interpreted in light of who he says it to. He's speaking to Maya Mercer's sister, who he knows believes he killed her. Whatever he says, his attorney will argue that it was calculated to perform a particular role for a particular audience—that he was playing a character for you, not confessing. Without corroboration from a second source, a statement made to you specifically is inherently suspect."

[SARAH]
"He'll say that about anything he says to anyone. That's the nature of who he is."

[FARRIS]
"Which is why corroboration matters. Is there anything that could physically place him at the apartment that night? After ten years, forensics is almost certainly not available. But is there anything else?"

[NARRATOR]
Sarah thought about the book. About the gap on the shelf. About what he'd said in the coffee shop: *she offered it.* If she could prove he'd taken the book from the apartment—if she could establish provenance, demonstrate that the book was Maya's, show that he had it and had brought it to her as a deliberate act—

But proving provenance on a book required something she didn't have. Maya's name in the cover wasn't enough. He'd said she'd lent it to him.

She thought about the prior victim. The civil complaint, withdrawn.

[SARAH]
"There was a prior harassment complaint. Eight years ago, withdrawn after three weeks. A woman."

[FARRIS]
"Cole mentioned this."

[SARAH]
"If she would speak—if his pattern of behavior could be established through a prior victim—"

[FARRIS]
"Prior bad act evidence is admissible in certain circumstances. It doesn't prove he killed Maya, but it establishes pattern. Combined with a clear recording and your testimony, it changes the evidentiary picture significantly."

[SARAH]
"Has Cole found her?"

[FARRIS]
"That's not my department. But I'd suggest it's worth pursuing in parallel with the dinner."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah wrote on her notepad. The woman. Cole finding the woman. The dinner. The recording. Two parallel tracks.

[FARRIS]
"I want to say one more thing, off the record."

[SARAH]
"Say it."

[FARRIS]
"You are the sister of the victim. You have been running a private investigation for months while maintaining a professional relationship with the suspect. You have done this with intelligence and discipline, and you have brought us further than a decade of official work. I respect what you've done." A pause. "I also need you to understand that if this dinner goes wrong—if he recognizes what you're doing, if he acts in a way that could not have been predicted—I cannot protect you. There's no cavalry available. Whatever happens, happens first."

[SARAH]
"I know."

[FARRIS]
"Cole will be nearby."

[SARAH]
"Yes."

[FARRIS]
"That's not nothing. But it's also not enough if things move quickly."

[SARAH]
"I know that too."

[NARRATOR]
Farris looked at her for a moment. The direct, prepared, comfortable-with-silence attention of a woman who had spent fifteen years looking at the aftermath of things that had moved quickly.

[FARRIS]
"Why are you doing this? Not the investigation. Specifically the dinner. You could stop now. You have enough for the board complaint to proceed—the complaint against you is weak and your attorney will handle it. You could simply—exit. Leave the investigation to law enforcement and move on."

[SARAH]
"He killed my sister."

[FARRIS]
"Yes. That's why you started. Why are you still going?"

[NARRATOR]
Sarah thought about the coffee shop. About Thomas saying *a decision was made* in the tone of someone describing weather. About the gap in the box in the basement. About the voicemail with the breathing that wasn't Maya's.

[SARAH]
"Because he wants me to stop. He filed the complaint to make stopping seem like the rational choice. He's been operating for ten years on the assumption that no one will pay close enough attention long enough to matter. I need to be the thing that he didn't account for."

[NARRATOR]
Farris looked at her for a long moment.

[FARRIS]
"Set the dinner for somewhere well-lit with multiple exits. Tell me the location and time twenty-four hours in advance."

[SARAH]
"I will."

[FARRIS]
"And find the woman."

[SARAH]
"I'll tell Cole today."

[NARRATOR]
She walked back to her car through the mid-February cold. Two tracks. The dinner. The prior victim.

She thought about Thomas's patience—the decade of it, the building of a plan he'd been executing with the quiet precision of someone who had been thinking about a specific problem for years. She had been inside that plan, running her own alongside it, for four months.

She thought: this is almost over. One way or another, this is almost over.

She thought about what almost meant, when the thing at stake was the only version of justice Maya was ever going to get.

She got in the car. Called Cole.

[SARAH]
"The prior victim. I need you to find her."

[COLE]
"I've been looking. I might have her."

[SARAH]
"When can we meet?"

[COLE]
"Tomorrow."

[SARAH]
"I'll come to you."

[NARRATOR]
She set the phone down and looked at the parking garage wall in front of her.

Two tracks. Running in parallel. She needed both.

She needed to be the thing Thomas Vane hadn't accounted for.

She started the car.
