[NARRATOR]
She went to Maya's boxes again in the morning.

Not the book box. The one labeled *Maya's papers*—letters, documents, the physical accumulation of a life that had ended at thirty-one. She had opened this box once, four years ago, and put the lid back on after ten minutes and gone upstairs and not returned.

She sat on the cold concrete floor and went through it now.

There was a calendar. The year of Maya's death, a paper desk calendar with handwriting in the squares—appointments, reminders, the small administrative notations of a person managing a full life. She flipped to November.

The square for November twenty-eighth was blank except for one notation, in the evening: *S — ?*

S was Sarah. Maya had been considering calling her. The question mark beside the initial suggested uncertainty—whether to call, what to say, whether it would go anywhere. She had tried four times and then she had stopped trying and then she was dead.

Sarah set the calendar down.

She found the police report. A copy of the original report Maya had filed in September, two months before she died—the one that Thomas had checked through his contact, the one the police had been unable to act on. She read it in the cold basement light.

Maya's handwriting was precise in the body of the form. She described the incidents in chronological order: the feeling of being watched beginning the previous spring, the items in the apartment that were not moved but felt disturbed, the figure she'd seen in the parking structure adjacent to her building, the call from a number she didn't recognize that went silent when she answered. She described a man named Thomas Vane, whom she had known socially for two years, who had expressed romantic interest she had not reciprocated, and who she believed was responsible.

The reporting officer had noted: *Complainant is distressed but coherent. No physical evidence of intrusion. No direct threats made. Referred to victim resources.*

*Referred to victim resources.*

Sarah sat for a long time.

She thought about the form of this—the institutional inadequacy of it, the gap between Maya's clear, chronological, coherent account and the officer's assessment that there was nothing actionable. Maya had done everything correctly. She had reported. She had named him. She had described specific incidents in credible detail. And the response had been: *referred to victim resources.*

She thought about Thomas reading this report through his contact and understanding that Maya had done everything right and it had not been sufficient. And then going to see her anyway two months later.

She put the report back in the box.

She found a photograph near the bottom—the two of them, Sarah and Maya, at an outdoor concert in the summer before Maya died. Maya was laughing at something offscreen. Sarah was looking at Maya. She looked, in the photograph, like someone who didn't know this was one of the last photographs.

She sat with the photograph for a long time.

Then she put everything back in the box, sealed it, and went upstairs.

[RUTH]
"Sarah. I've been thinking about you."

[SARAH]
"I need to tell you something. Not clinical. Personal."

[RUTH]
"Tell me."

[SARAH]
"I'm going to do something this week that I can't fully explain to you. I need to tell someone before I do it, and you're the person I trust most."

[NARRATOR]
A pause.

[RUTH]
"Tell me what you can."

[SARAH]
"I've been working toward a specific outcome for four months. The outcome involves the person who killed Maya. I believe I'm close to it. The path to it requires me to take a risk that—isn't quantifiable. And I've been telling myself for weeks that it's the only option, and this morning I sat in my basement for an hour and I realized I need to ask someone whether they think I'm right."

[RUTH]
"You're asking me if it's the right thing."

[SARAH]
"I'm asking you if I'm the right person to do it."

[NARRATOR]
Ruth was quiet for a moment in the way that was different from her clinical quiet—something more personal, the quiet of one woman thinking about another woman she'd known for eight years.

[RUTH]
"I'm going to ask you something I've never asked you in all the time we've known each other."

[SARAH]
"All right."

[RUTH]
"What do you actually want? Not the professional framing, not the strategic framing. What do you want."

[NARRATOR]
She sat at the kitchen table. The February light through the east window. The drawer with the card under the dish towels.

[SARAH]
"I want him to have to say it. Out loud. I want it to be documented and I want it to be used. I want—" She stopped. Started again. "I want Maya's death to be a fact that other people know about. That has official status. That is held somewhere outside my own body."

[RUTH]
"The closure."

[SARAH]
"I hate that word."

[RUTH]
"I know. I'm using it anyway. You want the fact of what happened to Maya to exist somewhere—to be recognized—so that it stops being something only you're carrying."

[SARAH]
"Yes."

[RUTH]
"Is what you're planning the only way to do that."

[NARRATOR]
She thought about Farris: *it's not enough.* She thought about Cole's burned investigation. She thought about Claire Novak, who had not yet responded. She thought about the address on the card in the drawer.

[SARAH]
"I think so. I've tried everything else."

[RUTH]
"Then I think you're the right person to do it. Not because you're the only person—but because you've been carrying this for ten years, and whatever it costs you, you already know the cost. You've been paying it."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah pressed her hand flat on the table.

[SARAH]
"I'm not sure I'll be the same after."

[RUTH]
"You haven't been the same for ten years. The question is whether you'll be—more yourself. Or less."

[NARRATOR]
A long pause.

[SARAH]
"I don't know."

[RUTH]
"That's the honest answer. Do it anyway."

[NARRATOR]
She sat for a moment after they hung up.

Then she went to her email. Claire Novak had written back.

Three sentences:

*I've been waiting for someone to ask. I'll meet you. Let me know when.*

[NARRATOR]
She texted Thomas from her personal phone that evening.

*Restaurant Soleil. Saturday, seven-thirty. I'll leave my name at the host stand.*

He replied within two minutes.

*I'll be there.*

She set the phone down.

She thought about what she was doing. Thought about it clearly, without the clinical framing and without the strategic framing, which was what Ruth had asked for. A woman whose sister had been killed by a man who had never been held accountable. A woman who had tried every institutional option and found them insufficient. A woman who was going to sit across a dinner table from that man and use the only tool she had left—the ability to receive him, to listen, to give him the shape of the understanding he'd been seeking for ten years—to get him to say what he had done.

And if that failed.

She thought about the address on the card. The shortcut he'd offered.

She thought: I won't need it.

She thought: but I know where it is.

She went upstairs. Set her alarm for six. Lay in the dark with the cold air coming through the window she'd cracked because she couldn't sleep in a sealed room and thought about Maya's face in the photograph—laughing at something offscreen, not knowing.

She thought: this is for you. All of it.

She thought: I'm sorry it took this long.

She thought about Cole's voice on the phone: *stay afraid. Fear is information.*

She was afraid.

She closed her eyes. The dark held her.

Saturday was in three days.
