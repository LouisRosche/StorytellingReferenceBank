[NARRATOR]
She met Claire Novak the next morning, Sunday, before she went to the address.

Cole had arranged it: a coffee shop in the north end, equidistant from both their offices, a neighborhood where neither of them was known. Claire was already there when Sarah arrived—a woman with a precise way of sitting, hands around a coffee cup, who looked up when Sarah came in with the expression of someone who had been rehearsing a version of this moment for a long time and wasn't sure which version they were in.

[SARAH]
"Claire."

[CLAIRE]
"You're taller than I expected."

[SARAH]
"I'm sorry—"

[CLAIRE]
"It's fine. It's a strange thing to say. Sit down." She looked at her hands. "I've been trying to work out what to say for two weeks. Since you wrote."

[SARAH]
"Take your time."

[CLAIRE]
"I don't think time is actually the issue. I've had eight years." She looked up. "How's your sister's case."

[SARAH]
"I'm working on it."

[CLAIRE]
"Is that why you found me."

[SARAH]
"Yes."

[NARRATOR]
Claire nodded. She had the look of someone who had been making decisions about how much to carry alone for so long that the weight had become structural—not a burden anymore but a feature.

[CLAIRE]
"I met him at a conference. Architectural design—I do graphic work for firms, I attend sometimes for networking. He was—" She paused. "He was the kind of person who notices you. Really notices. It's distinctive. Most people at professional events are looking past whoever they're talking to. He wasn't. He was completely present."

[SARAH]
"And afterward."

[CLAIRE]
"He found me. My professional website—it wasn't hard. He sent a very thoughtful email. We had coffee. I thought it was a professional connection." A pause. "It took me four months to understand what it actually was."

[SARAH]
"When did you understand."

[CLAIRE]
"When I found out how much he knew about me that I hadn't told him. My schedule. My sister's address—I was visiting her regularly that year. The coffee shop I went to on Wednesday mornings." She looked at the table. "And then I found out he'd been inside my apartment. A book was moved. A specific book I would have noticed because I'm particular about my shelves. And something in my bathroom cabinet was wrong—I couldn't say exactly what, just that it had been gone through."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah thought about the gap in Maya's box. The grief text. The window left open in February.

[CLAIRE]
"I filed the complaint. Civil harassment. I thought—" She stopped. "I thought if I named it officially it would stop."

[SARAH]
"What happened."

[CLAIRE]
"He came to see me. Before the hearing. He was very calm. He sat down across from me and he said he understood I was frightened and he respected that and he wasn't going to dispute the complaint. And then he told me something about myself that I hadn't told anyone. Something true, something I had been—keeping. Not criminal. But the kind of thing that if it reached my employer would have cost me significantly." She picked up her cup. Set it down. "He said he didn't want to share this information. He said he had no interest in harming me. He said he simply wanted me to understand that he was someone who paid very close attention, and that attention could be directed in any number of ways."

[SARAH]
"He threatened you with what he'd learned from watching you."

[CLAIRE]
"Yes. And I withdrew the complaint because I was twenty-nine and alone and I didn't know what else to do." She looked at Sarah directly. "I'm thirty-eight now. I know what else to do. If you're asking whether I'll testify, I'll testify."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah held the words for a moment. The steadiness of them. The eight years of holding this alone, compressed into two sentences.

[SARAH]
"I need to tell you something. What we have right now—your testimony, the circumstantial evidence—is not quite enough for a criminal prosecution. It establishes pattern. It changes the picture significantly. But a jury would need more."

[CLAIRE]
"A confession."

[SARAH]
"Yes."

[CLAIRE]
"Do you have one."

[SARAH]
"I'm working on it."

[NARRATOR]
Claire looked at her.

[CLAIRE]
"What does 'working on it' mean."

[SARAH]
"It means I'm going to speak with him directly. Today. Without the clinical frame, without a wire. As Maya's sister."

[CLAIRE]
"Alone."

[SARAH]
"My colleague will be outside."

[NARRATOR]
Claire was quiet for a moment. The coffee shop around them. Saturday morning—relaxed, bright, the particular ease of a city that didn't know anything was happening.

[CLAIRE]
"I need to ask you something I have no right to ask."

[SARAH]
"Ask."

[CLAIRE]
"Is this about justice for your sister or is it also about something else."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah thought about the basement and the photograph. About the voicemail with the breathing that wasn't Maya's. About Ruth's voice: *do it anyway.*

[SARAH]
"Both. I won't pretend otherwise."

[CLAIRE]
"Good. The people who say it's only justice are usually lying." She picked up her cup. "I hope you get what you need."

[SARAH]
"Thank you for meeting me."

[CLAIRE]
"Thank you for writing." She paused. "No one asked before. That sounds like something that should be obvious—that someone would ask. You'd be surprised."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah walked back to her car in the February morning light thinking about all the people who hadn't asked. The officer who had written *referred to victim resources.* The colleagues who had known Maya was frightened and called it anxiety. Herself, on the four nights she hadn't answered because she'd been asleep.

All the ways of not asking that had accumulated into a decade of Thomas Vane walking around in the world.

She got in the car.

She texted Cole: *Ready. One hour.*

She sat for a moment and thought about what she was going to say to him. Not the strategy. Not the frame. She'd been using frames for four months. What she would say as herself, with no frame, in a room that was his and not hers.

She thought about what it meant to listen for the last time.

Then she started the car.
