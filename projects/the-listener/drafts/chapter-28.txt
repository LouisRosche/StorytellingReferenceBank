[NARRATOR]
The building was in the east end—a converted textile mill, like the restaurant, the city having been through that particular phase of itself in the nineties and leaving behind a row of converted buildings that now held the kind of apartments for which the word *curated* was used without embarrassment. The exterior was brick, carefully maintained. Seven floors. A lobby she could see from the street through the glass doors, a doorman at a desk.

Cole parked across the street and two car lengths down.

[SARAH]
"I know."

[COLE]

[SARAH]
"That's not enough time—"

[COLE]

Cole at sixty-three, retired, who had been carrying this case for a decade and who had put his remaining credibility behind her, whose contact at the phone company was now out of a job. [COLE]
"Don't thank me yet. Go."

She crossed the street. The doorman was young, bored, and accepted her name without checking a list—which meant Thomas had told him she might be coming, and had not specified a particular window, which meant he had been expecting her since the card.

The elevator was slow. Seventh floor.

She had her phone in her coat pocket, voice memo running. She had started it in the car, before she crossed the street—not a wire, not a device, just her phone recording at close range in a coat pocket, the way anyone might accidentally leave their phone recording. She didn't know if it would be enough. She didn't know if anything would be enough. She was going in anyway.

Apartment 714. She knocked.

He opened the door quickly. He had been near it.

[THOMAS]
"I wasn't sure of the timing."

[SARAH]
"I had a morning appointment."

[THOMAS]
"Of course. Come in."

[NARRATOR]
The apartment was what she would have built for him if she'd been designing his space: high ceilings, the converted-mill bones of it still visible in the exposed beam work. A wall of windows facing east, the winter light coming through without warmth. The furniture was expensive and carefully chosen—not a display of wealth but a display of taste, which was its own kind of claim. Books on three walls. A drafting table in the corner with drawings spread across it.

Clean. Ordered. The specific quality of a space maintained by someone who lived alone and thought about arrangement.

She looked at the shelves.

She was looking for Maya's things. She had told herself she wouldn't—that it wasn't the point of this visit—but she looked anyway. The spines, the objects. A photograph on one shelf she couldn't quite see from where she stood.

[THOMAS]

[SARAH]
"I'll keep it."

"No wire this time."

[SARAH]

She thought about Maya. About the photograph in the boxes. The afternoon light on her face, reading in their shared bedroom, twelve years old.
