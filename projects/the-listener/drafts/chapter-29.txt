[NARRATOR]
Law moves at its own pace and in its own register, and what Sarah had understood abstractly she now understood in the body: the gap between *it's done* and *it's done* was vast, and was filled with procedures she could not speed up.

Farris took the recording that afternoon. She listened to it in full, twice, with Cole present and Sarah in the hallway. When Farris came out of the conference room she looked at Sarah with the expression of someone who had been waiting for this specific piece of evidence and was now deciding what it meant in the larger architecture.

[FARRIS]
"You obtained this by entering a private residence."

[SARAH]
"He invited me. Repeatedly, in writing. I have the letter and the card."

[FARRIS]
"One-party consent recording, voluntary disclosure by the subject." She paused. "It's good. I want to be careful—his attorney will challenge the conditions under which it was obtained, the prior relationship, the context. But the content is—it's good."

[SARAH]
"What happens now."

[FARRIS]
"I take this to the detective on the case. The case isn't cold—it's open, classified as unsolved. There's a detective assigned. We present the recording along with the corroborating evidence: the parking lot photographs, the phone tower data—"

[SARAH]
"The tower data was obtained—"

[FARRIS]
"I'm aware. We don't present that as primary evidence. But it forms part of the background investigation. Combined with the building security footage, Claire Novak's testimony, and the recording, we're building a picture that's significantly more actionable than what existed three months ago."

[SARAH]
"How long."

[FARRIS]
"To an arrest, potentially weeks. The case has to go to a prosecutor—I can facilitate that but I'm not the prosecutor. They have to decide whether the evidence is sufficient to support charges that can survive a trial."

[SARAH]
"And if it isn't."

[FARRIS]
"It is. I believe it is. But the decision isn't mine."

[NARRATOR]
She looked at Farris. Farris looked back. Two women in a conference room at four o'clock on a Saturday in February.

[SARAH]
"He said *good* when he found out I was recording. He knows what's coming."

[FARRIS]
"That's consistent with what you've described of his psychology throughout. He may not resist arrest. He may have been building toward this outcome."

[SARAH]
"He wanted to be received. Understood."

[FARRIS]
"He's going to get a trial instead."

[NARRATOR]
She thought about that. About what a trial would mean—the public record, the evidence laid out, Thomas on a stand being asked to account for himself in a language that wasn't his. She thought about whether that was the same as being received, or whether it was something entirely different.

She thought: it doesn't matter. It's what's available.

[SARAH]
"What about the licensing complaint."

[FARRIS]
"In light of an arrest and charges, the complaint becomes—context. An attorney representing a murder suspect who filed a harassment complaint against the investigating party is not going to press that complaint. And your licensing board will understand the full picture once the case is public."

[SARAH]
"Will it affect the case? His filing it—could that be used against him."

[FARRIS]
"Potentially, as evidence of consciousness of guilt. An ongoing effort to suppress investigation." She paused. "I wouldn't lead with it. But it's there."

[NARRATOR]
Sarah nodded. She had nothing else to ask. The questions she'd been holding for four months were out of her hands now—passed to Farris, to the detective assigned to the case, to a system she had been working around and was now working with.

She thought about the difference. The discomfort of it. The relief of it.

[SARAH]
"Is there anything else you need from me tonight."

[FARRIS]
"Go home. I'll call you when there's movement."

[NARRATOR]
Cole drove her back. They didn't talk much. The city at five o'clock on a Saturday—the particular quality of the early-winter dark, the streetlights coming on, the commerce of ordinary life proceeding on ordinary terms.

[COLE]
"How are you doing."

[SARAH]
"I don't know yet."

[COLE]
"That's the right answer."

[SARAH]
"Is it."

[COLE]
"It means you're paying attention to what's actually happening rather than the version you prepared for."

[NARRATOR]
She thought about the apartment. The east light. Thomas's face when he said *good*. The relief in it.

She thought about the photograph on the shelf—the two women she didn't recognize, laughing. The others she didn't know about, wouldn't know about unless they were found.

[SARAH]
"He's had others. Besides Claire."

[COLE]
"I know. I've suspected it."

[SARAH]
"When this is public—when the case is filed—some of them may come forward."

[COLE]
"Some may. Some won't. That's always the way."

[SARAH]
"It matters."

[COLE]
"It does. But it's not today's work. Today's work is done."

[NARRATOR]
He dropped her at her door. She went inside.

The house. The quiet. The kitchen with the drawer where the card had been, which she'd moved to her files.

She stood in the kitchen for a moment. Then she called Owen.

He answered immediately, the way he had been answering since she'd asked him to go.

[SARAH]
"I think it's done."

[NARRATOR]
A pause.

[OWEN]
"Done how."

[SARAH]
"I have a recording. Farris has it. There's a process now—there are weeks ahead of it, possibly longer. But the piece I was responsible for—I have it."

[NARRATOR]
She heard him exhale.

[OWEN]
"Are you okay."

[SARAH]
"I don't know yet."

[OWEN]
"Can we come home."

[SARAH]
"Yes. Come home."

[NARRATOR]
She made tea after she hung up. Sat at the kitchen table with it. The house was quiet in the way she'd been living in for eleven days, the wrong quiet of a house that was supposed to be full.

She thought about Thomas in his apartment. Tonight, after she'd left. What he would do. Whether he would call someone. Whether there was anyone to call.

She thought: probably not. The quality of a life lived in sustained attention to others was the absence of anyone attending in return.

She thought about the expression on his face when she'd given him the actual reception he'd been looking for—not performed, not strategic—and how it had lasted approximately three minutes before she stood and put on her coat and left.

Whether that had been enough for him.

Whether it should have been.

She didn't know. She suspected she would spend a significant portion of the rest of her professional life thinking about what it meant to give a person who had done something unforgivable the one thing they'd been looking for, in exchange for the account of the unforgivable thing, and whether that exchange was moral, and whether it was still therapy or had become something else.

She suspected Ruth would have thoughts about this.

She drank the tea. It had gone only slightly cold.

Outside, the February evening was darkening toward night, and in two days Owen and Emma would be home, and in some number of weeks Thomas Vane would be charged with the murder of Maya Mercer, and the case would become public, and the work she'd been doing in the dark for four months would have official status.

Would be held somewhere outside her own body.

She got up and turned on the kitchen light.

She thought: it isn't closure. Ruth had said that word and she'd said she hated it and she still did. Closure suggested a door that could be sealed.

What she had was something different. A fact that was now shared—not just hers and Thomas's and Cole's, but Farris's, and soon a prosecutor's, and eventually a jury's. A fact that would have a public record. A name on a document. A case with a defendant.

Maya's death, made official at last.

She thought: that will have to be enough.

She thought: it's more than I had this morning.

She left the light on and went to sit in the living room in the quiet, waiting for Sunday, waiting for Owen, waiting for the thing that came after the end of the thing she'd been doing.

Whatever that was.
