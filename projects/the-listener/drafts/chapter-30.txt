The arrest was on a Thursday, nineteen days after the recording.

Sarah was in her office when it happened. Not because she had scheduled sessions—the licensing inquiry was still formally open, her caseload at partial suspension pending the board's review of circumstances that were, as Farris had predicted, now significantly recontextualized. She was in the office because she had nowhere better to be and because the office was where she'd been trying, for four months, to think clearly.

Cole texted at eleven-forty-three.

*Done.*

One word. She held the phone and read it and sat very still.

She had known it was coming. Farris had called the evening before—the arrest warrant had been issued, she'd said. Tomorrow morning, she'd said. I wanted you to know before it's public.

Sarah had said: thank you. And then she'd sat on the edge of the bed in the dark—Owen asleep behind her, the house returned to its proper full quiet—and thought about tomorrow morning. About what it would feel like.

It felt like *done.* One word. Insufficient and also exact.

She called Cole.

[SARAH]

"How was it."

[COLE]

She sat in her office after the call. The white noise machine at the threshold—she had not turned it on, because there were no patients to protect. The chair across from her, empty.

She thought about what the chair had held. Twelve sessions. Thomas Vane in the chair—the composed posture, the careful language, the pale eyes tracking everything. The performance of openness and the thing underneath it.

She thought about all the people who had sat in that chair over the years who were not Thomas Vane. The man who could not stop thinking about his father. The young woman learning, slowly, that she was allowed to take up space. The teacher who had been carrying survivor's guilt from a childhood accident like a stone in his chest for twenty years.

The work. The actual work. Which was not what she had been doing for four months.

She thought about whether she could still do it. Whether the four months had taken something from the capacity—the native listening that Thomas had described, that she'd been trained to develop but that was there before the training. Whether using it the way she'd used it had depleted it or only redirected it.

She didn't know yet.

She would need to find out.

She turned on the white noise machine.

She sat in the chair. Let the room be what it was: a small room with a window and a radiator and two chairs and the accumulated weight of a hundred and forty patients over nine years, all the things that had been said and received and processed and carried away slightly lighter.

The work.

She thought: I need to find out if it's still there.

She picked up the phone and called the first patient on her deferred list. Said she was ready to return to full hours. Said she was sorry for the disruption. Said she hoped they'd been okay.

The patient—the man with the complicated grief about his father—said he'd been managing. Said he'd missed the sessions. Said he was glad she was back.

She said: me too.

She set her next appointment for Monday.

She sat in her office for a while after the call. The white noise. The radiator. The empty chair.

Outside her window, the day was happening—February becoming March, the city at midday going about its business. Someone's car on the street below. The restaurant across the way setting out its lunch board.

She thought about Maya. Thought about her without the archive of grief—not the voicemails, not the photograph, not the police report and the *referred to victim resources.* A different memory: Maya at their parents' house at Christmas, helping Emma with some card game Emma was too young for and didn't care, both of them laughing at something neither of them would remember. The ordinary abundance of a person alive.

The memory held.

She let it.

The chair was empty across from her. She looked at it.

She thought: someone will sit there on Monday. Someone with something they need to say and no one to say it to. And I will listen.

She thought: yes. It's still there.

She turned off the light, locked the office, and went home.
