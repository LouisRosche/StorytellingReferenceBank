[NARRATOR]
The licensing board closed its inquiry six weeks after the charges were filed, on a Monday in late April that was the first genuinely warm day of the year.

Withdrawn by the complainant, technically. The letter from the board's administrative office was brief, formal, and arrived by email rather than certified post, which was its own small commentary on the weight it assigned the matter. *The above-referenced inquiry is hereby closed. No adverse findings on record.*

She read it in her car in the parking garage. Then she forwarded it to Chen, her licensing attorney, who replied within four minutes: *Congratulations. This is the correct outcome. Send my regards to whoever handled the DA side.*

She wrote back: *I will. Thank you for everything.*

She sat in the car for a while. The parking garage. The familiar light of it, the smell, the distant sound of the elevator. She had sat in this garage many times in the past year, thinking about various versions of what was coming. She sat in it now thinking about what had come.

The charges were homicide in the first degree and one count of criminal stalking for the prior conduct documented in the proceedings against Maya. The stalking charge had been added at the prosecutor's request on the basis of Claire's testimony and the documentary evidence of the pattern of surveillance. The trial was scheduled for October.

She would testify. Her attorney had confirmed it and she had agreed, and the shape of what she would say was something she was still working on—the version that preserved privilege for the session content she was required to protect while contributing the things she was permitted to say. Her own account of being approached by Thomas. The book. The recordings she had made with consent disclosed, which was legally distinct from the session recordings she had made with consent implied. The parking lot photographs and the journal entry, entered through Cole.

The actual session disclosures—the twelve recorded sessions—were subject to a motion that the prosecutor's office had filed. Whether they would be admitted was a question for October and for the judge, and she had agreed to let that process take its course.

She was not thinking about October. She was thinking about the board letter and the word *closed* and the phrase *no adverse findings on record.*

She got out of the car.

Owen had found a recipe. He had opinions about the recipe, and the opinions were interesting, and she listened to them.

She thought about the empty chair. About Monday. About the man with his father's grief and what they had left to say to each other.

She thought about Maya. The ordinary abundance of a person alive.

She thought about the room with the locked door—the grief, the ordinary grief, now in the room with her where it belonged.

She was here. Owen was here. Emma was upstairs, alive, unsuspecting, brilliant. The year was turning. The light outside was lasting longer every day.

She drank her water.

She was here.
