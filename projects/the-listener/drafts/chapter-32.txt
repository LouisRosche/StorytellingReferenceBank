[NARRATOR]
She went through Maya's boxes properly in May, on a Saturday when Owen had taken Emma to a soccer tournament two hours away and the house was hers. Not the emergency excavation of February—this was deliberate, unhurried, the full accounting.

She brought the boxes upstairs. All three of them. Set them on the living room floor in the May morning light.

She went through them methodically: books first, then papers, then the miscellaneous box that turned out to contain the objects that didn't fit anywhere else—a set of measuring spoons Maya had inexplicably kept, a collection of postcards from cities she'd visited, the small wooden box that had belonged to their grandmother and that Sarah had not known Maya had.

She put the books on the shelves in the living room, where they belonged. Maya's color-ordered system was impossible to replicate exactly—Sarah didn't have the same palette on her shelves—so she arranged them by color as best she could, making something new that contained Maya's logic.

The papers she sorted: things to keep, things to recycle, things that were Maya's ongoing life and would have meant something to Maya but meant nothing except memory now. She kept more than she would have six months ago. Less than she would have a year ago.

The photograph from the bottom of the papers box: the two of them at the concert, Maya laughing. She put it in a frame—she had frames in a drawer somewhere, she found one—and set it on the shelf among the color-organized books.

Maya's handwriting on the inside cover of the grief text: she had the book back, had kept it since the session where Thomas had handed it to her in a paper bag. She'd considered whether it should be evidence. Farris had looked at it and taken photographs of the inscription and returned it—*you should have it,* Farris had said, without further explanation. She put it on the shelf among Maya's other books. The cloud with a face. The looping M.

The grandmother's box, which turned out to contain: a folded note in the grandmother's handwriting that Sarah couldn't fully read, two silver buttons, a photograph of a woman she didn't recognize, and a key to nothing she could identify. She put it back in the box and put the box on the shelf because it was Maya's grandmother's box and it was hers now.

When she was done, the living room shelves had a new section. Not a shrine—she was specifically not doing a shrine. A presence. The color-organized books, the framed photograph, the grandmother's box.

She sat on the living room floor and looked at it.

She had been maintaining the absence for ten years. The management of grief as a practice of containment—don't let it spread, don't let it move around, keep it where you put it in the first weeks and check on it periodically to make sure it hasn't grown. It had been good clinical practice applied to her own life and it had also been, she now understood, a way of keeping Maya dead.

Not letting her be a presence. Keeping her a wound.

She sat on the floor for a while.

She set down the phone.

She thought about the empty boxes, which she should break down for recycling. She thought about dinner—Owen and Emma would be back by seven, she should figure out something.

She thought about Monday. The patient with the father's grief. A new patient she'd taken on last week, the first new intake since the suspension period ended, a young woman with insomnia and a feeling she couldn't describe that her therapist had written in the chart as *pervasive dread.* Sarah had her own thoughts about what it was and what it would take. She was looking forward to finding out.

She thought about the trial in October. What she would say. How she would say it. The prosecutor had been through it with her twice and would go through it again in September. She knew what she was permitted to say and what she wasn't and she had made a certain peace with the constraints.

She thought about Thomas. Twice a week now she thought about Thomas, which was different from the constant low-level occupation of the previous months. She thought about the apartment. The east light. The face with the seam in it, the seam that ran between the version of himself he'd been performing for ten years and the thing it was covering.

She thought: I gave him what he wanted. For approximately three minutes, in a room with a recording running, I gave him genuine reception without management, and in those three minutes he told me what he did, and the genuine reception was also the price I was willing to pay to hear it.

She didn't know what to do with that. She had discussed it with Ruth and would discuss it again. It sat in her like something she was still digesting.

She thought: that's all right. Some things take time.

She got up off the floor. Took the empty boxes to the recycling. Went to the kitchen and figured out dinner.

The May light came through the windows and the shelves in the living room held Maya's books in their color-organized logic and the framed photograph caught the light in a way that was good, and the house was quiet, and Sarah was in it, and this was her life.

She put water on for pasta.

She thought: I'm here.

She thought: that will have to be enough.

She thought: it is.
