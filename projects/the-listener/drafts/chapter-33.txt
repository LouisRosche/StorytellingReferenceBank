[NARRATOR]
The trial ran eleven days in October, in a courtroom on the fourth floor of the city's main criminal courts building. Sarah attended every day, which her attorney said was her right and which the prosecutor said was useful and which Cole said was something he understood without agreeing with it entirely.

She sat in the gallery. Not behind the prosecution—she was a witness and the rules of the proceeding governed where she could be when. She sat toward the back, in the section where family of victims were permitted, which was the first time an official document had named her in that capacity.

She had thought about what it would feel like to sit in a room with Thomas for eleven days. She had thought about it specifically, clinically, the way she thought about anticipated stressors—what the physiological response would be, how she would manage it, what she would tell a patient in her position.

She had been wrong about most of it.

Thomas did not perform. She had expected performance—the controlled presentation she had watched for four months, the careful language, the equanimity. Instead, she watched a man sit through eleven days of evidence and testimony with something closer to attention than affect. He watched the proceedings. He listened to the witnesses. He conferred with his attorney in the brief intervals, but the conferences were short, and she could not read them from where she sat, and once or twice she caught him looking at something other than the witness or the evidence—looking at the middle distance, the way people look when they are thinking about something they haven't decided yet.

She did not know what he was deciding.

She testified on the fifth day. The prosecutor had prepared her carefully, and what she had to say in the permitted scope was specific and factual: the circumstances of his initial appointment, the nature of the items he had brought to sessions, her professional observations of his behavior in and around her practice. The book, identified as belonging to her sister through the inscription and photographs submitted as exhibits. The parking lot photographs, entered through Cole.

She was on the stand for four hours. Defense cross-examination tried three separate approaches: the personal stake she had in the outcome, the professional violations she had committed during the investigation, and the argument that everything she had done was motivated by grief-driven fixation rather than legitimate concern. She answered everything directly and without hedging, which was what the prosecutor had told her to do and which was also simply how she spoke.

The last question from the defense was: "Dr. Mercer, after four months in a therapeutic relationship with my client—is it your testimony that you understood him?"

She looked at Thomas. He was looking at her. For the first time in the eleven days, his attention was fully on her face, not the middle distance.

[SARAH]
"I listened to him. That's not the same as understanding. And it's not the same as forgiveness."

[NARRATOR]
Thomas looked down at the table.

The defense had no further questions.

When she stepped down, she walked back to her seat in the gallery. She did not look at Thomas's table.

Cole testified on the sixth day. Claire testified on the seventh. Claire was on the stand for six hours and was steady throughout, and when she described the moment Thomas had told her he was someone who paid very close attention and that attention could be directed in various ways, Sarah watched two jurors' faces change.

The recording was admitted on the eighth day, after the judge had heard the motion and ruled. Sarah sat in the gallery and listened to her own voice and Thomas's voice come out of the courtroom speakers, and the courtroom was very still, and Thomas at the defense table was very still, and Sarah held the railing on the gallery bench with one hand and let it be real.

The jury took two days.

Guilty on both counts.

She heard it. Heard it with her whole body—not relief, not triumph, something more interior than either. The fact of it. The official status of the thing. Maya's death, named.

Cole was in the gallery. She had not known he was there until she turned around after the verdict and found him three rows back, hands folded, face doing something careful and contained that she recognized from four months of parking lots and diner booths.

She made her way through the crowd to him.

He said nothing for a moment. Then:

[COLE]
"Ten years."

[SARAH]
"Ten years."

[NARRATOR]
They stood in the courtroom as it emptied. The reporters were in the corridor—she would have to go through them, there was no other way out. She had prepared a brief statement she would not read from because she had memorized it: a sentence about Maya, a sentence about the process, a sentence about gratitude to the people who had made it possible. She had not included anything about the four months, about the sessions, about what she had done. That was not the public record, and its absence from the public record was something she had made her peace with.

[SARAH]
"Will you come? To the corridor."

[COLE]
"I'll be beside you."

[NARRATOR]
She went through the corridor. She said what she had prepared. The cameras were there and she spoke to them and she said Maya's name in a sentence with the word *justice* and she felt, saying it, that she was not performing.

That it was true.

After, in the car with Cole, she was quiet for a long time.

[COLE]
"You should go home."

[SARAH]
"I know."

[COLE]
"Owen's waiting."

[SARAH]
"I know."

[NARRATOR]
She looked out the window at the October city. The particular light of October—lower and more golden than September, already containing winter. People on the street who did not know what had just happened in a courtroom on the fourth floor of a building they were walking past.

She thought about Thomas. The sentencing was in December. She would not attend. She had decided this some weeks ago—that the trial was her obligation and the sentencing was not, that there was a limit to what she was able to witness.

She thought about the verdict. Guilty on both counts. The official naming of what had happened. The fact that now existed in a public record in a way that could be looked up, cited, verified. That was not nothing. She had spent four months pursuing it and it was not nothing.

She thought about Maya. Not the grief, not the photograph, not the locked room. Just Maya. Just her sister. The specific person—the cloud doodle, the color-organized books, the laugh in the concert photograph directed at something offscreen.

She thought: you deserved better. You deserved someone who asked sooner.

She thought: I'm sorry. I'm sorry I didn't answer.

She thought: I hope this is something. I hope wherever you are, if anywhere, this is something.

The car moved through the October city. Cole drove. She sat and looked out the window.

She thought: come home now.

She told Cole to take her home.
