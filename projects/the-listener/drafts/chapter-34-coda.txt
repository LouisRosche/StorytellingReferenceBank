She went to the cemetery in November, on a Tuesday afternoon when the light was doing something honest. Maya was buried in the city's oldest cemetery, in the section that her family's plot had been in for three generations. Sarah had visited on Maya's birthday every year—a practiced thing, a ritual maintained partly because she needed it and partly because she believed in the utility of ceremony even without faith in what the ceremony addressed.

Today was not Maya's birthday. Today was just a Tuesday.

She stood at the stone. The name, the dates, the space in between.

She said: I heard him say it. She said: I heard him say it and it's on a record now and he's going to spend the rest of his life accountable for it, in the way that's available.

She said: I'm sorry about the four calls. I've been sorry for ten years. I'm still sorry. But I think I've been sorry in a way that kept you frozen, and I'm trying to grieve instead, which is different. Grief moves. Sorry stays put.

She said: I put your books on the shelves. The color system is a little off but I did my best.

She said: Emma is doing well. She's been asking about you—more lately, since the trial. I've been telling her things. Not everything, but things. The cloud doodle. The concert photograph. The way you organized by color, which Emma finds baffling and also correct.

She stood for a while longer. The November cold. The other stones, their names and dates.

She thought about what she'd told the prosecutor in September when he'd asked how she wanted to describe Maya in her testimony: *She was my sister and she paid close attention to the world. She noticed things. She was going to do something with what she noticed.*

She thought: I don't know what she would have done with what she noticed. I'll never know. That's the specific shape of this grief and it doesn't get smaller, it just becomes more familiar.

She said: I love you. She had not said this out loud in ten years at this stone, because she had been managing the grief rather than being in it, and you don't say *I love you* to someone when you're managing. You only say it when you're actually there.

She was there.

She walked back to the car. Got in. Sat for a moment.

On a Wednesday in December, she got the text from Cole.

*Forty-two years. No early release eligible. It's done.*

She was in her office, between patients. She read it. Held the phone.

She wrote back: *Thank you for staying with this.*

He wrote: *Thank you for finishing it.*

She sat for a moment, and then she set the phone face down and pulled up her notes for the next session—the young woman with the insomnia and the pervasive dread, who had, over the past three months, been slowly identifying that the dread was connected to a series of choices she'd made in her twenties that she believed were unforgivable. They had been working, carefully, on the difference between consequences and punishment. On what she owed herself, now, versus what had already been paid.

It was interesting work. Sarah was finding it interesting in the way she had found this work interesting before—not the managed interest of performance but the actual thing, the curiosity about a particular person's particular experience of being alive.

She had found the room. The locked room with the grief in it. And with the grief, it turned out, was the rest of what had been locked away: the capacity, the presence, the native listening that Thomas had described and that she had been performing for four months while the real thing waited for her to come back.
