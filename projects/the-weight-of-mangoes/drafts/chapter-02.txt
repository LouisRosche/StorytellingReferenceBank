[NARRATOR]
The photographs spilled across the coffee table like leaves in autumn, each one a window into a world that no longer existed. Nirmala sat very still, her hands folded in her lap, watching her grandchildren pick through the images as if they were artifacts in a museum.

Which, she supposed, they were.

[MAYA]
"Who's this, Nani? She looks like you."

[NARRATOR]
Maya held up a photograph, sepia-toned and cracked at the edges. A young woman in a sari, standing in front of a wooden house with a zinc roof, her face turned slightly away from the camera as if she'd been caught mid-movement.

Nirmala felt her breath catch.

[NIRMALA]
"That is my mother. Your great-grandmother. She was maybe... twenty-five? Twenty-six? Before I was born."

[MARCUS]
"I didn't know we had pictures this old."

[ANITA]
"They were in a box at the back of Ma's closet. I found them when I was looking for the good tablecloth."

[NARRATOR]
Anita stood near the doorway, uncertain, as if she wasn't sure whether bringing the photographs had been a gift or a trespass. Nirmala understood the feeling. Some boxes were meant to stay closed.

But the box was open now. And her grandchildren were looking at her with those hungry eyes—the same hunger Marcus had shown when he arrived early, asking questions about the old days. They wanted something from her. They wanted to know where they came from.

The trouble was, Nirmala wasn't sure she could give it to them. Some things didn't translate. Some losses didn't have words.

[KAMLA]
"Ma? You okay?"

[NARRATOR]
Kamla's hand on her shoulder, warm and familiar. Her eldest daughter, the one who had stayed closest, who understood the weight of carrying a family even when it crushed you.

[NIRMALA]
"I'm fine. Just... remembering."

[SUNIL]
"We should probably put these away. This is supposed to be a party."

[NARRATOR]
Her son's voice, tight and clipped, the way it always was when he came to this house. Sunil had never been comfortable with the old photographs, the old stories, the old ways. He had built a life in California that didn't include any of this—a white wife, a son who could pass, a career that demanded he be American first and everything else never.

Nirmala understood. She did not forgive, but she understood.

[MAYA]
"Uncle Sunil, with all due respect—shut up."

[SUNIL]
"Excuse me?"

[MAYA]
"This is important. Look at her face. She wants to tell us something."

[NARRATOR]
Maya pointed at Nirmala, and all eyes turned to her. Seven people in the room—her children, her grandchildren, all waiting for something she wasn't sure she knew how to give.

[KAMLA]
"Ma? Is there something you want to say?"

[NARRATOR]
Nirmala looked at the photographs scattered across the table. Her mother at twenty-five. Her grandmother at seventeen, standing on a dock she would never see again. Faces she had known and faces she had only heard described, stories passed down like beads on a string, each generation dropping a few until only fragments remained.

What did she want to say?

Everything. Nothing. The truth, which was too large for words.

[NIRMALA]
"That one. Pick up that one."

[NARRATOR]
Marcus reached for the photograph she indicated—the oldest one in the pile, brown and fragile, the edges crumbling when he touched them.

[MARCUS]
"Careful—it's falling apart."

[NIRMALA]
"Yes. Everything falls apart. That is what time does."

[NARRATOR]
She took the photograph from him, held it in her weathered hands. A young woman—a girl, really, barely more than a child—standing on a wooden dock with a bundle in her arms. Behind her, the hull of a ship. She was looking at something outside the frame, something the camera couldn't capture.

She was looking at a country she would never see again.

[NIRMALA]
"This is my grandmother. Your great-great-grandmother. Her name was Savitri. She was seventeen years old when this picture was taken."

[MAYA]
"Seventeen? She looks—"

[NIRMALA]
"Old. Yes. That is what happens when you carry too much too young."

[NARRATOR]
The room was quiet now. Even Sunil had stopped checking his phone. Jason had moved closer, standing near the edge of the group, his pale face uncertain but listening.

Good, Nirmala thought. Let them listen. Let them finally hear.

[KAMLA]
"Ma, you don't have to—"

[NIRMALA]
"Hush. I am eighty years old. If I don't tell this now, who will tell it when I am gone? You? You know pieces only. Bits and scraps I gave you when you were small, when I thought you couldn't carry the whole weight."

[NARRATOR]
She looked around the room—at Kamla, who had carried so much already; at Sunil, who had run from carrying anything; at Anita, who had stayed so close she could barely breathe; at the grandchildren, who were hungry for a history they had never been taught.

[NIRMALA]
"Sit. All of you. Sit."

[NARRATOR]
They sat. Even Sunil. Even Jason.

Nirmala held the photograph of her grandmother and felt the old stories rising in her chest like water from a deep well. She had not spoken these things in years. Had not allowed herself to remember them fully, because remembering meant feeling, and feeling meant grieving all over again for what could never be recovered.

But her grandchildren were looking at her. And they deserved to know.

[NIRMALA]
"My grandmother Savitri was born in a village in Bihar. A small village—I don't remember the name. She used to tell me, but I forgot. Isn't that a terrible thing? I forgot the name of the place my own grandmother was born."

[MARCUS]
"It's okay, Nani. It was a long time ago."

[NIRMALA]
"Yes. And no. Time is funny. Some things that happened yesterday feel far away. Some things that happened a hundred years ago feel like they are still happening, right now, in my chest."

[NARRATOR]
She touched the photograph, traced the outline of her grandmother's face.

[NIRMALA]
"When Savitri was sixteen, a man came to her village. He was what they called an arkati—a recruiter. He told the young people that there was work in a place called the West Indies. Good work, on tea plantations. Five years of labor, and then they could return home with money enough to buy land, to marry well, to live the life they could never have as poor farmers' daughters."

[MAYA]
"That was a lie."

[NIRMALA]
"Of course it was a lie. Everything they told her was a lie. But how was she to know? She was sixteen. She had never been more than five miles from her village. She didn't know where Trinidad was. She didn't know what indenture meant. She only knew that her family was hungry, and a man was offering her a way out."

[SUNIL]
"Ma, we've heard this story. The British brought indentured workers from India after slavery was abolished. It's history."

[NIRMALA]
"History! You think history is something you read in a book? History is my grandmother's hands, cracked from cutting cane. History is the babies she lost because there was no doctor, no medicine, no one who cared if a coolie woman's child lived or died. History is what she carried in her body until the day she died, and what she passed to my mother, and what my mother passed to me."

[NARRATOR]
The room was very quiet.

[NIRMALA]
"You want to know about history? Let me tell you about history. Let me tell you what my grandmother told me, when I was a little girl sitting on her lap in our house in San Fernando. Let me tell you what it was like to leave everything you know and cross an ocean to a place where they would work you like an animal and call you less than human."

[JASON]
"I want to hear it."

[NARRATOR]
Everyone turned to look at Jason. He had been so quiet, standing at the edge of the group, that Nirmala had almost forgotten he was there. Her grandson who didn't look like her, who had been raised so far from this house, from this family, from these stories.

But he was looking at her now with something like need in his eyes.

[NIRMALA]
"You want to hear it?"

[JASON]
"Yes, ma'am. I—I don't know any of this. Dad never... we never talked about it."

[NARRATOR]
Nirmala looked at Sunil, who had gone very still. Her son, who had spent forty years trying to erase the past, was now confronted with his own child, asking for exactly what he had refused to give.

Good, she thought. Let him see what his silence has cost.

[NIRMALA]
"Then sit closer, child. And listen."

[NARRATOR]
Jason moved forward, settling on the floor near her feet the way Marcus had done when he was small. The room shifted, gathered, drew closer around the old woman with the photograph in her hands.

Nirmala looked at the picture of her grandmother one more time. Savitri, seventeen years old, standing on a dock in Calcutta, waiting to board a ship that would take her halfway around the world to a place she had never heard of.

She had been so young. They had all been so young.

[NIRMALA]
"It began with a lie. Like most terrible things do."

[NARRATOR]
And then Nirmala began to speak, and the room fell away, and time folded in on itself, and they were all standing on that dock together—seven people in a Brooklyn brownstone, listening to the voice of a woman who had been dead for sixty years, speaking through the mouth of her granddaughter, passing on the story that must not be forgotten.

That must never be forgotten.

[NIRMALA]
"She was seventeen years old when they put her on that ship. Seventeen years old, and she would never see India again..."
