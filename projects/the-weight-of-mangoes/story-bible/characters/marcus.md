# Character Sheet: Marcus

## Basic Information

**Name**: Marcus Persaud-Morrison
**Role**: Protagonist (ensemble), third generation
**Age**: 28
**Gender**: Male
**Occupation**: High school history teacher, public school in Brooklyn

## Physical Description

**Appearance**: Tall—six-one—takes after his father's side. Brown skin that falls between his parents' tones. Face is his grandmother's: same cheekbones, same deep-set eyes. Hair worn in short locs. Broad shoulders, carries himself like he played basketball (he did, through college).

**Clothing style**: Clean but relaxed. Chinos, button-downs, sneakers. African print shirts on weekends. One gold chain, thin, his grandfather's.

**Physical mannerisms**: Gestures when excited, arms wide, claiming space. Leans in when listening—close listener. Rubs the back of his neck when uncertain.

**Voice**: Deep. Brooklyn cadence, unhurried. Inflects up when asking genuine questions. Laughs from the belly. Can quiet a classroom or a room.

## Psychology

### External Goal
To learn what his grandmother knows. To connect with heritage before it's too late.

### Internal Need
To know he belongs—to his culture, to his family, to himself.

### Core Fear
That he's performing identity he hasn't earned. That his interest in heritage is aesthetic, not authentic.

### Core Wound
Growing up mixed (Indo-Caribbean and Jamaican-American) in Brooklyn, he was never fully one thing. Both sides claimed him, but he sometimes felt like a visitor to both. His grandmother's world felt magical but unreachable.

### Lie They Believe
That heritage is something you receive, not something you build.

### Truth They'll Learn
That belonging isn't about bloodline or knowledge—it's about presence, attention, choosing to stay.

## Personality

**Dominant traits**: Curious, earnest, gentle, searching, romantic (in the philosophical sense)

**Contradictions**: Intellectually confident but culturally uncertain. Wants roots but also wants to define himself. Admires his grandmother's groundedness but wouldn't choose her life.

**How they see themselves**: A good person trying to figure it out. Worried about being corny.

**How others see them**: Kind. Thoughtful. A little intense when he gets going. The one who asks questions.

**Under stress, they**: Goes quiet. Goes for walks. Calls his mother.

## Voice and Speech

**Vocabulary level**: Educated, comfortable with academic language from teaching, but prefers plain speech.

**Sentence structure**: Casual American. Asks a lot of questions.

**Speech patterns**:
- Brooklyn cadence: "Nah, for real though"
- Uses Jamaican from his father: "soon come," "mi hear you"
- Tries Trini with Nani—careful, sometimes overpronounced
- Asks meaning of words: "Nani, what does tabanca mean again?"

**Topics they return to**: History (professional interest), family stories, his students, the neighborhood changing

**Topics they avoid**: Whether he's doing enough with his life, his dating life, his uncertainties about faith

### Dialogue Sample

```
[MARCUS]: Nani, can you teach me? I want to learn how you do it.

[MARCUS]: My students, they don't even know where their grandparents came from. I didn't want to be like that.

[MARCUS]: Sometimes I feel like I'm on the outside looking in. Like I'm studying my own family.

[MARCUS]: I'm here. Whatever you need. I'm not going anywhere.
```

## Background

**Formative experiences**:
- Summer visits to Nani's house as a child—the smell, the food, the other language
- College Black Student Union—first time he had to articulate his mixed identity
- Starting teaching—found a purpose

**Family/Origin**: Born in Brooklyn, raised there. Visits to Trinidad twice as a child. Jamaica a few more times. Never been to India.

**Education**: Public school, SUNY for undergrad (history major), MAT program after.

**Skills/Expertise**: Teaching. Listening. Making teenagers feel seen. Cooking rice and peas (from his dad's side).

## Arc

**Starting state**: Seeking connection with heritage through knowledge—asking questions, gathering facts.

**Key turning points**:
1. Nani's story—realizes heritage isn't information, it's experience
2. The forgotten song—sees loss is present, not past
3. The mango lesson—learns that inheritance is presence, attention, practice

**Ending state**: No longer seeking heritage as external thing. Understanding that being there, staying, is the act.
