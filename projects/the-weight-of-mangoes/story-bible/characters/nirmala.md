# Character Sheet: Nirmala ("Nani")

## Basic Information

**Name**: Nirmala Doodnath (nee Doodnath, married Doodnath—same surname by coincidence, common in Debe)
**Role**: Protagonist (ensemble)
**Age**: 80 (turning 80 at story's start)
**Gender**: Female
**Occupation**: Retired seamstress; ran a alterations shop in Flatbush for 30 years

## Physical Description

**Appearance**: Small—five feet even, was taller before her spine curved. Skin the brown of milked tea, creased deeply around eyes and mouth from decades of squinting at stitches and smiling. Hair silver-white, still thick, worn in a braid down her back or coiled at her nape. Hands show her trade: fingers slightly crooked, calluses on thumb and index finger, a slight tremor she tries to hide. Eyes dark brown, still sharp.

**Clothing style**: House dress most days—the kind with snaps down the front, cotton, practical. For company: good sari or salwar kameez, saved for occasions. Always gold studs in her ears (her mother's), thin gold bangles that haven't come off in fifty years, a mangalsutra she still wears though Jairam's been gone 26 years.

**Physical mannerisms**: Touches her earrings when nervous. Presses her palms together—not namaste, just thinking. Sucks her teeth (steups) for disapproval. Moves slowly now but precisely—a seamstress's economy of motion.

**Voice**: Medium pitch, slightly hoarse from age. Musical Caribbean rhythm, rises at the end of sentences. Speaks quietly but projects when she needs to. Can still fill a room. Occasional rasp when tired.

## Psychology

### External Goal
To have her family together. To see them fed, to hear them laugh, to know they're alright.

### Internal Need
To be remembered accurately—not as a symbol of heritage but as a person who was young once, who lost things, who chose things.

### Core Fear
That she will be forgotten. That her mother's face, already blurred, will blur further; that her grandchildren will remember her the way she remembers Ajji—as a story, not a person.

### Core Wound
Her grandmother Ajji died when Nirmala was three. Everything she knows of India, of the crossing, of the old life, came secondhand through her mother. She carries a history she didn't witness. She's afraid she hasn't passed it on properly.

### Lie They Believe
That silence protects. That not speaking about loss prevents it from hurting the people she loves.

### Truth They'll Learn
That her grandchildren are hungry for the stories. That they can carry what she's carried. That telling doesn't diminish—it distributes the weight.

## Personality

**Dominant traits**: Observant, steady, private, practical, tender (though she hides it), stubborn

**Contradictions**: Wants connection but creates distance through silence. Loves her children equally but treats them differently. Believes in God but bargains with Him like an adversary.

**How they see themselves**: Old. Tired. Lucky to have made it this far. Uncertain if she did right by her children.

**How others see them**: The center. The keeper. The one who must be pleased, protected, consulted. Mysterious—she's been there all their lives but none of them know her fully.

**Under stress, they**: Go quiet. Cook. Pray. Clean something that's already clean.

## Voice and Speech

**Vocabulary level**: Educated but informal. Can speak standard English perfectly; chooses not to most of the time.

**Sentence structure**: Mixed. Simple declarations when sure; longer when explaining.

**Speech patterns**:
- "Eh" as conversational tag (right? / isn't it?)
- "Go" for future: "I go tell she"
- Drops articles casually
- Hindi terms of endearment: beta, beti
- Religious exclamations: Hai Ram

**Topics they return to**:
- Whether people have eaten
- The weather in Trinidad (follows it still)
- The news (worried about the world her grandchildren inherit)
- Memories, but only when prompted

**Topics they avoid**:
- Jairam's death
- Her mother's last years
- Anything to do with Sunil's distance
- The crossing (until Chapter 2)

## Background

**Formative experiences**:
- Ajji's death (age 3)—no memory, but the absence shaped everything
- Her mother's stories—filled the absence with history
- Marriage at 20—arranged, then chosen
- Emigration (age 30)—losing Trinidad to save her children
- Jairam's death (age 54)—widowhood as her longest state

**Family/Origin**: Father was a cane worker, then a taxi driver. Mother kept the house, raised five children. Nirmala was the middle child. Only surviving sibling is a brother in Toronto they rarely see.

**Education**: Through secondary school in Trinidad—enough to be literate in English and Hindi, to keep accounts, to navigate. Self-taught in everything that mattered.

**Skills/Expertise**: Sewing (expert). Cooking (legendary). Reading people (survival). Silence (practiced).

## Arc

**Starting state**: Contained. Holding everything. Unsure if anyone wants what she carries.

**Key turning points**:
1. The photographs arrive—door opens
2. She begins to tell Ajji's story—commitment
3. She forgets the song's words—loss made visible
4. She teaches Marcus to cut a mango—finds a way to pass it on

**Ending state**: Still herself, but witnessed. Not unburdened but lighter. Having learned that legacy is not information but presence.
