# Language Guide: The Weight of Mangoes

## Code-Switching Principles

### The Rule
Code-switching in this story is character, not decoration. Each character has a linguistic home base and switches under specific conditions. The text does not italicize non-English words (following Junot Diaz's practice)—they belong to the narrative as much as English does.

### Why People Switch

1. **Emotional intensity**: When feelings are too strong, people reach for their first language
2. **Intimacy**: Speaking the home language signals closeness, in-group membership
3. **Authority**: Elders may switch to command attention or respect
4. **Exclusion**: Switching can deliberately exclude others from conversation
5. **Lack**: Some concepts have no English equivalent
6. **Performance**: Younger generations may code-switch to signal cultural allegiance

## Character Language Profiles

### Nirmala (Nani)

**Base**: Trinidadian English, with educated standard capacity

**Code-Switches To**:
- **Hindi phrases**: For religious contexts, proverbs, emotional extremes
- **Trini Creole**: With Kamla, with old friends, when relaxed or upset
- **Standard English**: With doctors, officials, grandchildren she doesn't know well

**Speech Patterns**:
- Uses "eh" as discourse marker (interrogative, confirmatory)
- "Oui" occasionally (French Creole influence)
- Drops copula: "You hungry?" instead of "You are hungry?"
- Uses "go" for future tense: "I go tell she" (I will tell her)
- Hindu-influenced constructions: "What to do?" (kya karein)

**Characteristic Phrases**:
- "Chile" (child) as endearment/exclamation
- "Ent?" (isn't it? / right?)
- "Tanty" for older women
- "Maco" (nosy person, gossip)
- "Tabanca" (lovesick grief)
- "Beta" (Hindi: child, term of endearment)
- "Hai Ram" (oh God)
- "Accha" (okay, I see)

### Kamla

**Base**: American English with Caribbean substrate

**Code-Switches To**:
- **Trini Creole**: With mother, when stressed, when speaking to Caribbean friends
- **Jamaican patterns**: Picked up from husband Michael
- **Standard American**: Professional contexts, with non-Caribbean people

**Speech Patterns**:
- More American sentence structure than Nani
- Caribbean vocabulary breaks through: "liming" (hanging out), "fete" (party)
- Rhythm shifts Caribbean when emotional
- Uses standard "r" sounds but softens them when code-switching

**Characteristic Phrases**:
- "Lord have mercy" (American but Caribbean delivery)
- "Watch yourself, eh"
- "What stupidness is this?"
- "I done told you"

### Michael (Kamla's husband, Jamaican-American)

**Base**: American English with Jamaican substrate

**Code-Switches To**:
- **Jamaican Patois**: With Jamaican family, for humor, for emphasis
- **Trini English**: Has absorbed some from Kamla over 30 years

**Characteristic Phrases**:
- "Cho" (expression of dismissal/frustration)
- "Mi nuh know" (I don't know)
- "Yuh see it" (you understand?)
- "Soon come" (be right there / eventually)

### Sunil

**Base**: Standard American English

**Code-Switches To**:
- **Trini Creole**: Rarely, accidentally, when very emotional
- Actively suppresses non-American markers

**Speech Patterns**:
- Flattened accent, assimilated
- Occasional Caribbean construction slips through: "You're always in some bacchanal"
- Gets uncomfortable when others speak Creole around him

**Characteristic Phrases**:
- Uses American idioms: "at the end of the day," "to be honest"
- Avoids Caribbean vocabulary; this is noticeable

### Anita

**Base**: American English, educated

**Code-Switches To**:
- **Trini Creole**: With Nani, when channeling her mother
- **Hindi words**: Has learned more than her siblings, uses them with Nani

**Speech Patterns**:
- Can move fluidly; has paid attention
- Uses Hindi endearments correctly: "Nani," "beta"
- Caribbean rhythm when speaking to Nani

**Characteristic Phrases**:
- "Ma" for Nani when alone with her
- Uses food vocabulary correctly: "bodi," "saijan," "baigan"

### Marcus

**Base**: Brooklyn English (Black/Caribbean-influenced American)

**Code-Switches To**:
- **Trini phrases**: Consciously, showing respect/connection to Nani
- **Jamaican patterns**: From father's side
- **AAVE**: With friends, in comfortable contexts

**Speech Patterns**:
- Searching for connection; sometimes over-performs Caribbean-ness
- Asks Nani to translate; wants to learn
- Brooklyn accent is his natural register

**Characteristic Phrases**:
- "Nah, for real though"
- "That's crazy" (genuine interest)
- Mixes in Jamaican and Trini he's picked up: "soon come," "ent?"

### Maya

**Base**: American English, academic/activist inflection

**Code-Switches To**:
- **Academic/political register**: Her comfort zone
- **Caribbean phrases**: Less than Marcus; more analytical about language
- **Spanish occasional**: Has studied, sometimes reaches for it

**Speech Patterns**:
- Precise vocabulary; names things
- Questions others' language use (not judgmentally, but analytically)
- Slightly formal even in casual conversation

**Characteristic Phrases**:
- "That's actually a really important point"
- "Can we talk about how..."
- Uses "y'all" but also "one must consider"

### Jason

**Base**: West Coast American English, tech-influenced

**Code-Switches To**:
- Nothing. He has no Caribbean register.
- This is part of his alienation.

**Speech Patterns**:
- Clean, standardized American
- Tech vocabulary: "optimize," "sync up," "circle back"
- Noticeably no accent; this is conspicuous in the family

**Characteristic Phrases**:
- "That makes sense"
- "I hear you"
- Filler words: "like," "basically"

## TTS Pronunciation Notes

### General Principles

1. **Non-English words should be pronounced correctly**, not Anglicized
2. **Caribbean English has its own rhythm**—not "accented" English but a distinct language variety
3. **Code-switching should sound natural**, not performed
4. **Emotional context affects pronunciation**—stressed speech is less formal

### Specific Guidance

**Trinidadian English**:
- Rhythm is syllable-timed (like Spanish), not stress-timed (like American English)
- Questions rise at end more dramatically than American
- "Th" often becomes "d" in casual speech: "that" → "dat"
- Final consonant clusters simplified: "text" → "tex"

**Hindi words**:
- Retroflex consonants (t, d) are not like English—tongue curls back
- Aspirated consonants (kh, th, ph) have breath after them
- "a" at end of words is often reduced or dropped: "beta" closer to "bet"

**Jamaican Patois**:
- More dramatic intonation than American English
- Vowels are often different: "come" → sounds more like "kom"
- "H" often dropped at word beginning

### Voice Direction by Character

**Nani**: Caribbean English narrator style. Musical rhythm, unhurried. Hindi words pronounced correctly (not Anglicized). When code-switching to Creole, slightly faster, more animated.

**Kamla**: American base with Caribbean breakthrough. The shift should be audible—her standard American gives way to Caribbean rhythm when emotional.

**Michael**: Jamaican patterns audible but not exaggerated. The voice of someone code-switching comfortably.

**Sunil**: Flat American. The absence of Caribbean features is the point.

**Marcus**: Brooklyn register, code-switches consciously. When he uses Caribbean phrases, slight over-articulation—he's reaching for something.

**Maya**: Educated American, slightly precise. When Caribbean vocabulary appears, it's thoughtful, not performance.

**Jason**: West Coast American, tech-neutral. Clean, accent-free. This should feel isolating in the context of the family.

## Cultural Context for Dialogue Choices

### When Characters Speak Standard
- With outsiders
- In formal situations
- When guarded
- When angry and trying to control it

### When Characters Switch to Home Language
- With family (safe space)
- When relaxed
- When emotional (joy, grief, anger)
- When asserting cultural identity
- When excluding outsiders from conversation

### What's Never Translated
Some words have no English equivalent and are not glossed in the text. The reader either learns from context or accepts not knowing:
- **Tabanca**: The specific grief of lost love that remains
- **Bacchanal**: Drama, but more than drama—the specific chaos of communal conflict
- **Maco**: Not just gossip but the act of seeking others' business

These untranslated moments are intentional. The reader experiences, briefly, what it's like to be outside the language.
