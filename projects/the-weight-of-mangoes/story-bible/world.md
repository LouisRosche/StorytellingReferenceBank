# World Building: The Weight of Mangoes

## Cultural Context

### The Indo-Caribbean Diaspora

This story traces a specific, underexplored migration pattern: India to Trinidad to Brooklyn.

**Historical Background**:
- 1838-1917: Following abolition of slavery, British colonial system brought ~144,000 Indians to Trinidad as indentured laborers (called "coolies," now reclaimed)
- Most came from Bihar and eastern Uttar Pradesh; some from South India
- Called "girmitiya" (from "agreement"—the indenture contract)
- Promised return passage after 5 years; most never returned
- Created distinct Indo-Caribbean culture: Hindu/Muslim traditions adapted to Caribbean context

**Cultural Fusion**:
- Hindu practices merged with Caribbean rhythms: Phagwah (Holi) with soca music
- Creole languages absorbed Hindi words: "baigan" (eggplum), "roti," "dal"
- Caste system weakened but didn't disappear
- Inter-generational conflict: children born in Trinidad less "Indian," grandchildren even less

**The Second Migration** (1960s-1980s):
- Trinidad's oil economy fluctuated; many families emigrated to US, UK, Canada
- "Twice migrants"—carrying loss upon loss
- Settled in Brooklyn (Flatbush, Crown Heights), Queens, New Jersey
- Maintained transnational ties: sending money home, returning for weddings/funerals

## Settings

### Primary: Nani's Brownstone, Crown Heights, Brooklyn

**Address**: Fictional, but imagined as Sterling Street near Nostrand

**Physical Description**:
- Three-story brownstone purchased 1985
- Front stoop with iron railings; Nani still scrubs it weekly
- First floor: Living room, dining room, kitchen
- Second floor: Bedrooms (Nani's, Anita's former room, guest room)
- Third floor: Rented to a Haitian family for decades
- Backyard: Small, with curry leaf plant in pot (brought inside for winter), mango tree that never fruits in Brooklyn's climate but Nani keeps it alive

**Sensory Details**:
- Smell: dhalpuri cooking, incense from small puja corner, old wood, bay rum
- Sound: Caribbean radio on low (sometimes soca, sometimes Hindi film songs), traffic on Nostrand, church bells on Sunday
- Touch: Plastic covers on furniture (removed only for company), worn Persian rug, cool porcelain of puja figurines
- Sight: Photographs everywhere—Trinidad, India, grandchildren's graduations; one formal portrait of Nani's parents (colorized, slightly unnatural)

**Puja Corner**:
- Small table in living room corner, facing east
- Brass murtis of Lakshmi, Hanuman, framed photograph of Sai Baba
- Diya (oil lamp), incense holder, small brass bell
- Plastic flowers (Nani says fresh ones attract too many insects now)
- Worn copy of Ramayana in Hindi she can no longer read fluently

### Secondary: Trinidad (In Memory/Flashback)

**Debe** (Nani's birthplace):
- Southern Trinidad, heavily Indo-Trinidadian area
- Sugar cane country, flat, humid
- Hindu temples with brightly painted deities
- Nani's family home (no longer exists): wooden house on stilts, zinc roof, chickens in yard, outdoor latrine
- The river where she bathed as a child; the cinema where she met her husband

**Chaguanas** (Larger town nearby):
- Where family went for supplies, festivals
- Divali decorations remembered as magical
- The mandir where Nani was married

**Port of Spain** (Capital):
- Where the ship docked that brought her grandparents
- Where Nani and her husband flew out from in 1972

### Tertiary: Bihar, India (In Deep Memory)

**The Village** (name fading from memory):
- Nani's grandmother Ajji came from a village near Patna
- Ajji's family were landless agricultural workers
- She was recruited (some say deceived) at age 16; arrived in Trinidad in 1920 at age 17
- The village exists only in story now—Nani has never been to India

**Imagined Details** (from Ajji's stories, possibly embellished):
- Mango orchards (the fruit that means home)
- The well where women gathered
- The landlord's house on the hill
- The recruiter who came with promises

## Cultural Markers

### Food as Memory

**Trinidadian-Indian Fusion**:
- **Doubles**: Fried bara bread with curried channa; Trini street food
- **Roti**: Not Indian roti but Trini dhalpuri (split pea filling) or paratha (buttery layers)
- **Curry**: Trini curry is distinct—influenced by African, Chinese, Indian; green seasoning, scotch bonnet
- **Pelau**: Rice dish with pigeon peas, meat; Caribbean, but Nani's version has Bihari influence
- **Mango**: The central symbol—memory of India, taste of Trinidad, imported expensive to Brooklyn

**What Nani Cooks for the Birthday**:
- Curry chicken (her mother's recipe, scotch bonnet adjusted for American palates)
- Dhalpuri roti (from scratch, takes all morning)
- Callaloo (Dasheen leaves, okra, crab—the Trinidadian way)
- Pholourie (fried dough balls with tamarind chutney)
- Black cake (Trinidadian fruitcake, soaked in rum for months)
- Mango chow (green mango with pepper and chadon beni)
- Sorrel drink (hibiscus, spices—Kamla makes this)

### Religious/Spiritual Practice

**Hinduism, Trinidadian style**:
- Simplified puja adapted for Caribbean life
- Pandit visits for major events; day-to-day practice is personal
- Ramayana central text (Tulsidas Hindi version, though Nani reads it less now)
- Ancestor veneration mixed with Hindu practice
- Obeah (Caribbean folk magic) acknowledged but not practiced by this family; mentioned as something "other people do"

**Nani's Practice**:
- Morning prayers, simplified: lights diya, says mantra, touches feet of murtis
- Observes major festivals: Divali, Phagwah (Holi), Navratri
- Does not impose on children/grandchildren, but notices who participates

### Music

**In the House**:
- Sundar Popo (chutney music legend)
- Classic soca: Lord Kitchener, Mighty Sparrow
- Hindi film songs from 1950s-70s (the soundtrack of Nani's youth)
- Caribbean gospel on Sunday mornings

**At the Party**:
- Mix of generations: chutney soca, reggae, R&B, current stuff the grandchildren play
- The moment Nani sings: old Hindi bhajan, "Raghupati Raghava Raja Ram"

## Brooklyn's Caribbean Community

**The Neighborhood (Crown Heights/Flatbush area)**:
- Major Caribbean immigrant community since 1960s
- Jamaican, Trinidadian, Guyanese, Haitian, Barbadian
- Labor Day: West Indian Day Parade on Eastern Parkway
- Roti shops, Caribbean bakeries, doubles vendors
- Churches and temples sharing blocks

**Nani's World**:
- Knows every Trinidadian within ten blocks
- Walks to the roti shop on Nostrand; owner is from San Fernando
- Hindu temple on Lefferts: attends for Divali, other times too far (she's slowing down)
- Community has changed: gentrification bringing new people, old neighbors dying or moving to Florida

**Generational Difference**:
- Nani: Brooklyn is where she landed, but Trinidad is home
- Kamla: Brooklyn is home, Trinidad is where she visits
- Marcus/Maya: Brooklyn is home, Trinidad is where Nani is from
