[NARRATOR]
The bed was still unmade. Eleanor stood in the doorway of her mother's room, watching the early light crawl across the quilt Ruth had sewn forty years ago—patches of old dresses, a curtain from the kitchen on Maple Street, a square of fabric Eleanor recognized as her father's favorite shirt. The pillow still held the impression of her mother's head, a shallow valley in the white cotton that seemed obscene in its intimacy.

She could not bring herself to cross the threshold.

Two weeks. Fourteen days since the stroke that felled Ruth Callahan like a tree in a storm—sudden, violent, irrevocable. Twelve days in the hospital watching machines breathe for her mother. Two days in hospice, holding a hand that no longer squeezed back. And now this: the house, the silence, the goddamn pillow.

[ELEANOR]
"I should strip the bed."

[NARRATOR]
She said it aloud, as if speaking would make her body obey. It didn't. Her feet remained fixed to the hallway floor, her hand gripping the doorframe until her knuckles whitened.

The house in Port Clyde had been in the family for three generations. Eleanor had learned to walk in this hallway, had hidden in these closets during thunderstorms, had snuck boys through the back door when she was sixteen. She knew every creak in the floorboards, every draft from the windows, every water stain on the ceiling that her father had promised to fix and never did. But standing here now, she felt like an intruder.

She was sixty-five years old, and she had never felt more like a child.

The sound of tires on gravel pulled her from the doorway. She moved to the window at the end of the hall—the one that looked out over the driveway and, beyond it, the gray chop of the Atlantic.

A rental car. Silver. Washington plates.

[ELEANOR]
"Christ. He's early."

[NARRATOR]
Marcus wasn't supposed to arrive until noon. Eleanor had planned for noon. She had planned to have the house presentable, the coffee made, her composure intact. She had planned to greet him from a position of strength, the capable sister who had handled everything, who deserved acknowledgment for handling everything.

Instead, she was still in yesterday's clothes, her hair unwashed, her mother's bedroom a shrine she couldn't enter.

She heard the car door slam. Heard footsteps on the porch. Heard three knocks—not the rhythm of someone who lived here once, but the rhythm of a guest.

Eleanor descended the stairs slowly, giving herself time to arrange her face into something neutral. By the time she reached the front door, she had almost succeeded.

[MARCUS]
"Elle."

[NARRATOR]
He looked older. She always thought that when she saw him—which was rarely now, once every two or three years. His hair had gone fully gray at the temples, and he'd grown a beard that made him look like their father in the photographs from the seventies. He was wearing a jacket too light for Maine in October, because Marcus had never understood weather that wasn't Seattle's particular brand of damp.

[ELEANOR]
"You're early."

[MARCUS]
"Flight got in ahead of schedule. I thought I'd come straight here."

[ELEANOR]
"You could have called."

[MARCUS]
"I did. Three times."

[NARRATOR]
Eleanor remembered, vaguely, her phone buzzing in the pocket of her coat. She had ignored it. She had been ignoring everything.

[ELEANOR]
"I was busy."

[NARRATOR]
Marcus nodded slowly, the way he always did when he was deciding not to argue. He stepped past her into the house, and Eleanor caught the scent of airplane, of recycled air and exhaustion.

[MARCUS]
"It smells the same."

[ELEANOR]
"What?"

[MARCUS]
"The house. It smells exactly the same. Lavender and salt and that weird mustiness from the basement."

[ELEANOR]
"It's a ninety-year-old house, Marcus. It's going to smell."

[NARRATOR]
He walked into the living room, his eyes moving across the furniture, the photographs on the mantle, the stack of sympathy cards Eleanor hadn't had the energy to open. He paused at the window that faced the water.

[MARCUS]
"When's Helen getting in?"

[ELEANOR]
"Four. She's driving down from Burlington. Sophie's with her."

[MARCUS]
"Sophie's coming?"

[ELEANOR]
"Ruth was her grandmother too."

[MARCUS]
"I know. I just—I haven't seen Sophie since she was in high school."

[ELEANOR]
"She's twenty-five now. Has a job. An apartment. A life you might know about if you ever visited."

[NARRATOR]
The words came out sharper than she intended. Or maybe exactly as sharp as she intended—she couldn't tell anymore.

Marcus turned from the window. His face was carefully blank, the expression of a man who had trained himself not to react to his family.

[MARCUS]
"I'm here now, Eleanor."

[ELEANOR]
"You're here now."

[NARRATOR]
They stood in silence for a long moment. Outside, a lobster boat chugged past the point, its engine a low thrum that Eleanor had heard every morning of her childhood.

[MARCUS]
"Have you started going through her things?"

[ELEANOR]
"Some. The closet. I found—"

[NARRATOR]
She stopped. The locked box. She hadn't told anyone about the locked box.

[MARCUS]
"Found what?"

[ELEANOR]
"Nothing. Just old clothes. You know how she was. Never threw anything away."

[MARCUS]
"That's not nothing. That's a project. Do you want help? I can start in the attic, or—"

[ELEANOR]
"No."

[NARRATOR]
The word came out too fast, too forceful. Marcus raised an eyebrow.

[ELEANOR]
"I mean, not yet. We should wait for Helen. Do it together."

[MARCUS]
"Since when do the three of us do anything together?"

[NARRATOR]
He had a point. The Callahan siblings had not operated as a unit in decades—not since their father's death in 1998, when the fault lines between them had cracked into canyons. Eleanor the eldest, the responsible one, who had stayed in Maine and married Ray and built a life within driving distance of their mother. Marcus the middle child, the escapist, who had fled to the West Coast at eighteen and returned only for funerals and the occasional Thanksgiving. Helen the baby, the artist, who had drifted through Vermont and New York and Paris, always searching for something their family couldn't provide.

They loved each other. Eleanor was fairly sure of that. But love and like were different things, and love and understanding were different still.

[ELEANOR]
"There's coffee. In the kitchen. If you want."

[MARCUS]
"Thanks."

[NARRATOR]
She watched him walk toward the kitchen, his gait slightly uneven—an old injury from a hiking accident she'd heard about secondhand. When had her brother become a stranger? When had any of them become strangers to each other?

She thought about the locked box, sitting on her mother's dresser where she'd left it last night. Heavy wood, brass fittings, no key. Something shifted inside when she tilted it.

She had told herself she would wait. Wait for her siblings. Wait for the right moment. But the right moment never came, and the box sat there, refusing to be ignored.

Eleanor climbed the stairs again. This time, she crossed the threshold.

The room smelled like her mother—that particular combination of lavender lotion and wool and something indefinably Ruth. Eleanor breathed it in, let it fill her lungs, tried to memorize it before it faded.

The box was where she'd left it, beside the jewelry case that held her mother's pearls and the watch her father had given Ruth for their twenty-fifth anniversary. It was old, clearly—the wood dark with age, the brass tarnished. Eleanor didn't recognize it, which was strange. She had grown up in this house. She had helped her mother organize every drawer, every closet, every corner. She should know every object.

But she had never seen this box before.

She picked it up again, felt the weight of it. Something definitely moved inside—paper, maybe, or photographs. A small sound, a whisper of shifting.

There was a keyhole, but no key. Eleanor had searched the dresser, the nightstand, the pockets of her mother's coats. Nothing fit.

[MARCUS]
"Elle?"

[NARRATOR]
His voice came from the bottom of the stairs. Eleanor set the box down quickly, as if she'd been caught doing something wrong.

[ELEANOR]
"Coming."

[NARRATOR]
She descended to find Marcus holding two mugs of coffee, steam rising from both. He handed her one without a word.

[MARCUS]
"Black, no sugar. You still take it the same?"

[ELEANOR]
"Some things don't change."

[NARRATOR]
They sat in the living room, in the chairs that had been their parents' chairs, looking out at the water. The silence was almost companionable. Almost.

[MARCUS]
"I'm sorry I wasn't here."

[NARRATOR]
Eleanor didn't respond. She didn't know how to respond. Sorry for what? For missing the two weeks of hospital vigil? For missing the last three years of their mother's life? For missing everything?

[MARCUS]
"I know it fell on you. The arrangements, the—everything. I should have come sooner."

[ELEANOR]
"Yes. You should have."

[MARCUS]
"Elle—"

[ELEANOR]
"What do you want me to say, Marcus? That it's fine? It's not fine. Nothing about this is fine."

[NARRATOR]
She set her mug down too hard, and coffee sloshed over the rim onto the side table. Their mother's side table. The one Ruth had inherited from her own mother.

Eleanor stood to get a cloth, and when she came back, Marcus was looking at her with an expression she couldn't read.

[MARCUS]
"You look exhausted."

[ELEANOR]
"I am exhausted. I've been exhausted for two weeks. I've been exhausted for years."

[MARCUS]
"Eleanor—"

[ELEANOR]
"Do you know what it's like? Being the one who's here? The one who gets the calls in the middle of the night, the one who drives her to doctors' appointments, the one who holds her hand in the hospital while she's dying?"

[NARRATOR]
Her voice cracked on the last word. She hadn't meant to say any of this. She had meant to be composed, controlled, capable. The responsible one.

[MARCUS]
"I don't know. You're right. I don't."

[ELEANOR]
"Then don't tell me you're sorry. Sorry is easy. Sorry costs nothing."

[NARRATOR]
Marcus set down his own mug. When he spoke, his voice was quieter than she'd expected.

[MARCUS]
"What do you want from me, Elle? I can't change the past. I can't un-leave. I made choices you disagree with, and I have to live with that. But I'm here now. Tell me what you need, and I'll do it."

[NARRATOR]
Eleanor stared at him. This was not the Marcus she remembered—the sarcastic, defensive brother who deflected everything with irony. This was someone older, maybe wiser, certainly more tired.

[ELEANOR]
"I don't know what I need."

[NARRATOR]
It was the most honest thing she'd said in weeks.

The crunch of tires on gravel interrupted them. Eleanor checked her watch—2:30. Helen was early too, it seemed. This family and its inability to follow a schedule.

But when she looked out the window, it wasn't Helen's SUV in the driveway. It was a Buick, old and well-maintained, with a clergy sticker on the bumper.

[ELEANOR]
"Pastor Oakes."

[MARCUS]
"Who?"

[ELEANOR]
"From the church. Mother's church. He's here to discuss the service."

[NARRATOR]
She opened the door before he could knock. David Oakes was seventy-three, with a shock of white hair and the kind of face that had been hearing confessions for fifty years. He had baptized all three Callahan children. He had married Eleanor and Ray. He had buried their father.

[PASTOR OAKES]
"Eleanor, my dear."

[NARRATOR]
He embraced her, his arms surprisingly strong for a man his age. Eleanor let herself be held for a moment, then stepped back.

[ELEANOR]
"Thank you for coming, Pastor. This is my brother Marcus. He's come from Seattle."

[PASTOR OAKES]
"Marcus. I remember you as a boy. You had a terrible singing voice."

[MARCUS]
"Still do."

[NARRATOR]
The pastor chuckled and settled into the chair Eleanor offered. She brought him tea—he didn't drink coffee—and the three of them began to discuss the service. Readings. Hymns. The order of eulogies.

It was the kind of administrative grief that Eleanor understood: boxes to check, decisions to make, a structure to impose on chaos. She felt herself relaxing into it, into the familiar role of organizer, of manager.

And then Pastor Oakes said something that changed everything.

[PASTOR OAKES]
"Given what Ruth carried all these years, I imagine she's at peace now."

[NARRATOR]
Eleanor's pen stopped moving. Beside her, she felt Marcus go still.

[ELEANOR]
"I'm sorry?"

[PASTOR OAKES]
"The burden she bore. The secret. I know she found it difficult, especially toward the end."

[NARRATOR]
The room was very quiet. The lobster boat had passed. Even the gulls seemed to have fallen silent.

[MARCUS]
"What secret?"

[NARRATOR]
Pastor Oakes looked between them, and Eleanor watched his face change as he realized something had gone terribly wrong.

[PASTOR OAKES]
"I assumed—I thought surely she had told you. Before she—"

[ELEANOR]
"Told us what?"

[NARRATOR]
The old man's hands were trembling now. He set down his teacup with exaggerated care.

[PASTOR OAKES]
"The baby. I thought you knew about the baby."

[NARRATOR]
Eleanor heard the words, but they didn't make sense. She arranged them in her mind and rearranged them, but they refused to form a coherent picture.

[MARCUS]
"What baby?"

[PASTOR OAKES]
"In 1955. Before your father. Ruth—Ruth had a child. A daughter. She gave her up for adoption."

[NARRATOR]
The silence that followed was absolute. Eleanor felt something in her chest—a tightening, a constriction, as if her heart was being squeezed by a fist.

Her mother. Her capable, proper, secretive mother. A child. A daughter no one knew about.

A sister.

[ELEANOR]
"That's not possible."

[PASTOR OAKES]
"Eleanor, I'm so sorry. I thought—she told me years ago, in confession. She said she was going to tell you. I assumed—"

[MARCUS]
"You assumed wrong."

[NARRATOR]
His voice was flat, controlled, but Eleanor could hear the tremor beneath it.

She looked at the room—her mother's room, her mother's house, her mother's life—and saw it differently now. Every photograph was a question. Every silence was a lie.

The locked box upstairs. The box she couldn't open.

Eleanor stood so quickly her chair nearly toppled.

[ELEANOR]
"I need to show you something."

[NARRATOR]
She didn't wait for them to follow. She climbed the stairs two at a time, her heart pounding in her ears, and retrieved the box from her mother's dresser.

When she returned, Pastor Oakes had his head in his hands. Marcus was standing at the window, his back to the room.

Eleanor set the box on the coffee table. It made a heavy sound, final and damning.

[ELEANOR]
"I found this in her closet. I can't open it. There's no key."

[NARRATOR]
Marcus turned. He looked at the box for a long moment.

[MARCUS]
"Then we force it open."

[ELEANOR]
"Marcus—"

[MARCUS]
"What? She's dead, Eleanor. She doesn't get to have secrets anymore."

[NARRATOR]
And there it was—the anger she'd been suppressing for two weeks, maybe for years. Eleanor felt it rise in her chest like bile.

But before she could speak, they heard the front door open, and Helen's voice carried through the house.

[HELEN]
"Hello? Elle? We're here early, the traffic was—"

[NARRATOR]
She stopped in the doorway of the living room, Sophie behind her. Helen was fifty-two, the youngest, with their mother's cheekbones and their father's restless eyes. She looked between them—Eleanor flushed with fury, Marcus rigid at the window, Pastor Oakes collapsed in his chair—and her face went pale.

[HELEN]
"What happened? What's wrong?"

[NARRATOR]
Eleanor opened her mouth, but no words came. How do you say it? How do you tell your sister that your mother had another daughter, a secret daughter, a life none of them knew about?

It was Pastor Oakes who spoke, his voice cracked and ancient.

[PASTOR OAKES]
"I'm afraid I've made a terrible mistake."

[NARRATOR]
Helen stepped into the room. Sophie followed, her young face confused and frightened. Outside, the tide was beginning to turn, the water shifting from slack to ebb.

And on the coffee table, the locked box sat in silence, holding its secrets a little longer.

[HELEN]
"Will someone please tell me what's going on?"

[NARRATOR]
Eleanor looked at her sister—her known sister, her present sister—and felt the ground beneath her feet begin to shift.

[ELEANOR]
"Sit down, Helen. You're going to need to sit down."

[NARRATOR]
The afternoon light was fading now, the gray sky pressing closer. In a few hours, it would be dark. In a few hours, everything they thought they knew about their mother, about their family, about themselves, would be different.

But for now, there was only this: a locked box, a secret kept, and the long shadow of a woman who had died with the truth still inside her.
