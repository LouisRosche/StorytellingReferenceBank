# Cross-Curricular Story Matrix

How to plan a scientist story that serves multiple curricular standards simultaneously. The most powerful stories don't check one subject's boxes — they work at the intersection where science, history, language, mathematics, and civic life meet.

All standards codes reference:
- `@references/ngss-standards-5-12.md` — Science (NGSS/MLS-S)
- `@references/mls-ela-5-12.md` — English Language Arts (MLS-ELA)
- `@references/mls-social-studies-5-12.md` — Social Studies (MLS-SS)
- `@references/mls-math-5-12.md` — Mathematics (MLS-Math)
- `@references/mls-arts-health-languages-5-12.md` — Fine Arts, Health, World Languages

---

## Why Cross-Curricular Matters for a Charter School

A 5–12 charter school with limited staff and schedule is already under pressure to serve every standard with limited time. A story that simultaneously addresses science content, literacy skills, historical context, and civic reasoning is not a luxury — it's an efficiency.

More important: the real world doesn't organize itself into subject areas. Scientists work inside histories, economies, civic structures, and languages. A story that pretends otherwise is false — and students know it.

The matrix below is a planning tool, not a constraint. Not every story must hit every column. But if a story is serving only one subject, ask why. The omission is usually a storytelling failure, not a curricular one.

## The Cross-Curricular Planning Matrix

To use this matrix: read down the column for your target scientist, then find which standards naturally appear. You're not forcing standards in — you're recognizing that they were always there.

### Scientist: **Eunice Newton Foote** (1819–1888)

*First person to describe the greenhouse effect.*

| Subject | Standards | Story Integration |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Science** | MS-ESS2-6 (human activity → temperature), HS-ESS2-4 (climate change), HS-PS3-4 (thermal energy) | The experiment itself: filling glass cylinders with different gases, measuring temperature in sunlight |
| **ELA** | RI._.6 (author's purpose, point of view), RI._.9 (compare accounts), W._.7 (research), RL._.6 (point of view shapes content) | Foote's 1856 paper vs. Tyndall's 1859 paper: two accounts, one discovery, one name attached |
| **Social Studies** | H.1.B (primary sources, perspective), H.7.5 (Enlightenment, rights), CG.1.D (women's rights), G.3.D (climate geography) | 1856: women cannot present their own papers to the AAAS; who speaks for whom |
| **Math** | N.Q (units of measurement), S.ID.1–4 (data from temperature readings), F.LE.3 (exponential growth of CO₂) | Temperature measurements; what data Foote gathered and how she analyzed it |
| **Fine Arts / WL** | FA-VA.Cr2 (scientific illustration as art); RI context for understanding German-language Tyndall reception | — |

**Dramatic question**: Why was Foote's name erased from the history of climate science for 150 years? Who benefits when a discoverer is forgotten?

**Grade band**: 6–8 primary; adaptable to 9–12 for deeper treatment of climate policy and gender in science history.

### Scientist: **John Snow** (1813–1858)

*Physician who mapped the Broad Street cholera outbreak (London, 1854).*

| Subject | Standards | Story Integration |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Science** | MS-LS1-2 (body systems — disease disrupts), HS-LS1-1 (homeostasis, disease), MS-ESS3-3 (water as human health factor) | What cholera does to the body; how Snow traced the source before germ theory was established |
| **ELA** | RI._.3 (how events, ideas interact), RI._.7 (integrate information — map + text + data), RI._.8 (evaluate Snow's argument), RST._.7 (quantitative information integrated with text) | Snow's original 1855 report as a primary source; reading a dot map as text |
| **Social Studies** | G.1.A (geographic tools — maps), G.3.B (human activity and environmental consequence), H.7.6 (Industrial Revolution — urbanization, water systems) | What industrialization did to London's water supply; cholera as a disease of poverty and density |
| **Math** | S.ID.6 (scatter plots — cholera cases mapped), S.IC (statistical inference from the pump handle removal), G.GPE (spatial analysis on the coordinate plane — map as coordinate system) | The map itself; counting cases by street; proving it was the pump by counting cases around it |
| **Health** | H.1 (health concepts — germ theory), H.3 (valid health information — Snow's methodology), H.8 (health advocacy — persuading city officials) | The argument Snow had to make to get the pump handle removed |
| **World Languages** | Context: the medical establishment was in France and Germany; miasma theory had French and German scientific backing — WL.CO.2 (accessing viewpoints available only through the language) | — |

**Dramatic question**: How do you prove something is true when your entire field has already decided what the answer is?

**STL connection**: The Mississippi River and cholera epidemics hit St. Louis in 1849, 1866; city water supply debates parallel London's. The STL Water Tower (1871) was built partly in response to waterborne disease. This story has a direct local echo.

**Grade band**: Grades 7–9; ideal cross-curricular unit with geography and data literacy.

## Cross-Curricular Story Design Principles

### 1. Let the History Be Load-Bearing

Social studies isn't background decoration. The historical moment is the force that makes the science matter. A scientist working in a vacuum is neither realistic nor dramatic. Ask:
- What political event created the conditions for this research?
- What economic structure funded or denied the work?
- What geographic place shaped what the scientist could observe?

If those questions don't affect the plot, the social studies integration is cosmetic.

---

### 2. Let the Math Be Visible

If the scientist uses math, show the math — not to teach it, but to reveal character. The scientist who stares at a number and sees its implications is a different person from the scientist who can't stand the imprecision. Specific numbers, specific equations, specific errors create specificity.

The math should appear the way any craft or skill appears in literary fiction — as a window into the character's way of inhabiting the world.

---

### 3. Let the ELA Standards Diagnose Narrative Failure

When a scientist story isn't working, ELA standards often identify the failure:
- Story has no point of view → RL._.6 failure
- All claims are stated, none shown → RST._.7 failure (not integrating quantitative information)
- The argument has no counterargument → RI._.8 failure
- The theme is stated, not dramatized → RL._.2 failure

Reading ELA standards backward, as a diagnostic, locates precisely where the story needs work.

---

### 4. Fine Arts Integration Is Not Optional for a Full Charter Curriculum

Many charter schools have limited Fine Arts staffing. Cross-curricular stories that explicitly address Fine Arts standards (FA-VA.Cr2, FA-Th.Cr2, FA-Mu.Cn11) support that instruction rather than requiring it separately. A story about Ramón y Cajal serves biology *and* visual art. A story performed as reader's theater serves theatre and science and ELA simultaneously.

---

### 5. World Languages Create Authentic Motivation

The most powerful motivation for learning a language is needing it to access something specific. A science story in which knowledge is locked in another language (Tu Youyou's classical Chinese medical text; Einstein's German papers; Ibn al-Haytham's Arabic optics) creates authentic motivation for language study. WL.CO.2 — accessing viewpoints available only through the language — is a direct invitation to build this into scientist stories.

**Column definitions**:
- **Primary Standards**: The 1–3 standards that the story *directly serves*
- **Evidence in Story**: The specific scene, character choice, or story element that addresses the standard

A scientist story that leaves most cells empty is not failing — it may be doing one thing exceptionally well. But seeing the empty cells is itself useful: it shows what the unit's complementary activities need to provide.
