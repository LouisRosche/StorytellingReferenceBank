# Missouri Learning Standards — Fine Arts, Health/PE, and World Languages (Grades 5–12)

These standards apply to all Missouri public and charter schools. For scientist story planning, Fine Arts and World Languages have the most direct connection points. Health/PE standards surface in stories about scientists who work in medicine, public health, and human performance.

---

## Fine Arts

Missouri's Fine Arts standards cover four disciplines: **Visual Art**, **Music**, **Theatre**, and **Dance**. All four are organized around the same four artistic processes:

| Process | Description | Parallel to Science Practices |
|---------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| **Creating** | Conceiving, developing, and producing artistic work | SEP 2 (Developing Models), SEP 6 (Constructing Explanations) |
| **Performing/Presenting/Producing** | Realizing artistic work | SEP 3 (Carrying Out Investigations) |
| **Responding** | Understanding and evaluating artistic work | SEP 4 (Analyzing Data), SEP 7 (Argument from Evidence) |
| **Connecting** | Relating artistic ideas to personal meaning and external context | SEP 8 (Communicating Information) |

The parallel structure is not coincidental. The National Core Arts Standards were designed with the same philosophical foundation as NGSS — practices-based, inquiry-centered.

## Health and Physical Education

Missouri's Health and PE standards are organized around two frameworks:

### Health Standards (MO-Health)

Based on the National Health Education Standards (NHES):

| Standard | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| H.1 | Students comprehend health-promoting concepts to enhance health |
| H.2 | Students analyze the influence of family, peers, culture, media, technology, and other factors on health behaviors |
| H.3 | Students demonstrate the ability to access valid health information and health-promoting products/services |
| H.4 | Students demonstrate the ability to use interpersonal communication skills to enhance health |
| H.5 | Students demonstrate the ability to use decision-making skills to enhance health |
| H.6 | Students demonstrate the ability to use goal-setting skills to enhance health |
| H.7 | Students practice health-enhancing behaviors and avoid or reduce health risks |
| H.8 | Students demonstrate the ability to advocate for personal, family, and community health |

**Health content areas** (by grade band):

| Content Area | Grades 5–8 Focus | Grades 9–12 Focus |
|-------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Nutrition | Nutrients, portion, eating patterns | Chronic disease prevention, eating disorders |
| Physical activity | Benefits, FITT principle | Lifetime fitness; sports medicine |
| Mental health | Stress, coping, emotions | Mental illness literacy; help-seeking |
| Substance use | Tobacco, alcohol, drugs — effects | Addiction; opioid epidemic |
| Sexual health | Puberty, reproduction | STIs, contraception, consent, relationships |
| Injury prevention | First aid, fire safety | Seat belts, sports injury, violence prevention |
| Community/environmental health | How environment affects health | Environmental health science; health disparities |

**Health standards most relevant to scientist stories**:
- H.1 (health concepts): stories about scientists who discovered health-related knowledge
- H.2 (influences on health behavior): environmental and social determinants of health
- H.3 (valid health information): how to evaluate health claims — connects to SEP 7 and RST._.8
- H.8 (health advocacy): scientists who advocate for public health policy

## World Languages

Missouri's World Languages standards are based on the ACTFL (American Council on the Teaching of Foreign Languages) framework, organized around five goal areas:

| Goal | Code | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| Communication | WL.CM | Use the language to communicate in meaningful contexts |
| Cultures | WL.CU | Gain knowledge and understanding of other cultures |
| Connections | WL.CO | Connect with other disciplines and acquire information |
| Comparisons | WL.CP | Develop insight into the nature of language and culture |
| Communities | WL.CM2 | Participate in multilingual communities at home and around the world |

### Communication Modes

| Mode | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Interpersonal | Two-way, real-time communication (speaking, writing to a known audience) |
| Interpretive | One-way, receptive communication (reading, listening, viewing) |
| Presentational | One-way, expressive communication (speaking or writing to an audience) |

### Proficiency Levels (ACTFL)

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Novice | Isolated words and phrases; highly familiar topics |
| Intermediate | Simple sentences; predictable situations; present time |
| Advanced | Extended discourse; narrate and describe in paragraph-length speech; past, present, future |
| Superior | Discussion of abstract topics; hypothetical; professional topics |

Typical grade-band proficiency targets for a 5–12 school (beginning language instruction in grade 5 or 6):

| Grade | Approximate Target |
|-------|-------------------|
| Grade 5–6 | Novice Mid/High |
| Grade 7–8 | Intermediate Low/Mid |
| Grade 9–10 | Intermediate Mid/High |
| Grade 11–12 | Advanced Low (heritage speakers: Advanced High) |

## Fine Arts, Health, and World Languages × Scientist Story Alignment

### Fine Arts Connection Points

| Story Element | Fine Arts Standard |
|---------------|--------------------|
| Scientist's handwritten notebooks / diagrams | FA-VA.Cr2, FA-VA.Re7 — observational drawing as scientific method |
| Scientific illustration (botany, anatomy) | FA-VA.Pr5 — technique in service of precise representation |
| Science and music (acoustics, Pythagoras) | FA-Mu.Cn11 — historical connection; math and sound |
| Theatrical presentation of a scientist's argument | FA-Th.Pr4–6 — character, motivation, performance |
| Scientific visualization as art (space photos, microscopy) | FA-VA.Re8, FA-VA.Cn11 — interpretation and meaning |
| Historical context of scientific images (propaganda posters, WWII) | FA-VA.Cn11, FA-Th.Cn11 |

**Notable scientist-artist connections**:
- **Santiago Ramón y Cajal** — Nobel Prize neurologist who created stunning ink drawings of brain cells; his scientific illustrations are collected in fine art museums. A story about Cajal hits HS-LS1-1 (cell structure) and FA-VA.Cr2 simultaneously.
- **Maria Sibylla Merian** (1647–1717) — German naturalist/artist who illustrated insects and plants in Suriname; her illustrations were the first scientific documentation of insect metamorphosis. Hits MS-LS1 and MS-LS2 standards; FA-VA.Cr2 and FA-VA.Cn11.
- **Ernst Haeckel** — created biologically detailed (if sometimes inaccurate) illustrations that shaped how the public understood Darwin's evolution.

### Health Standards Connection Points

| Story | Health Standard |
|-------|----------------|
| John Snow (cholera map, 1854) | H.1 (health concepts); H.3 (valid information); H.8 (advocacy) |
| Henrietta Lacks (HeLa cells, informed consent) | H.3 (valid health information and rights); H.2 (social determinants) |
| Ignaz Semmelweis (handwashing, 1840s) | H.1; H.7 (health-enhancing behaviors) |
| Rachel Carson (DDT, environmental health) | H.2 (environment affects health); H.8 (advocacy) |
| Rebecca Lee Crumpler (medicine for freed people) | H.2 (social determinants); H.8 (advocacy) |
| Alice Hamilton (industrial medicine) | H.2 (occupational health); H.8 (advocacy) |

**Alice Hamilton** (1869–1970) — American physician who founded the field of industrial medicine. She documented lead poisoning, mercury poisoning, and other occupational hazards in early 20th-century factories. Her work connects: HS-PS1-7 (lead, toxic heavy metals), H.2 (environment and health), H.8 (health advocacy), CG.2.B (influencing policy), E.11.7 (government regulation of industry).

### World Languages Connection Points

| Language | Scientist Story Opportunity |
|----------|----------------------------|
| Spanish | Ynés Mexía (Mexican-American botanist); WL.CM.2 (interpret scientific text in Spanish) |
| French | Marie Curie worked in French; Louis Pasteur; Hélène Langevin-Joliot |
| German | Lise Meitner; Max Planck; Albert Einstein; WL.CO.2 (access to German-language primary sources) |
| Arabic | Ibn al-Haytham (optics, 1000 CE — first person to articulate the scientific method); Al-Biruni |
| Mandarin | Tu Youyou (Nobel Prize 2015, artemisinin for malaria — worked in China during Cultural Revolution) |
| Swahili / French | Wangari Maathai; connects to WL.CM2.1 (communities of practice) |

**WL.CO.1 and WL.CO.2 are particularly powerful** for scientist stories: the claim that important scientific knowledge exists *only* in another language is historically true. Much of the Islamic Golden Age's contributions to optics, algebra, and medicine were available in Arabic and Persian before translation. Many 20th-century physics papers were in German. Understanding this motivates language learning as access, not just communication.
