# Missouri Learning Standards — English Language Arts (Grades 5–12)

Missouri's ELA standards (MLS-ELA) are substantially identical to the Common Core State Standards for ELA/Literacy, with minor Missouri-specific adjustments. They apply to all Missouri public and charter schools.

This document covers all strands across grades 5–12 with notes on how each maps to scientist story planning and production.

---

## Overview of Strands

| Strand | Code | Scope |
|--------|------|-------|
| Reading: Literature | RL | Narrative, poetry, drama — fictional and literary nonfiction |
| Reading: Informational Text | RI | Nonfiction, primary sources, exposition, argument |
| Writing | W | Argument, informational/explanatory, narrative |
| Speaking and Listening | SL | Discussion, presentation, media |
| Language | L | Conventions, knowledge of language, vocabulary |

**For grades 6–12**, the standards also include a separate strand:
| Literacy in History/Social Studies, Science, and Technical Subjects | RH/RST/WHST | Reading and writing in disciplinary contexts |

---

## The Anchor Standards

These apply across all grade bands and are the foundation each grade-specific standard builds toward.

### Reading Anchor Standards

**Key Ideas and Details**
- CCRA.R.1 — Read closely; cite textual evidence; make logical inferences
- CCRA.R.2 — Determine central ideas/themes; summarize
- CCRA.R.3 — Analyze how characters, events, or ideas develop and interact

**Craft and Structure**
- CCRA.R.4 — Interpret words and phrases; technical, connotative, figurative meanings
- CCRA.R.5 — Analyze structure of texts
- CCRA.R.6 — Assess point of view, purpose, and how they shape content

**Integration of Knowledge and Ideas**
- CCRA.R.7 — Integrate content across text, image, audio, media
- CCRA.R.8 — Evaluate the argument and evidence in a text (literature: not applicable at anchor level)
- CCRA.R.9 — Analyze how multiple texts address similar themes or topics

**Range of Reading**
- CCRA.R.10 — Read complex texts independently and proficiently

---

### Writing Anchor Standards

**Text Types and Purposes**
- CCRA.W.1 — Write arguments supported by evidence
- CCRA.W.2 — Write informative/explanatory texts
- CCRA.W.3 — Write narratives using effective techniques

**Production and Distribution**
- CCRA.W.4 — Produce clear writing appropriate to task, purpose, audience
- CCRA.W.5 — Develop and strengthen writing through planning, revising, editing
- CCRA.W.6 — Use technology to produce and publish writing

**Research to Build Knowledge**
- CCRA.W.7 — Conduct short and sustained research projects
- CCRA.W.8 — Gather relevant information; avoid plagiarism; integrate sources
- CCRA.W.9 — Draw evidence from texts to support analysis and research

**Range of Writing**
- CCRA.W.10 — Write routinely over extended and shorter time frames

---

### Speaking and Listening Anchor Standards

**Comprehension and Collaboration**
- CCRA.SL.1 — Engage in collaborative discussions; build on others' ideas
- CCRA.SL.2 — Integrate multiple information sources presented in diverse formats
- CCRA.SL.3 — Evaluate a speaker's point of view, reasoning, and evidence

**Presentation**
- CCRA.SL.4 — Present information, findings, and evidence clearly
- CCRA.SL.5 — Use digital media strategically to enhance presentations
- CCRA.SL.6 — Adapt speech to context; demonstrate command of formal English

---

### Language Anchor Standards

**Conventions**
- CCRA.L.1 — Demonstrate command of grammar and usage
- CCRA.L.2 — Demonstrate command of capitalization, punctuation, spelling

**Knowledge of Language**
- CCRA.L.3 — Apply knowledge of language to understand effects of word choices

**Vocabulary**
- CCRA.L.4 — Determine meanings of unknown words using context and morphology
- CCRA.L.5 — Understand figurative language, word relationships, nuance
- CCRA.L.6 — Acquire and use academic and domain-specific vocabulary

## ELA Standards as Story-Planning Tools

### What ELA Standards Demand of a Scientist Story

A scientist story can directly serve ELA standards as a *text students read* and as a *model for writing*. Both uses require the story to meet different bars.

**As a text students read** (RL and RI standards):

The story must be complex enough to reward close reading (RL._.1, RI._.1). This means:
- Ambiguity that can't be resolved in a single reading
- Textual evidence that supports competing interpretations
- Word choices whose connotations matter (RL._.4, RI._.4)
- A point of view or narrator whose perspective shapes what is visible (RL._.6, RI._.6)

**As a model for student writing** (W standards):

The story should model techniques students are asked to deploy:
- Narrative technique (W._.3): dialogue, pacing, description, reflection, show don't tell
- Evidence-based argument (W._.1): if the scientist story argues a thesis, it should model how claims are supported
- Informative writing (W._.2): the story can model how domain-specific vocabulary is introduced in context

**For speaking and listening** (SL standards):

Scientist stories structured around contested discoveries are ideal for structured academic controversy (SL._.1, SL._.3) — students take on the role of the scientist's critics or defenders.

### Text Complexity and Grade-Band Calibration

Missouri ELA standards specify that texts must meet grade-band complexity benchmarks (RL._.10, RI._.10). For scientist stories:

| Grade Band | Qualitative Complexity | Quantitative |
|------------|----------------------|--------------|
| Grade 5 | Clear purpose; mostly familiar situations; some complex ideas | Lexile 770–980 |
| Grades 6–8 | More complex structure; multiple perspectives; abstract concepts | Lexile 955–1155 |
| Grades 9–10 | Multiple levels of meaning; complex characters; irony | Lexile 1080–1305 |
| Grades 11–12 | Highly complex themes; ambiguous purpose; mature content | Lexile 1215–1355 |

Scientist stories for grades 11–12 can hold complexity equivalent to published literary nonfiction — Rebecca Skloot, Tracy Kidder, Annie Dillard.

## Missouri-Specific ELA Notes

Missouri's MLS-ELA retains the full CCSS ELA structure with these additions/emphases:

- **Missouri authors and texts**: Missouri encourages inclusion of Missouri-connected authors. Relevant to scientist stories: George Washington Carver's writing, Mark Twain's nonfiction essays, Langston Hughes (raised in Joplin and Kansas City).
- **Informational text emphasis**: Missouri explicitly requires 50% informational text in grades 5–8, 70% in grades 9–12 (across all subjects, not just ELA class). Scientist stories as informational/literary nonfiction hybrid serve this directly.
- **Speaking and Listening formality**: Missouri emphasizes code-switching and awareness of formal vs. informal registers — relevant for scientist stories where characters move between community language and scientific register.
