# Missouri Learning Standards — Mathematics (Grades 5–12)

Missouri's Math standards (MLS-Math) are substantially aligned to the Common Core State Standards for Mathematics (CCSSM) with Missouri-specific modifications. They apply to all Missouri public and charter schools.

This reference covers the **Standards for Mathematical Practice** (which apply at every grade level) and the **content standards** by grade band. For scientist story planning, the Practices are the most important — they describe how mathematicians and scientists *think*, and they parallel the NGSS Science and Engineering Practices.

---

## Standards for Mathematical Practice (SMPs)

These eight practices apply at every grade level, K–12. They describe the habits of mind of a mathematically proficient person — which is to say, of any scientist who uses quantitative reasoning.

| # | Practice | Parallel to NGSS SEP | Story Moment |
|---|----------|---------------------|--------------|
| SMP.1 | **Make sense of problems and persevere in solving them** | SEP 1 (Asking Questions) | The scientist who refuses to give up when the problem resists |
| SMP.2 | **Reason abstractly and quantitatively** | SEP 5 (Mathematics and Computational Thinking) | The scientist who moves between concrete measurement and abstract equation |
| SMP.3 | **Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others** | SEP 7 (Argument from Evidence) | The mathematician who publishes a proof and waits for someone to find the flaw |
| SMP.4 | **Model with mathematics** | SEP 2 (Developing and Using Models) | Building the equation that describes something invisible |
| SMP.5 | **Use appropriate tools strategically** | SEP 3 (Planning Investigations) | Choosing the right instrument, measurement, or method |
| SMP.6 | **Attend to precision** | SEP 5 | The scientist who knows the difference between 3.14 and π |
| SMP.7 | **Look for and make use of structure** | SEP 2, SEP 4 | The mathematician who sees the same pattern in different domains |
| SMP.8 | **Look for and express regularity in repeated reasoning** | SEP 4 (Analyzing Data) | The scientist who notices that the same thing keeps happening and asks why |

**Story planning note**: The SMPs describe a specific character type — the person who sees the world as a structure that can be understood with precision. Scientists who are mathematicians (Milanković, Johnson, West, Turing) exemplify these practices as personality. The SMPs aren't what they do in the lab — they're who they are.

## Typical Course Sequence for Grades 5–12

| Grade | Course |
|-------|--------|
| 5 | Grade 5 Math |
| 6 | Grade 6 Math |
| 7 | Grade 7 Math |
| 8 | Grade 8 Math or Algebra I (accelerated) |
| 9 | Algebra I (or Geometry if accelerated) |
| 10 | Geometry (or Algebra II if accelerated) |
| 11 | Algebra II / Precalculus |
| 12 | Precalculus / AP Calculus AB / AP Calculus BC / AP Statistics |

Charter schools may offer different pathways. Common variations:
- **Integrated math** (integrating algebra, geometry, statistics across each year)
- **Acceleration tracks** starting Algebra I in grade 7–8
- **AP Statistics** as a 12th-grade option alongside or instead of Calculus

## Math Standards × Scientist Story Alignment

Mathematics is the language scientists use to make invisible things visible. A scientist story that includes math is not doing an add-on — it's showing the scientist's primary tool.

### Scientists Whose Stories Are Inherently Mathematical

| Scientist | Math Standards Most Relevant |
|-----------|------------------------------|
| Katherine Johnson | N.Q (quantities and units), F.IF (functions), G.GPE (coordinate geometry), F.TF (trig) |
| Gladys West | G.GMD (geometric measurement), S.ID (data fitting), A.CED (modeling equations) |
| Milutin Milanković | F.TF (periodic functions, orbital cycles), F.LE (exponential/sinusoidal models) |
| Alan Turing | N.CN (complex numbers), S.CP (probability), discrete math (logic, computation) |
| Florence Nightingale | S.ID (data visualization), S.IC (statistical inference from medical data) |
| Emmy Noether | Abstract algebra — connects to A.APR (polynomial structure) |
| Maryam Mirzakhani | G.CO, G.SRT (geometric proof and structure) |

### Mathematical Practices as Character Traits

The SMPs describe personality, not procedure. Use them to build the mathematician/scientist's character:

| SMP | Character manifestation |
|-----|------------------------|
| SMP.1 (persevere) | The scientist who stares at a wrong answer for hours instead of giving up |
| SMP.2 (abstract/quantitative) | The scientist who sees a number and immediately knows what it means about the world |
| SMP.3 (viable arguments) | The scientist who cannot let an unsound proof stand even if it produces the right answer |
| SMP.6 (precision) | The scientist who corrects everyone's rounding errors; for whom "approximately" is a moral failure |
| SMP.7 (structure) | The scientist who recognizes a pattern from topology appearing in a cellular biology problem |
| SMP.8 (regularity) | The scientist who is made uneasy by coincidences; who assumes patterns mean something |

|------------|------------------------|

## Statistical Literacy as a Critical Thinking Standard

**S.IC.6** — Evaluate reports based on data — deserves special attention for a STL charter school population. Students who understand statistical inference can:
- Evaluate claims made about their own communities
- Recognize when correlation is being presented as causation
- Understand how data about police violence, lead levels, health disparities, and environmental quality are constructed and what they mean

Scientist stories that model statistical reasoning (Florence Nightingale, John Snow's cholera map, Lonnie Thompson's ice cores, Eunice Newton Foote's greenhouse gas measurements) teach this standard while teaching science.

**John Snow's cholera map (1854)** is a canonical example of S.ID.6, S.IC, and MS-ESS3-3 simultaneously: a physician uses spatial data to construct an argument about disease transmission that the medical establishment rejects (SEP 7), mapping cases to a water pump in London's Soho neighborhood. The story is available for grades 6–12 at increasing levels of complexity.
