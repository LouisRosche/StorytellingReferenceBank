# Missouri Learning Standards — Social Studies (Grades 5–12)

Missouri's Social Studies standards are organized into four strands aligned to the national C3 Framework (College, Career, and Civic Life). They apply to all Missouri public and charter schools.

Social studies standards are rarely invoked in isolation — scientist stories almost always exist in historical, civic, economic, and geographic context. This reference maps both the standards themselves and their connection points to scientist story planning.

---

## Four Strands Overview

| Strand | Code | Core Questions |
|--------|------|----------------|
| **History** | H | What happened? Why? What changed? What remained? |
| **Civics and Government** | CG | How are societies organized? Who holds power? What are rights and responsibilities? |
| **Economics** | E | How do societies allocate scarce resources? Who benefits? Who bears costs? |
| **Geography** | G | Where? Why there? How does place shape people and events? |

All four strands appear in every grade band. The emphasis shifts by grade level.

---

## The Inquiry Arc (C3 Framework Foundation)

Missouri Social Studies adopts the C3 Inquiry Arc as its organizing process — the social studies equivalent of the SEPs in science.

| Dimension | Description | Parallel to Science SEPs |
|-----------|-------------|--------------------------|
| **D1** — Constructing Compelling Questions | Frame an inquiry worth pursuing | SEP 1: Asking Questions |
| **D2** — Applying Disciplinary Concepts | Use history, civics, economics, geography thinking | SEPs 2–5: Developing models, investigations, analysis |
| **D3** — Evaluating Sources and Using Evidence | Assess primary/secondary sources | SEPs 4, 7, 8: Analyzing data, argument, communicating |
| **D4** — Communicating Conclusions; Taking Informed Action | Share findings; take civic action | SEPs 6, 8: Explanations, communication |

**Story planning note**: The Inquiry Arc makes social studies a natural parallel discipline to science. A scientist story can serve both simultaneously — the scientist is doing scientific inquiry (SEPs) while operating inside historical, civic, economic, and geographic inquiry (the Inquiry Arc).

## Grade-by-Grade Content Focus

Missouri organizes social studies content by historical period and scale. Charter schools have some flexibility in sequencing, but the following reflects the standard Missouri scope and sequence.

| Grade | Primary Content Focus |
|-------|----------------------|
| 5 | U.S. History: colonial period through Reconstruction; geography of North America |
| 6 | World History: ancient civilizations through early modern period; world geography |
| 7 | World History: Renaissance through Industrial Revolution; global geography |
| 8 | U.S. History: Civil War era through World War I; Missouri history |
| 9 | World History: 20th century to present; global geography |
| 10 | U.S. History: World War I to present; Missouri history; contemporary issues |
| 11 | Government and Civics; economics; personal finance |
| 12 | Electives: AP History, AP Government, AP Economics, Contemporary Issues |

## Social Studies × Scientist Story Alignment

### Where Social Studies Standards Live Inside Scientist Stories

Every scientist operates inside a historical moment, a political structure, an economic system, and a geographic place. Social studies standards are not add-ons — they are the frame in which the science happens.

**History ↔ Scientist Story**

| Social Studies Standard | Scientist Story Connection |
|------------------------|---------------------------|
| H.1.B (primary sources, perspective) | Scientist's letters, lab notebooks, published papers as primary sources |
| H.7.4 (Scientific Revolution) | Stories of Copernicus, Galileo, Newton; challenge to institutional authority |
| H.8.1 (Industrial Revolution) | How industrialization created new scientific problems and new resources |
| H.10.2–10.6 (Harlem Renaissance, WWII, Civil Rights) | Context for Black and female scientists working in this era |
| H.9.3 (WWII, Manhattan Project, Holocaust) | Science and ethics; Meitner, Oppenheimer, Unit 731 |
| H.9.7 (climate, technology) | Contemporary scientists working on pressing global issues |

**Civics and Government ↔ Scientist Story**

| Social Studies Standard | Scientist Story Connection |
|------------------------|---------------------------|
| CG.1.D (rights) | Who gets institutional access; whose rights to education/employment are protected |
| CG.2.B (civic influence) | Scientists who testified before Congress; Rachel Carson and the EPA |
| CG.11.9 (public policy) | Science that shapes policy; policy that funds or restricts science |
| CG.11.6 (civil liberties) | Scientists whose work was suppressed; academic freedom cases |

**Economics ↔ Scientist Story**

| Social Studies Standard | Scientist Story Connection |
|------------------------|---------------------------|
| E.1.A (scarcity) | Research funding as scarce resource; who gets grants |
| E.1.F (incentives) | What motivates scientists; patents, publication, prestige |
| E.11.7 (government and markets) | Public vs. private research funding; pharmaceutical pricing (Percy Julian and cortisone) |
| E.11.6 (labor markets) | Underpaid technicians; uncredited lab workers; women in science |

**Geography ↔ Scientist Story**

| Social Studies Standard | Scientist Story Connection |
|------------------------|---------------------------|
| G.1.D (human-environment interaction) | Field scientists; scientists who change or are changed by environment |
| G.2.B (resource distribution) | Why certain science happens in certain places; STL as chemistry/aerospace hub |
| G.3.B (environmental consequences) | Scientists who document what human activity does to place |
| G.3.E (environmental justice) | Who lives near pollution; who bears the cost of "progress" |
