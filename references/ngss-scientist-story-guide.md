# NGSS Scientist Story Planning Guide

How to build scientist narratives that serve specific NGSS standards without becoming textbooks. The goal is a story that makes a student feel science from the inside — the uncertainty, the obsession, the fights with colleagues, the moment the world reorganizes itself.

Cross-reference: `@references/ngss-standards-5-12.md` for the standards themselves.

---

## The Core Problem

Most "scientist stories" fail in one of two directions:

**Too hagiographic**: The scientist is a genius who triumphed. Science is linear progress. Discovery is inevitable. The story has no tension because the outcome is never in doubt.

**Too didactic**: The story stops to explain concepts. Characters exist to receive information. The science is correct; the narrative is dead.

The fix is treating the scientist as a character in genuine conflict — with the unknown, with institutions, with their own assumptions. The science *is* the drama, not background to it.

---

## How the Three Dimensions Map to Story

### SEPs as Scene Types

Every scene in a scientist story can be anchored to one or more SEPs. This isn't a formula — it's a diagnostic. If your story has no SEP 7 moment (argument from evidence), you've probably written a fantasy where no one challenges the scientist.

**SEP 1 — Asking Questions / Defining Problems**

*Story function*: The inciting incident. Something doesn't fit. An anomaly appears. A problem surfaces that existing explanations can't handle.

*Craft note*: The question should feel personally urgent to the scientist, not abstractly important. Rosalind Franklin didn't just wonder about DNA structure — she was bothered by the imprecision of everyone else's methods.

*Grade-band scaling*:
- Grade 5: What is this thing and why is it doing that?
- Grades 6–8: Why does the data not match the model?
- Grades 9–12: What assumption are we all making that might be wrong?

---

**SEP 2 — Developing and Using Models**

*Story function*: The creative leap scene. Scientist constructs an internal visualization — a model — of something that cannot yet be directly observed. This is often the most visually rich scene in a scientist story.

*Craft note*: Show the model's limits, not just its power. Watson and Crick's early models of DNA were wrong. The model being wrong is part of the story.

*Opportunity*: This is where the scientist's imagination becomes visible. Let the reader see inside the scientist's mind.

---

**SEP 3 — Planning and Carrying Out Investigations**

*Story function*: The methodical grind. The design, the failure, the revision, the patience. This is where persistence becomes concrete and specific — not "she worked hard" but "she redesigned the experiment seventeen times."

*Craft note*: Specificity matters here. Name the actual equipment, the actual flaw in the method, the actual variable they couldn't control. Vague effort is unconvincing.

*Opportunity*: This is where class, gender, race, and institutional barriers enter the story as actual plot obstacles. Who gets the equipment? Who gets the funding? Who gets credit for the investigation?

---

**SEP 4 — Analyzing and Interpreting Data**

*Story function*: The revelation scene. Pattern emerges from noise. The scientist sees something in the data that no one has seen before.

*Craft note*: The revelation should feel earned, not magical. The reader should be able to trace the reasoning even if they couldn't have done it themselves. Show the scientist's reasoning, not just the conclusion.

*Warning*: Don't make this an "aha!" moment divorced from doubt. Real revelations are usually accompanied by "but that can't be right" — the instinct to disbelieve your own evidence.

---

**SEP 5 — Using Mathematics and Computational Thinking**

*Story function*: The precision scene. Quantifying, predicting, modeling. This is where the scientist makes the vague specific.

*Craft note*: Don't hide the math. A character doing actual calculation — even approximate — is more compelling than a character mysteriously "figuring things out." The math itself can be beautiful or terrifying.

*Grade-band scaling*:
- Grade 5: Measuring carefully; comparing quantities
- Grades 6–8: Graphing data; recognizing proportional relationships; using formulas
- Grades 9–12: Differential equations, statistical significance, Monte Carlo methods — whatever fits the scientist

---

**SEP 6 — Constructing Explanations / Designing Solutions**

*Story function*: The synthesis scene. The scientist assembles the evidence into a coherent claim. This is often the climax of the cognitive arc, though not necessarily the emotional arc.

*Craft note*: The explanation or solution should feel surprising in its simplicity. After all the complexity, the answer should be elegantly precise.

---

**SEP 7 — Engaging in Argument from Evidence**

*Story function*: The conflict scene. Peer review. Dismissal. The scientific community's resistance. Colleagues who are wrong but powerful.

*Craft note*: This is the most dramatically underused SEP. Real science is argument. The resistance isn't always villain behavior — sometimes the skeptics are right to be skeptical. Let the other side have a case.

*For underrepresented scientists*: This scene is where bias enters — not as abstract prejudice but as specific, documented behavior. Papers rejected. Credentials questioned. Credit taken. The argument is not just about evidence but about who is allowed to make arguments.

*Examples*:
- Eunice Newton Foote's 1856 greenhouse paper ignored; credit went to Tyndall
- Chien-Shiung Wu's parity violation experiment confirmed — her colleagues won the Nobel; she did not
- Barry Marshall drinking H. pylori broth because the medical community wouldn't believe him
- Ignaz Semmelweis dying in an asylum after being dismissed for recommending handwashing

---

**SEP 8 — Obtaining, Evaluating, and Communicating Information**

*Story function*: The legacy scene. How the scientist reads predecessors, publishes, is heard or ignored. This is where science as a collective enterprise enters the story.

*Opportunity*: This SEP opens the frame. A story can show how a current scientist stands on the shoulders of predecessors — creating a lineage of ideas across generations.

---

### CCCs as Thematic Throughlines

The crosscutting concept your story chooses determines what the story is *about* underneath the specific content.

**CCC 1 — Patterns**

*Theme*: Seeing is not the same as understanding. The pattern was always there; why did no one see it? Who gets to notice?

*Story application*: A scientist discovers a pattern that was visible to everyone but named by no one. Often connects to who is doing the looking — different vantage points see different patterns.

---

**CCC 2 — Cause and Effect**

*Theme*: Mechanism and accountability. Knowing how something works confers responsibility for what happens when it's misused.

*Story application*: The scientist who discovers a cause confronts what to do with that knowledge. Lead paint, tobacco, DDT, radioactivity — causation becomes moral weight.

---

**CCC 3 — Scale, Proportion, and Quantity**

*Theme*: The humbling of zoom. What is invisible at one scale dominates at another. Human-scale intuition misleads at the atomic or cosmic.

*Story application*: The scientist who must abandon what feels intuitively true. Atoms are mostly empty space. Geologic time makes human history a rounding error. Scale shifts produce vertigo and wonder.

---

**CCC 4 — Systems and System Models**

*Theme*: You can't change one thing. Intervening in a system always has downstream effects.

*Story application*: The scientist who solves one problem and creates another. Engineering, ecology, medicine — every intervention enters a system that will respond in unexpected ways.

---

**CCC 5 — Energy and Matter: Flows, Cycles, Conservation**

*Theme*: Nothing is destroyed, only transformed. Loss is not disappearance.

*Story application*: Often the quietly devastating theme — the scientist who discovers that harm doesn't end, it accumulates and cycles. Or the affirming reverse: the scientist who traces how life persists through transformation.

---

**CCC 6 — Structure and Function**

*Theme*: Form reveals purpose. What something looks like tells you what it does — and what you can do with it.

*Story application*: The scientist who reads the world through shape — the crystallographer, the anatomist, the engineer. Also: the scientist whose own life's structure is shaped by their function, or whose function is constrained by the structure of their society.

---

**CCC 7 — Stability and Change**

*Theme*: What holds, what breaks, what transforms. The threshold between equilibrium and disruption.

*Story application*: A system in balance is disturbed by the scientist's discovery — or the scientist tries and fails to restore a disturbed system to balance. This is the CCC most naturally suited to environmental justice narratives.

---

## Scientist Roster by Standards Domain

Real scientists, organized by the NGSS domain their work primarily addresses. Diversity is not optional here — it is structurally required for the stories to do their full work. A charter school in STL serving underrepresented students needs scientists who look like those students and who faced the same institutional barriers those students will face.

Each entry includes: name, background, standards match, a central dramatic situation, and the CCC that most fits their story.

---

### Physical Sciences (PS)

**Percy Julian** (1899–1975) — Black American chemist, born Montgomery, AL; worked in the Midwest
- Standards: HS-PS1 (organic synthesis, chemical reactions), HS-ETS1
- Story situation: Julian synthesizes physostigmine for glaucoma treatment cheaper than existing sources, working against an institution that won't hire him for his race. He later synthesizes cortisone at a fraction of the existing cost — making the drug affordable — out of soybean oil. His Oak Park, IL home was firebombed twice.
- Central drama: Excellence as provocation; the scientist whose success is a threat
- CCC: Cause and Effect (synthesis has consequences); Structure and Function

**Marie Curie** (1867–1934) — Polish/French woman, immigrant, two-time Nobel laureate
- Standards: HS-PS1-7 (radioactivity, nuclear processes)
- Story situation: Curie discovers radioactivity is an atomic property — not a chemical reaction — with Marie and Pierre working in a leaking shed, manually processing tons of pitchblende. She is denied entry to the French Academy of Sciences because she is a woman.
- Central drama: The cost of curiosity; the work that kills
- CCC: Energy and Matter (radioactive decay); Scale (atomic phenomenon invisible to human senses)

**Lise Meitner** (1878–1968) — Austrian Jewish physicist, worked in Germany, fled Nazi Germany in 1938
- Standards: HS-PS1-7 (nuclear fission), HS-PS2 (forces, binding energy)
- Story situation: Meitner explains nuclear fission — the physics that Otto Hahn's team couldn't explain — via letter while in exile in Sweden. Hahn wins the Nobel Prize. Meitner is not recognized. Element 109, meitnerium, is named for her posthumously.
- Central drama: The scientist who supplies the theory and receives none of the credit
- CCC: Energy and Matter (E=mc², mass-energy equivalence)

**Chien-Shiung Wu** (1912–1997) — Chinese-American experimental physicist
- Standards: HS-PS2-2 (forces), HS-PS4 (EM radiation and particles)
- Story situation: Wu designs the definitive experiment proving that parity is violated in weak interactions — a result her male colleagues proposed but couldn't prove. Lee and Yang win the 1957 Nobel Prize for the theoretical prediction. Wu does not.
- Central drama: Doing the work that wins someone else the prize; scientific credit and attribution
- CCC: Stability and Change (conservation law violated)

**Michael Faraday** (1791–1867) — English physicist/chemist, no formal education, born into poverty
- Standards: HS-PS3-5 (electric circuits), MS-PS2-3 (electric/magnetic forces)
- Story situation: Faraday, who taught himself chemistry from books he typeset as a bookbinder, becomes a laboratory assistant and eventually discovers electromagnetic induction — the principle behind electric motors and generators. The Royal Society dismisses him repeatedly.
- Central drama: Class as barrier; knowledge without credentials
- CCC: Structure and Function (field lines); Cause and Effect (moving magnet, electric current)

**Nikola Tesla** (1856–1943) — Serbian immigrant physicist/engineer
- Standards: HS-PS3-5 (AC electricity), HS-ETS1 (engineering design)
- Story situation: Tesla argues for alternating current against Edison's direct current. The "War of Currents" is waged partly through public spectacle, including Tesla standing in AC arcs to prove their safety. He dies destitute despite foundational patents.
- Central drama: Being right and losing anyway; intellectual property and power
- CCC: Energy and Matter (energy transmission and loss)

**Katherine Johnson** (1918–2020) — Black American mathematician, born White Sulphur Springs, WV
- Standards: HS-ESS1-3 (orbital mechanics, Kepler's laws), HS-PS2-1 (forces, motion), HS-ETS1
- Story situation: Johnson calculates the trajectories for Freedom 7, Friendship 7, and Apollo 11 by hand. John Glenn refuses to fly unless Katherine Johnson personally verifies the IBM computer's numbers. She works at a facility where she must use a separate bathroom marked "Colored."
- Central drama: Being indispensable while being treated as disposable
- CCC: Scale, Proportion, and Quantity (orbital mechanics); Systems (mission as integrated system)

**Gladys West** (1930– ) — Black American mathematician, born Sutherland, VA
- Standards: HS-ETS1-4 (computational modeling), HS-ESS1-3
- Story situation: West's mathematical modeling of Earth's shape — accounting for gravitational anomalies — becomes the foundation for GPS satellite systems. She works for decades at the Naval Proving Ground in Virginia; her contribution to GPS goes uncredited for fifty years.
- Central drama: Invisible labor; the credit gap; delayed recognition
- CCC: Systems and System Models (GPS is a system model of Earth's shape)

---

### Life Sciences (LS)

**Rosalind Franklin** (1920–1958) — British Jewish biophysicist
- Standards: HS-LS3-2 (gene expression, DNA structure), MS-LS3-1 (chromosomes and genes)
- Story situation: Franklin's X-ray crystallography images of DNA — especially Photo 51 — are shown to Watson and Crick without her knowledge by Wilkins. Watson and Crick publish the double helix structure. Franklin dies of ovarian cancer in 1958, before the Nobel is awarded to Watson, Crick, and Wilkins in 1962.
- Central drama: Stolen work; the question of who gets to tell the story of a discovery
- CCC: Structure and Function (structure of DNA determines function)

**Barbara McClintock** (1902–1992) — American geneticist
- Standards: HS-LS3-1 (chromosomal inheritance), HS-LS3-3 (environmental effects on phenotype)
- Story situation: McClintock discovers transposons — "jumping genes" — in 1951. The scientific community dismisses or ignores the finding for decades. She continues working alone at Cold Spring Harbor. She wins the Nobel Prize in 1983, thirty-two years after the discovery.
- Central drama: The long patience; continuing to work when no one believes you
- CCC: Stability and Change (genome instability); Patterns (seeing patterns others don't)

**Henrietta Lacks** (1920–1951) — Black American woman, born Roanoke, VA
- Standards: HS-LS1-3 (mitosis, cell division), HS-LS1-1 (homeostasis, feedback failure in cancer)
- Story situation: Lacks's cervical cancer cells — HeLa cells — are taken without consent during her 1951 treatment at Johns Hopkins and become the most widely used cells in biomedical research, contributing to the polio vaccine, cancer research, and more. Her family learns of this decades later, never compensated.
- Central drama: Whose body; whose knowledge; consent and exploitation in science
- CCC: Systems and System Models (cancer as failure of cell regulation system)

**Lynn Margulis** (1938–2011) — American biologist
- Standards: HS-LS4-2 (evolutionary evidence), HS-LS1-1 (cell structure)
- Story situation: Margulis proposes the endosymbiotic theory — that mitochondria and chloroplasts were once free-living bacteria absorbed by larger cells — in 1966. Her paper is rejected by fifteen journals before publication. The theory is now foundational.
- Central drama: The heterodox idea; institutional gatekeeping of knowledge
- CCC: Systems and System Models; Stability and Change

**Jane Goodall** (1934– ) — British primatologist
- Standards: MS-LS2-1 (predator-prey, competition), MS-LS4-4 (natural selection, behavior), MS-LS4-6 (adaptation)
- Story situation: Goodall goes to Gombe without a college degree, observes chimpanzees making and using tools, and is immediately challenged by the scientific establishment for naming the chimpanzees and applying emotional/psychological language to their behavior. Her methodology is dismissed as unscientific.
- Central drama: The observer as part of the observation; changing what "scientific" means
- CCC: Cause and Effect; Patterns

**Rachel Carson** (1907–1964) — American marine biologist and author
- Standards: MS-ESS3-3 (human impacts on Earth), MS-LS2-4 (human effects on biodiversity), HS-LS2-7
- Story situation: Carson writes *Silent Spring* (1962) while terminally ill with cancer, documenting the effects of pesticides on bird populations and ecosystems. The chemical industry launches a coordinated campaign to discredit her. Her work leads to the ban of DDT and the founding of the EPA.
- Central drama: Doing the most important work of your life while dying; the corporate attack on science
- CCC: Cause and Effect (chemical exposure → ecosystem collapse); Systems

**Mae C. Jemison** (1956– ) — Black American physician and astronaut, grew up in Chicago
- Standards: HS-LS1-7 (matter and energy in living systems), HS-ESS1-3, MS-LS1-2
- Story situation: Jemison is the first Black woman in space (1992), carrying the West African statuette she brought to connect her heritage with space. She later works on space medicine and the 100 Year Starship project.
- Central drama: Claiming space (literal and metaphorical); identity and authority
- CCC: Scale, Proportion, and Quantity (scale of the body in space)

**Rebecca Lee Crumpler** (1831–1895) — Black American physician, first Black woman MD in the US
- Standards: HS-LS1-1 (homeostasis, body systems), MS-LS1-2 (body systems)
- Story situation: Crumpler becomes the first Black woman to earn a medical degree in the US (1864), practices medicine for freed people in Richmond, VA after the Civil War, and writes *A Book of Medical Discourses* (1883) — the first medical text by a Black American.
- Central drama: Medicine as justice work; practicing in a context of deliberate obstruction
- CCC: Stability and Change (health and disease as stability disrupted)

**George Washington Carver** (~1864–1943) — Black American scientist, born into slavery in Diamond, MO
- Standards: MS-LS1-6 (photosynthesis, plant biology), HS-PS1 (chemical properties), HS-LS2-2 (biogeochemical cycles, soil)
- Story situation: Carver develops hundreds of products from peanuts, sweet potatoes, and soybeans to help Southern Black farmers diversify away from cotton, which depletes soil nitrogen. He turns down large offers from Edison and Ford to remain at Tuskegee, teaching.
- STL/MO connection: Born in Diamond, MO — direct geographic anchor for STL school
- Central drama: Science in service of community; refusing wealth to stay rooted
- CCC: Energy and Matter (nitrogen cycle, crop rotation); Systems

**Wangari Maathai** (1940–2011) — Kenyan environmental activist and biologist
- Standards: HS-ESS3-6 (sustainability design solutions), HS-LS2-3 (biodiversity), MS-ESS3-4
- Story situation: Maathai founds the Green Belt Movement, organizing Kenyan women to plant 51 million trees over thirty years while facing government repression and imprisonment. She wins the Nobel Peace Prize in 2004.
- Central drama: Ecological restoration as political act; women's labor as scientific work
- CCC: Stability and Change (deforestation → restoration); Systems

### Earth and Space Sciences (ESS)

**Eunice Newton Foote** (1819–1888) — American scientist, abolitionist, women's rights activist
- Standards: MS-ESS2-6 (human activities and global temperature), HS-ESS2-4 (climate change), HS-ESS3-4
- Story situation: Foote publishes the first description of the greenhouse effect in 1856, showing that CO₂ traps heat. Her paper is presented at the American Association for the Advancement of Science — by a man, because she could not present it herself. Irish physicist John Tyndall publishes a more detailed version three years later and receives full credit. Foote is unknown for 150 years.
- Central drama: Erasure; the long delay in recognition; the history that hides in plain sight
- CCC: Cause and Effect (CO₂ → warming); Energy and Matter

**Alfred Wegener** (1880–1930) — German meteorologist and geophysicist
- Standards: MS-ESS2-2, MS-ESS2-3 (plate tectonics), HS-ESS2-2
- Story situation: Wegener proposes continental drift in 1912 based on the fit of continents and fossil correlations. The geological establishment dismisses him for thirty years — in part because he was a meteorologist proposing geology, not a geologist. The mechanism he proposed (continents plowing through ocean floor) was wrong; the phenomenon was correct. He dies in Greenland on a supply expedition in 1930.
- Central drama: Being right for the wrong reason; disciplinary boundary as barrier
- CCC: Patterns (coastline shapes, fossil distributions); Stability and Change

**Lonnie Thompson** (1948– ) — American glaciologist, born Gassaway, WV, raised working class
- Standards: HS-ESS2-3 (geologic record of climate change), HS-ESS3-4 (climate change evidence)
- Story situation: Thompson drills ice cores at high altitudes on tropical glaciers — the Andes, Tibet, Kilimanjaro — where no one else went, preserving climate records going back 750,000 years. He is among the first to document rapid tropical glacier retreat due to warming. He had a heart transplant and returned to field work.
- Central drama: Collecting evidence the world doesn't want to hear; the scientist as witness
- CCC: Scale (geologic time); Stability and Change

**Ynés Mexía** (1870–1938) — Mexican-American botanist, began career at 55
- Standards: MS-LS2-1 (ecosystems, interdependence), MS-ESS2 (Earth systems)
- Story situation: Mexía begins botany fieldwork at age fifty-five with no formal education in science, eventually collecting 150,000 specimens — many new to science — across Alaska, Mexico, and South America. She works against the expectation that a woman her age without credentials cannot contribute.
- Central drama: Starting late; the scientific credential vs. the scientific capacity
- CCC: Patterns (botanical classification); Systems

**Milutin Milanković** (1879–1958) — Serbian mathematician/astronomer
- Standards: HS-ESS1-3 (orbital mechanics), HS-ESS2-3 (Milanković cycles and climate)
- Story situation: Milanković spends years calculating — by hand, in pencil — the variations in Earth's orbital parameters (eccentricity, obliquity, precession) over 600,000 years and their effect on solar radiation at each latitude. He does much of this work as a prisoner of war in Budapest. The theory is confirmed by deep-sea sediment cores in 1976.
- Central drama: Decades of solitary calculation; vindication arrived too late for vindication to matter
- CCC: Patterns; Cause and Effect

## Planning a Scientist Story: Process

### Step 1 — Anchor to Standards First

Identify the specific NGSS performance expectation(s) the story should serve. Use `@references/ngss-standards-5-12.md`.

Ask:
- Which grade band is this for?
- Is this a DCI-primary story (teaching content) or SEP-primary story (modeling scientific practice)?
- Which CCC will become the thematic throughline?

---

### Step 2 — Select a Scientist Whose Story Serves the Standards

Use the roster above or research further. Apply these filters:

**Representation filter**: Does the student audience see themselves in scientists? For a predominantly Black and Brown student population in STL, this is not a supplementary concern — it is a primary one. A scientist who looks like the reader, who faced institutional barriers like those the reader will face, transmits a different and more necessary message than the same content taught through a white male canonical figure.

**Dramatic situation filter**: Does this scientist's life contain genuine conflict, failure, and stakes? If the answer is no, look for a less famous scientist in the same domain.

**Specificity filter**: Can you find enough specific, concrete, sensory detail about how this person worked — what their lab smelled like, what their arguments sounded like, what they wrote in letters — to make the story feel real?

---

### Step 3 — Map the SEPs to Scenes

Assign each major scene a primary SEP. Not all SEPs need appear, but the story should contain at minimum:
- SEP 1 (the initial question — inciting incident)
- SEP 3 or 4 (the investigation/data scene — rising action)
- SEP 7 (the argument scene — conflict/climax)
- SEP 6 or 8 (the explanation or communication — resolution/legacy)

---

### Step 4 — Choose the CCC as Theme

The CCC shouldn't be imposed on the story; it should be the lens that makes the scientist's specific work feel *universal*. The right CCC is the one that makes a student say, after the story: "That's what all of science is, actually."

---

### Step 5 — Identify the Institutional Barrier

Every scientist story worth telling includes the institutional context — who controls access to equipment, funding, publication, recognition. This is not optional material to be included for political reasons; it is structurally necessary for dramatic tension.

For a STL charter school serving underrepresented students: the institutional barrier is *the story* as much as the science. The student who sees a scientist navigate those barriers with intelligence and dignity is receiving a map.

---

### Step 6 — Anchor in STL/MO Where Possible

Use the contextual hooks in `@references/ngss-standards-5-12.md`. A story set in abstract "a laboratory somewhere" is less powerful than a story that connects to something the student can see or touch.

George Washington Carver was born in Diamond, MO. Weldon Spring in St. Charles County, MO is a Superfund site with a complex chemical history. The New Madrid Seismic Zone is forty miles from the STL city limits. The Mississippi River has been engineered and re-engineered for over a century at the city's doorstep. Use it.

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

**The Lone Genius**: Science happens in community, in argument, in debt to predecessors. A scientist who works alone in a vacuum and arrives at truth untested is a myth, not a model.

**The Inevitable Triumph**: If the story's outcome is never in doubt, there is no story. The scientist should lose something — time, credit, health, certainty — even when they win.

**The Teaching Interrupt**: A scene where a character exists to receive information from the scientist is a sign the narrative has collapsed into lecture. Science should be *shown through doing*, not explained.

**The Colorblind Discovery**: Omitting the racial, gender, class, or national context of a marginalized scientist's experience erases the most important part of the story — the part that connects to the students' own lives.

**The Abstract Setting**: Specific places matter. Real equipment. Real institutions. Real cities. "A lab in the 1950s" is not a setting.

## Story Length and Grade-Band Calibration

| Grade Band | Appropriate Length | Structural Complexity | Language Register |
|------------|-------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Grade 5 | 500–1500 words | Single POV, linear | Accessible but not condescending |
| Grades 6–8 | 1500–5000 words | Can include time shifts, multiple perspectives | Challenging vocabulary in context |
| Grades 9–12 | 3000–10,000+ words | Full structural complexity; unreliable narrators possible | No ceiling — match the scientist's world |

Grade 5 stories: the science is felt through sensory experience. A student *observes* the experiment with the scientist.

Grades 6–8 stories: the science is felt through problem-solving. A student *thinks through* the problem alongside the scientist.

Grades 9–12 stories: the science is felt through implication. A student *inherits* the question the scientist couldn't fully answer.
