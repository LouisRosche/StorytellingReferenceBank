# Children's Picture Book Style Guide

Industry-standard specifications for ages 3-8. Constraints are features, not limitations.

## Word Count Limits

**These are hard limits, not guidelines.**

| Age Range | Word Count | Notes |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| Board books (0-3) | 0-100 | Often wordless or single words |
| Picture books (3-5) | 100-500 | Sweet spot: 200-400 |
| Picture books (4-8) | 300-600 | Absolute max: 600 |
| Early readers (6-8) | 200-1,500 | Leveled; specific publisher guidelines |

Agent Mary Kole: **"600 words max. Seriously. Max."**

### Classic Benchmarks

| Title | Words | Takeaway |
|-------|-------|----------|
| *Goodnight Moon* | 131 | Simplicity is power |
| *The Very Hungry Caterpillar* | 221 | Repetition + concept |
| *Where the Wild Things Are* | 336 | Emotional depth, minimal text |
| *Green Eggs and Ham* | 769 | Uses only 50 unique words |

## Page Structure

### The 32-Page Format

Printing economics dictate structure. Paper folds into 8-page signatures; 32 is the standard.

```
Pages 1-4:    Front matter (title, copyright, dedication)
Pages 5-31:   Story content (13-14 spreads)
Page 32:      Back matter or final image
```

### Spread Breakdown (26-28 pages of story)

| Spread | Pages | Story Position |
|--------|-------|----------------|
| 1-2 | 5-6 | Opening—establish world, character |
| 3-4 | 7-10 | Setup continues; problem hints |
| 5 | 9-10 | **End of beginning** |
| 6-9 | 11-18 | Development; **midpoint ~pages 16-18** |
| 10-12 | 19-24 | Rising action |
| 12-13 | 23-26 | **Climax ~pages 24-25** |
| 14 | 27-28 | Falling action |
| End | 29-31 | **Resolution by page 31** |

### Page Turn Mechanics

**End each spread with tension.** Page turns create suspense; use them.

**Effective page turn setups:**
- Question without answer
- Character about to discover something
- Interrupted action
- "And then..."

**What appears after the turn:**
- Answer to question
- The discovery
- Consequence of action
- Surprise or reversal

**Example pattern:**
```
Spread 3: "Max sailed through night and day..."
[PAGE TURN]
Spread 4: "...and in and out of weeks, and almost over a year, to where the wild things are."
```

## Language Guidelines

### Sentence Structure

- **Short sentences dominate.** 5-10 words average.
- **One idea per sentence.**
- **Active voice almost always.**
- **Concrete nouns over abstractions.** "Dog" not "pet." "Ran" not "moved quickly."

### Vocabulary

- Familiar words for core narrative
- One or two "stretch" words per book—children love learning new words in context
- Avoid: idioms, sarcasm, ambiguous pronouns

### Read-Aloud Qualities

**Test by reading aloud 10+ times.** Manuscripts should feel good in the mouth.

| Technique | Purpose | Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Alliteration | Phonemic awareness | "Silly Sally slid" |
| Repetition | Predictability, participation | "Brown bear, brown bear" |
| Refrain | Rhythm, callback | Repeated phrase at key moments |
| Onomatopoeia | Engagement, vocabulary | "Splash! Crash! Boom!" |
| Cumulative | Memory, anticipation | Each page adds to a growing list |

### Call-and-Response

Design moments where children chime in. Predictable phrases, repeated questions, counting sequences.

## Rhyme: Handle with Care

**Publishers are wary of rhyme.** Most submissions fail.

**Risks:**
- Bad meter is "like nails on a blackboard"
- Forced rhymes sacrifice meaning for sound
- Translation impossible—kills foreign rights revenue
- Near-rhymes and slant rhymes rarely work in children's books

**Requirements if using rhyme:**
1. Perfect meter—every line scans consistently
2. Story works stripped of verse—prose summary must hold
3. Rhymes feel inevitable, not forced
4. Read aloud flawlessly on first attempt

**Safer alternatives:**
- Prose with strong rhythm
- Occasional internal rhyme
- Repetition without rhyme

## Character & Theme

### Protagonist Requirements

- **Child or child-analog** (animal, toy, creature at child's developmental level)
- **Agency**: Protagonist solves their own problem; adults don't rescue
- **Clear want**: External goal stated early
- **Flaw or fear**: Something to overcome
- **Transformation**: Character learns/grows (can be subtle)

### Theme Handling

- **Show, don't preach.** No moral lectures.
- **Trust the reader.** Children extract meaning without explanation.
- **Avoid heavy topics** unless you're specifically writing about them—death, divorce, illness require intentional handling
- **Universality**: Emotions children recognize—fear, loneliness, jealousy, joy

## Audiobook Considerations (Qwen3-TTS)

### TTS-Specific Adjustments

- **Indicate page turns** in script: `[PAGE TURN]` for natural pauses
- **Mark character dialogue** for voice differentiation
- **Flag emotions** for prosody: `[excited]`, `[whispered]`, `[scared]`
- **Spell out sounds** phonetically if unusual: "WHOOOOSH" not "whoosh"

### Pacing Notes

- **Slower overall pace** than adult narration
- **Longer pauses** at page turns (1-2 seconds)
- **Exaggerated emotional shifts**—subtle doesn't work for young listeners
- **Consistent character voices**—children notice inconsistency

### Sample TTS Direction Block

```
[NARRATOR: Warm, playful female voice. Clear enunciation. Natural smile.]

"Max wore his wolf suit and made mischief of one kind"
[pause]
"and another."
[PAGE TURN - 2 second pause]
"His mother called him 'WILD THING!'"
[CHARACTER VOICE: Mother - slightly stern but loving, raised volume on 'WILD THING']
```

## Submission Checklist

- [ ] Word count under 600 (ideally under 500)
- [ ] Story works in 13-14 spreads
- [ ] Page turns create anticipation
- [ ] Protagonist has agency
- [ ] Read aloud 10+ times—sounds natural
- [ ] No preaching; theme emerges from action
- [ ] If rhyming: perfect meter, story works without verse
- [ ] Adult appeal exists (parents read this 100+ times)
