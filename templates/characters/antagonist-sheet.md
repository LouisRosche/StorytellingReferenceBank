# Antagonist Development Sheet

A dedicated template for antagonist construction. Use alongside `@templates/characters/character-sheet.md` — that sheet handles physical description, TTS persona, and consistency tracking. This sheet handles the structural and philosophical work that makes an antagonist function.

Store completed sheets in the project's `story-bible/characters/` directory.

**Before you start**: Can you articulate your antagonist's worldview with genuine sympathy? Could you write a version of the story from their point of view where they're the hero? If no to either, the character isn't ready. See `@references/the-craft-of-lasting-work.md` (Dostoevsky section) and `@references/common-pitfalls.md` (entry 8: Antagonist as Cardboard).

## Their Worldview

### What They Believe About How the World Works

<!-- Not what they want — what they believe is TRUE about reality. This is the philosophical foundation everything else rests on. A compelling antagonist has a coherent epistemology.

Examples of real worldview statements:
  - "Power flows only from fear. Love is what the weak call their manipulation."
  - "Systems exist to be gamed. Everyone who tells you otherwise is already gaming them."
  - "History is written by survivors. Morality is a story winners tell to keep the losers cooperative."

Flat statement: "He believes the world is cruel." (Not enough — this could belong to anyone.)
Specific worldview: "He believes human decency is a performance sustained by stability; strip away comfort and every person reveals the same appetite. He's seen it happen. He watched good people become something else." (This has history, evidence, and produces specific behavior.) -->

### What Justifies Their Actions (In Their Own Mind)

<!-- The antagonist isn't the villain of their own story. What argument do they make to themselves? This should be articulable without sarcasm — state it as they would state it. If you can only articulate it mockingly, you haven't understood them.

Avoid: "He justifies it because he's just evil."
Aim for: A paragraph written from their perspective, first person if it helps, that explains why what they're doing is not only necessary but arguably right. -->

### What They Are Right About

<!-- This is not optional. A villain who is never correct about anything is a cartoon. Dostoevsky's Grand Inquisitor makes an argument about human freedom that Christ does not directly refute — because it cannot simply be refuted. The antagonist's insight should be something the protagonist has to reckon with, not dismiss.

The test: Could a reasonable person, with the antagonist's experiences and information, arrive at this conclusion? If yes, you have a real antagonist.

What are they right about? -->

### What They Get Wrong

<!-- The distortion, the missing piece, the place where their worldview breaks. This is usually where the story's argument lives — the thing the protagonist understands or learns that the antagonist refuses to.

This should not be obvious from the start. If the antagonist's error is immediately apparent to any reader, the conflict isn't real. Their wrongness should require demonstration, not announcement. -->

## The Mirror Section

<!-- This is the structural heart of antagonist development. The antagonist and protagonist are in dialogue about the same question; they've given different answers. If you can't articulate the shared question, the conflict is external only — about events, not meaning. -->

### What They Share

**Shared wound or formative experience**:
<!-- The protagonist and antagonist often emerge from the same kind of pain. Not necessarily the same event, but the same category of loss: abandonment, betrayal, powerlessness, the failure of an institution they trusted, the death of someone they depended on. What's the wound both carry? -->

**Shared desire or goal**:
<!-- Beneath the conflict, they often want the same thing. Safety. Recognition. Justice. A world that makes sense. Control over the chaos that broke them. What is it? -->

**Shared capacity or gift**:
<!-- They are often made of similar material — intelligence, ambition, empathy, violence. The antagonist is not the protagonist's inferior; they're often frighteningly similar. What can both do? -->

### Where They Diverged

<!-- The story of the antagonist is often: same starting conditions, different choice. What was the fork? What did the antagonist choose that set them on this path? The choice should be understandable — even sympathetic. The best antagonist origin isn't a fall into inexplicable evil; it's a recognizable decision made under unbearable pressure that had consequences the character couldn't foresee or refused to face. -->

**The divergence point**:
<!-- A specific moment, choice, or realization, if you can identify one. -->

**The logic of the choice**:
<!-- Why did it seem right — or at least survivable — at the time? -->

### What the Protagonist Would Become

<!-- Following the antagonist's path to its end: if the protagonist took the antagonist's road, what would they look like? This is what's at stake psychologically. The antagonist is one possible future of the protagonist. Be specific. Name the qualities that would calcify, the relationships that would be sacrificed, the self-deceptions that would become permanent. -->

### The Story They Tell Themselves

<!-- Every antagonist has a self-narrative that makes their actions bearable. What is the story? Who are they the hero of? What do they think their life means? What do they believe they're building or protecting or avenging?

Write it as they would tell it — no ironic distance, no authorial correction. This is the story that keeps them moving. -->

### What They Would Never Admit

<!-- The thing that, if named aloud, would make their worldview collapse. The gap between who they are and who they believe themselves to be. Often this is:
  - That they are afraid
  - That they are lonely
  - That they still want what they gave up on
  - That someone they harmed didn't deserve it
  - That the thing that made them this way was not inevitable — they chose it

This doesn't have to appear in the story explicitly. But you need to know it, because it will leak into their behavior in ways that make them human. -->

## Their Arc

<!-- Antagonists are defined partly by the fact that they usually don't change — that's what distinguishes them structurally from the protagonist. But "don't change" covers a range of outcomes, and the nature of the resolution carries meaning. -->

**Arc type**:
<!-- Mark one:

  - **Defeat** — Overcome by the protagonist without fundamental change in themselves. Their worldview is wrong and the story demonstrates this through consequence. The most common outcome.

  - **Doubling-down** — Confronted with evidence that their worldview is flawed, they recommit to it anyway. This is tragedy (if the antagonist is sympathetic) or horror (if they're not). It argues: some people cannot change, and the cost of that is real.

  - **Revelation without change** — They understand, in the final act, exactly what they've done and what they've become. They see clearly. And they still do it. Or they see clearly and it's too late. The knowledge comes but the transformation doesn't.

  - **Tragic understanding** — They were right about something, wrong about the method, and the story lets them know it. They lose, or they die, or they achieve their goal and find it empty. Something like sorrow is the result. Use this when the antagonist deserves more complexity than a defeat and less redemption than a full arc.

  - **Redemption** — They change. This is rare for antagonists and requires that the change be earned — that they pay a real cost, not simply that they see the light. If used, examine whether this character was ever really an antagonist or always a flawed ally. (A character who changes is structurally a protagonist, even in a supporting role.)

  - **Corruption of something good** — What ends the story is not their defeat but the realization of what they've done to something or someone they once valued. The corruption is the arc. Often the most devastating option. -->

## Relationship to Theme

<!-- The antagonist exists to make the theme a real contest. If the theme is "connection saves us," the antagonist embodies "isolation is strength" — and they need to have a case. If the antagonist's position is obviously wrong from the beginning, the theme isn't being tested; it's being illustrated. -->

**What thematic argument does the antagonist embody?**
<!-- State it as a claim they'd endorse. Not "darkness," but: "Power without accountability is the only honest form of order." Not "selfishness," but: "Every act of apparent generosity is covert self-interest, and pretending otherwise is hypocrisy." -->

**What is the protagonist's counter-argument?**
<!-- The argument the protagonist's arc enacts — not necessarily articulates. -->

**Is the antagonist's position entirely wrong?**
<!-- If yes, the theme is closed. If no — if the antagonist has a real point that the story cannot fully refute — the theme is open, which is more honest and more durable. Where does the antagonist's argument hold? -->

**What does defeating or not defeating the antagonist say?**
<!-- Protagonist wins: argues the protagonist's worldview is right, or at least functional enough to survive.
Protagonist loses: argues the opposite, or something harder.
Protagonist wins but the antagonist's point stands: the most complex outcome. What's being said?
Antagonist and protagonist both lose: tragedy; the question was unanswerable by either position. -->

### Tracking Checklist

<!-- Items that commonly break antagonist consistency. Check these across revision passes: -->

- [ ] Their knowledge of the protagonist is consistent with when they learned things
- [ ] Their methods escalate — they don't use their most extreme tool in chapter two and then pull back
- [ ] The line they won't cross holds unless there is a specific, motivated break (and if it breaks, that break is a major story moment)
- [ ] Their speech patterns don't drift toward the protagonist's
- [ ] Their goal doesn't shift without a documented cause
- [ ] They respond to the protagonist's actions — they're not static between encounters
- [ ] The wound they carry surfaces in behavior, not just backstory
- [ ] At no point do they do something stupid because the plot needed them to lose
