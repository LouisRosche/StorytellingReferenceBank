# Global Narrative Frameworks

Beyond Western three-act structure. These frameworks offer different relationships between story, emotion, community, and meaning.

---

## Rasa Theory (Indian)

**Origin**: Natyashastra (~200 BCE - 600 CE), refined by Abhinavagupta (10th-11th century)

**Core concept**: Story exists to create *rasa* (aesthetic-emotional experience) in the audience. The goal is not plot resolution but emotional transportation.

### The Nine Rasas

| Rasa | Emotion (Bhava) | Color | Deity | Application |
|------|-----------------|-------|-------|-------------|
| **Shringara** | Love/Beauty | Light green | Vishnu | Romance, longing, union |
| **Hasya** | Mirth/Laughter | White | Shiva | Comedy, wit, absurdity |
| **Karuna** | Compassion/Sorrow | Grey | Yama | Tragedy, loss, empathy |
| **Raudra** | Fury/Anger | Red | Rudra | Confrontation, injustice |
| **Veera** | Heroism/Courage | Yellowish | Indra | Adventure, triumph |
| **Bhayanaka** | Terror/Fear | Black | Kala | Horror, existential dread |
| **Bibhatsa** | Disgust/Aversion | Blue | Mahakala | Body horror, moral revulsion |
| **Adbhuta** | Wonder/Amazement | Yellow | Brahma | Fantasy, revelation, sublime |
| **Shanta** | Peace/Serenity | White | Vishnu | Resolution, transcendence |

### The Rasa Formula

```
Vibhava (Causes) + Anubhava (Effects) + Vyabhicharibhava (Transitory states)
                            ↓
                         RASA
```

**Vibhava**: What causes the emotion (setting, characters, situation)
**Anubhava**: How characters express the emotion (actions, words, gestures)
**Vyabhicharibhava**: Transitory feelings that support the main emotion (33 states like anxiety, fatigue, joy)

### Application to Writing

1. **Choose your dominant rasa** before plotting. What emotional experience should the audience have?
2. **Layer supporting rasas** to create texture (a Karuna story might include Hasya for relief)
3. **Design vibhavas** (causes): setting, character relationships, situation
4. **Write anubhavas** (expressions): how characters embody the emotion
5. **Track the sahrdaya** (ideal audience member): are they experiencing the rasa?

### Key Distinction from Western Structure

| Western | Rasa |
|---------|------|
| "What happens next?" | "What do I feel now?" |
| Plot drives emotion | Emotion drives plot |
| Climax resolves conflict | Climax achieves rasa saturation |
| Success = satisfying ending | Success = transportation (transcendent experience) |

### When to Use Rasa

- Literary fiction prioritizing emotional depth over plot
- Stories about internal transformation
- Romance, tragedy, horror (emotion-forward genres)
- When you want readers to *feel* rather than *know*

### Rasa + Other Frameworks

Rasa can overlay any structure. A three-act thriller can be designed with Bhayanaka (terror) as dominant rasa. Save the Cat beats can each target specific supporting rasas.

## Jo-ha-kyū (Japanese)

**Origin**: Gagaku court music, codified by Zeami Motokiyo (~1400 CE) for Noh theater

**Core concept**: All movement follows a natural rhythm: slow beginning, breaking/acceleration, rapid conclusion. This rhythm exists at every scale simultaneously.

### The Three Phases

| Phase | Meaning | Energy | Function |
|-------|---------|--------|----------|
| **Jo (序)** | Beginning | Slow, deliberate | Establish, orient, create anticipation |
| **Ha (破)** | Breaking | Accelerating | Develop, complicate, intensify |
| **Kyū (急)** | Rapid | Swift, decisive | Climax, resolve, release |

### Multi-Scale Application

Jo-ha-kyū operates at every level simultaneously:

**Program level** (multi-work collection):
- Jo: Opening piece (lighter, accessible)
- Ha: Middle pieces (varied, building complexity)
- Kyū: Final piece (most intense, cathartic)

**Work level** (single story):
- Jo: First act/section
- Ha: Middle acts (Zeami recommended 3 sections here)
- Kyū: Final act

**Scene level**:
- Jo: Establish scene, slow dialogue
- Ha: Conflict emerges, pace increases
- Kyū: Scene climax, rapid resolution

**Sentence level**:
- Jo: Subject establishment
- Ha: Complication/elaboration
- Kyū: Swift conclusion

### The Nested Structure

```
Story Jo-ha-kyū
├── Jo (Act 1)
│   ├── Scene Jo
│   ├── Scene Ha
│   └── Scene Kyū
├── Ha (Acts 2-4)
│   ├── Ha-1: Rising action
│   ├── Ha-2: Complication
│   └── Ha-3: Crisis
└── Kyū (Act 5)
    └── Swift resolution
```

### Key Distinction from Western Pacing

| Western | Jo-ha-kyū |
|---------|-----------|
| Even distribution | Weighted toward kyū |
| Climax near end | Kyū can be very brief |
| Build-release | Build-build-swift release |
| Tension sustains | Tension accelerates |

### The Felt Rhythm

Zeami: "Since the Jo-ha-kyū pattern also exists within the body of the onlooker, the audience experiences a sense of organic 'rightness' when this rhythm is used."

Audiences *feel* jo-ha-kyū even when not consciously aware of it. Violating it creates discomfort.

### When to Use Jo-ha-kyū

- Action sequences (martial arts, chases, battles)
- Ceremony and ritual scenes
- Building dread (horror)
- Any scene where pacing *is* the content
- Audiobook production (natural breath rhythm)

### Jo-ha-kyū for TTS

TTS pacing benefits from jo-ha-kyū awareness:
- **Jo**: Slower speech, longer pauses, establishment
- **Ha**: Normal pace, building energy
- **Kyū**: Faster delivery, shorter pauses, momentum

## Griot Tradition (West African)

**Origin**: Mande Empire (~13th century), practiced by Mandinka, Wolof, Fulani, and other peoples

**Core concept**: Story is communal, participatory, and functional. The storyteller mediates between past and present, individual and community.

### Roles of the Griot

- **Historian**: Preserves genealogies and historical events
- **Praise singer**: Celebrates achievements, motivates action
- **Moral teacher**: Transmits values through narrative
- **Entertainer**: Engages through music, drama, humor
- **Mediator**: Resolves conflicts through story

### Structural Elements

**Call and Response**:
```
Griot: "Is the story sweet?"
Audience: "It is sweet!"
Griot: "Shall I continue?"
Audience: "Continue!"
```

This isn't decoration—it's structural. The audience's participation shapes the story's direction.

**Formulaic openings/closings**:
- Opening: Establishes frame, invokes tradition
- Closing: Returns to present, draws moral

**Episodic structure**:
- Stories can be expanded or contracted based on audience
- Episodes are modular, reorderable
- Repetition with variation (same event, different perspective)

**Musical integration**:
- Kora, balafon, or drum accompaniment
- Music signals transitions, emotion, importance
- Songs embedded in narrative

### Narrative Principles

| Principle | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Community over individual** | Protagonist represents/serves community |
| **Ancestry matters** | Genealogy provides authority and context |
| **Moral functionality** | Every story teaches something applicable |
| **Audience as participant** | Story adapts to response |
| **Repetition as emphasis** | Important truths repeated, varied |
| **Time is circular** | Past, present, future interconnect |

### Ubuntu Influence

*"Umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu"* — "A person is a person through other persons"

Stories embody this through:
- Characters defined by relationships
- Resolution requires community
- Individual triumph is communal triumph
- Isolation = tragedy

### When to Use Griot Principles

- Stories about community, family, heritage
- Multigenerational narratives
- Oral performance (audiobooks, podcasts)
- Interactive fiction
- When moral clarity matters
- Didactic but engaging children's stories

### Adapting for Written/Audio Form

**Call and response** becomes:
- Rhetorical questions
- Direct address to reader/listener
- Repeated refrains

**Musical integration** becomes:
- Rhythm in prose
- Recurring motifs
- TTS pacing variations at key moments

**Audience participation** becomes:
- Reader decisions (interactive)
- Multiple valid interpretations
- Stories that reward re-reading

## Circular Narrative (Indigenous/Global)

**Origin**: Many Indigenous traditions worldwide; also found in Hindu, Buddhist, and Celtic traditions

**Core concept**: Time is cyclical, not linear. Stories return to beginnings, transformed.

### Structure

```
        ┌─────────────────┐
        │                 │
        ▼                 │
    Beginning ────────────┘
        │                 ▲
        ▼                 │
    Journey ──────────────┤
        │                 │
        ▼                 │
    Transformation ───────┤
        │                 │
        ▼                 │
    Return ───────────────┘
        │
        ▼
    (New cycle begins)
```

### Key Distinction from Linear Narrative

| Linear | Circular |
|--------|----------|
| Point A to Point B | Point A to Point A' |
| Resolution ends story | Resolution enables new beginning |
| Change is destination | Change is process |
| Future differs from past | Future rhymes with past |

### Application

- Seasonal stories (agricultural, ritual)
- Coming-of-age (child → adult → elder → ancestor)
- Reincarnation/rebirth themes
- Generational sagas
- Stories about cycles (addiction, abuse, healing)

## Framework Selection Guide

| If your story emphasizes... | Consider... |
|-----------------------------|-------------|
| Emotional experience | Rasa |
| Pacing and rhythm | Jo-ha-kyū |
| Community and morality | Griot tradition |
| Cycles and return | Circular narrative |
| Contrast without conflict | Kishotenketsu |
| Escalating conflict | Three-Act |
| Beat-level precision | Save the Cat |
| Scene-level pacing | Scene-Sequel |

### Combining Frameworks

Frameworks are tools, not religions. Combine as needed:

**Rasa + Three-Act**: Choose dominant rasa, structure via three acts, design scenes to achieve emotional goals

**Jo-ha-kyū + Save the Cat**: Apply pacing rhythm to each beat, overall structure follows Save the Cat percentages

**Griot + Kishotenketsu**: Episodic community story with twist-based meaning rather than conflict

**Circular + Rasa**: Journey returns to beginning, but character's relationship to that place has transformed; design return scene for Shanta (peace) rasa
