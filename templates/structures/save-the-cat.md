# Save the Cat! Beat Sheet

Blake Snyder's 15-beat structure. More granular than Three-Act, with specific percentage targets.

## The 15 Beats

| # | Beat | Position | Purpose |
|---|------|----------|---------|
| 1 | Opening Image | 1% | Visual "before" snapshot; sets tone |
| 2 | Theme Stated | 5% | Someone (not protagonist) states the theme—protagonist doesn't understand yet |
| 3 | Set-Up | 1-10% | Introduce protagonist, world, "six things that need fixing" |
| 4 | Catalyst | 10% | Inciting incident; life will never be the same |
| 5 | Debate | 10-20% | Should I go? Protagonist weighs decision |
| 6 | Break Into Two | 20% | Protagonist chooses to enter Act Two; active decision |
| 7 | B Story | 22% | Secondary story begins (often love interest/mentor); carries theme |
| 8 | Fun and Games | 20-50% | "Promise of the premise"; why audience bought the ticket |
| 9 | Midpoint | 50% | False victory or false defeat; stakes raise; "fun and games" ends |
| 10 | Bad Guys Close In | 50-75% | External pressure mounts; internal doubts grow; team fractures |
| 11 | All Is Lost | 75% | Opposite of Midpoint; "whiff of death" (literal or metaphorical) |
| 12 | Dark Night of the Soul | 75-80% | Protagonist processes loss; hits bottom |
| 13 | Break Into Three | 80% | Eureka moment; A and B stories combine; protagonist knows what to do |
| 14 | Finale | 80-99% | Protagonist executes plan; dispatches all bad guys in ascending order |
| 15 | Final Image | 99-100% | Opposite of Opening Image; proves transformation |

## The "Save the Cat" Moment

The eponymous beat: early in Act One, protagonist does something—often small—that makes audience root for them. Not required, but effective.

**Examples:**
- Helps someone vulnerable
- Shows competence at their craft
- Displays wit under pressure
- Reveals hidden vulnerability

## Genre-Specific Applications

**Monster in the House**: Catalyst = monster appears; Midpoint = monster seems defeated but isn't; All Is Lost = monster kills a beloved character

**Buddy Love**: B Story IS the A Story; Midpoint = relationship appears solid; All Is Lost = relationship breaks

**Whydunit**: Catalyst = crime discovered; Midpoint = detective thinks they've solved it; All Is Lost = wrong suspect, real killer escapes

## Snyder's "Save the Cat" Genres

1. Monster in the House
2. Golden Fleece (road/quest)
3. Out of the Bottle (wish fulfillment)
4. Dude with a Problem (ordinary person, extraordinary circumstances)
5. Rites of Passage (life transitions)
6. Buddy Love (relationships)
7. Whydunit (mysteries)
8. The Fool Triumphant (underdog)
9. Institutionalized (group dynamics)
10. Superhero (extraordinary person, ordinary world)

## Common Problems

| Problem | Symptom | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Passive protagonist | Things happen TO them; no Break Into Two decision | Force a choice with consequences |
| Missing B Story | Theme feels bolted on; transformation unconvincing | Add relationship that embodies theme |
| Weak Midpoint | Second act sags; "Fun and Games" drags | Create false victory/defeat that raises stakes |
| All Is Lost without death | Moment lacks weight | Add "whiff of death"—literal, symbolic, or relationship death |
| Rushed Dark Night | Protagonist bounces back too fast | Let them sit in defeat; earn the insight |
| Break Into Three without synthesis | A and B stories don't merge | Eureka must combine external goal with internal truth |
| Finale as new information | Deus ex machina feeling | Everything in Finale must be set up earlier |
| Opening/Final Image mismatch | Transformation unclear | Make visual/thematic bookends explicit opposites |
