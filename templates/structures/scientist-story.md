## V. Standards Content Integration Checklist

Use this to audit the draft. Each checked item should appear *in scene*, not as explanation.

### Disciplinary Core Ideas
- [ ] Core science content visible in what the scientist *does*, not in what characters explain to each other
- [ ] Scientific vocabulary appears in context, not defined in prose
- [ ] Student can reconstruct the key concept from the story's events

### Science and Engineering Practices
- [ ] At least one scene shows the scientist planning or revising a method (SEP 3)
- [ ] At least one scene shows interpretation of results (SEP 4)
- [ ] The SEP 7 scene (argument) is not skipped or compressed to a single paragraph
- [ ] The scientist's reasoning is visible, not just their conclusions

### Crosscutting Concepts
- [ ] The CCC appears in the story's structure, not just as a theme stated in dialogue
- [ ] A student could identify the CCC without being told what it is

### Representation
- [ ] The scientist's identity is specific and present in the story, not erased for "universality"
- [ ] The institutional barrier is named and specific
- [ ] The story does not reduce the scientist to their identity (they are a full person who also faces these barriers)

## VI. Grade-Band Calibration Notes

### Grade 5
- Science visible through sensory observation: the student sees, hears, feels what the scientist experiences
- Abstract concepts anchor in physical, observable phenomena
- Vocabulary: introduce 5–8 key science terms through context; do not explain them
- Length: 500–1500 words
- Point of view options: first-person (scientist), close third, or second-person ("you")

### Grades 6–8
- Science visible through problem-solving: the student thinks through the problem alongside the scientist
- Data, graphs, measurements can appear as narrative objects (the scientist stares at a graph; the numbers tell a story)
- Institutional barriers: name them clearly; don't soften
- Length: 1500–5000 words
- Time shifts allowed: flashback to mentor, flash-forward to legacy

### Grades 9–12
- Science visible through implication: the student inherits the question the scientist couldn't fully answer
- Complexity of scientific debate can be portrayed with nuance — the skeptics may have valid points
- Moral weight: discoveries have consequences; scientist is accountable for what their work is used for
- Length: 3000–10,000+ words
- Full structural complexity available
