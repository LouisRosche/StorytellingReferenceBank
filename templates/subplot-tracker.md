# Subplot Tracker

Long-form fiction requires managing multiple storylines simultaneously. This template tracks all subplots at the manuscript level and provides a per-subplot card for detailed planning and revision.

Every subplot must earn its place: thematic, character, or plot function — ideally more than one. A subplot that runs in parallel without intersecting the A-plot is a narrative liability.

**Related**: `@templates/structures/three-act.md` (subplots intersect with main structure beats) | `@templates/scene-card.md` (tag each scene with its subplot label) | `@templates/revision-workflow.md` (subplot check belongs in Pass 2)

#### 3. Mini-Arc

Every subplot needs its own setup, escalation, and payoff. It doesn't need to be symmetrical with the A-plot — but it must have all three phases.

| Phase | What Happens | Approximate Story Position |
|-------|-------------|---------------------------|
| **Setup** — introduce the subplot and its stakes | | ~___% |
| **Escalation** — complications specific to this storyline | | ~___% |
| **Payoff** — resolution, transformation, or irresolution (if earned) | | ~___% |

**Setup position note**: Subplots typically introduce within the first 30% of the manuscript. A subplot introduced after the midpoint will feel underdeveloped unless it's a deliberate late-breaking revelation.

**Payoff position note**: Subplots typically resolve before or during the climax, not after. A subplot that closes in the denouement should be brief.

**Minimum intersections by manuscript length**:
- Novella (40-60K): 2-3 intersections per subplot
- Novel (80-100K): 3-5 intersections per subplot
- Epic / series installment (120K+): 5+ intersections per subplot

## Part Three: Subplot Integration Diagnostic

Run this when a subplot isn't working. Answer each question honestly.

### The Existence Test

- Does this subplot have a thematic function distinct from — but related to — the A-plot? If not, it's decoration.
- Could you remove this subplot and still have a complete story? If yes, what's lost? Name it specifically. If nothing is lost, cut the subplot.
- Is the subplot present because it earns its place, or because you're attached to it?

### The Connection Test

- Does this subplot intersect with the A-plot at least three times, with escalating stakes each time?
- Or does it run in parallel — existing in its own world without affecting the main story? Parallel subplots are the most common structural failure. They feel productive during drafting and invisible to readers.
- At the climax: does this subplot contribute something — a resource, a revelation, an obstacle, an emotional counterpoint? If the climax would be identical without this subplot, the subplot is not integrated.

### The Mini-Arc Test

- Does this subplot have a setup, escalation, and payoff?
- Is the payoff specific — a concrete change in character, relationship, or situation — or is it vague resolution that trails off?
- If the subplot ends without payoff: is that intentional (a strand left open by design) or an oversight? Open strands in literary fiction can be powerful. Open strands that are oversights damage reader trust.

### The Character Test

- Do the characters in this subplot have their own goals, not just functional roles in relation to the protagonist?
- Does this subplot change at least one character — not just advance them through events?
- Are the subplot characters as specific as your main cast, or are they flattened by their secondary status?

### The Thematic Test

- Can you state in one sentence what this subplot argues about the story's central question?
- Does it argue something different from the A-plot's position, or does it just restate it?
- If a reader finished only this subplot — skipping the A-plot entirely — would they encounter a complete thematic statement? They should.

### The TTS Test

- Are subplot-specific speaker transitions clearly attributed in the prose?
- If this subplot has a different tonal register than the A-plot, is that transition marked in the narrative so the TTS director can account for it?
- Does a character who is minor in the A-plot become primary here? If so, do they have a persona file that covers their full range?

## Quick Reference: Subplot Failure Modes

| Problem | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Subplot feels irrelevant | No thematic connection; no intersection with A-plot | Find the thematic link or cut |
| Subplot feels like a different book | Tonal or thematic mismatch | Integrate through a shared intersection scene; or cut |
| Subplot disappears for long stretches | Dormancy without referencing | Add reference touches; or restructure to consolidate scenes |
| Subplot payoff feels unearned | Insufficient setup; rushed closure | Check setup/payoff register; add planted elements earlier |
| Subplot upstages the main plot | More dramatic or interesting than A-plot | Reconsider which story is the real story |
| Subplot resolves too early | Closed before climax; nothing left to contribute | Push resolution to just before or during climax |
| Subplot has no mini-arc | Events happen but no arc shape | Identify the subplot's own All Is Lost moment |
